Quin E. Denfeld, PhD, RN, FAHA, Vice Chair; Stephanie Turrise, PhD, RN, FAHA; Eric J. MacLaughlin, PharmD; Pei- Shiun Chang, PhD, RN; Walter K. Clair, MD, MPH, FAHA; Eldrin F. ABSTRACT: Falls and fear of falling are a major health issue and associated with high injury rates, high medical care costs, and significant negative impact on quality of life. Adults with cardiovascular disease are at high risk of falling. However, the prevalence and specific risks for falls among adults with cardiovascular disease are not well understood, and falls are likely underestimated in clinical practice. Data from surveys of patient- reported and medical record- based analyses identify falls or risks for falling in  $40\%$  to  $60\%$  of adults with cardiovascular disease. Increased fall risk is associated with medications, structural heart disease, orthostatic hypotension, and arrhythmias, as well as with abnormal gait and balance, physical frailty, sensory impairment, and environmental hazards. These risks are particularly important among the growing population of older adults with cardiovascular disease. All clinicians who care for patients with cardiovascular disease have the opportunity to recognize falls and to mitigate risks for falling. This scientific statement provides consensus on the interdisciplinary evaluation, prevention, and management of falls among adults with cardiac disease and the management of cardiovascular care when patients are at risk of falling. We outline research that is needed to clarify prevalence and factors associated with falls and to identify interventions that will prevent falls among adults with cardiovascular disease.

Falling and fear of falling are significant health care problems with far- reaching implications beyond a specific event, including effects on the broader aspects of a patient's self- confidence, social interaction, and well- being. The prevalence of falls among adults with cardiovascular disease (CVD) has not been quantified, yet in 1 population of adults hospitalized with CVD,  $60\%$  had a moderate to high risk for falls. Fall rates are particularly high among older adults. One in 4 older adults reports a fall each year, incurring significant adverse outcomes such as injuries, hospitalizations, and deaths. Falls are also costly; in the United States in 2015, the estimated medical cost of fatal and nonfatal falls in adults  $\geq 65$  years of age was \(\) 50$ billion. Even when falls are not associated with significant trauma, they can herald progressive fear of falling and associated tendencies for functional decline, leading to diminished quality of life.6,7

A fall, defined as 'an event which results in a person coming to rest inadvertently on the ground or floor or other lower level' may be attributable to CVD causes such as hypotension from medications or autonomic dysfunction, reduced cardiac output, or syncope. Falls also can be caused by co- occurring non- CVD risks such as visual impairment, gait and balance problems, or cognitive impairment. These problems especially contribute to falls in people with a history of stroke, among whom  $\approx 25\%$  fall. Moreover, falls may relate to medications that confuse, weaken, dehydrate, or vasodilate. Most often, a fall occurs as a result of multiple contributing factors (Figure). Despite the high prevalence of falls and the need to manage fall risk, assessing for falls and risk of falling is challenging. Overall, despite being common and highly consequential, falls are rarely acknowledged and addressed as a modifiable health benchmark by patients or clinicians. Many older adults who fall are reluctant to mention it to others, including their clinicians, because they perceive that it will undermine how they are regarded and treated. $^{11}$  Moreover, up to one- third of patients who experience falls do not recall a fall within 3 months of the event. $^{12}$  Clinicians who care for patients with CVD can offset these challenges by asking specifically about falls or fear of falling and reviewing for fall risks as part of their assessments.

# PREVALENCE OF FALLS AMONG ADULTS WITH CVD: STATE OF THE SCIENCE

Because few prospective studies have specifically focused on identifying falls among adults with CVD, data on falls in CVD come from secondary analyses of surveys relying on patient report or on injury data coded in medical records. $^{13}$  An analysis of adults hospitalized with CVD (ie, myocardial infarction, atrial fibrillation, and heart failure [HF]) found that, regardless of age,  $>60\%$  have a moderate to high risk for falls. $^{3}$  Community- dwelling adults in the Irish Longitudinal Study on Ageing with 4 to 5 self- reported CVDs (hypertension, angina, heart attack, congestive HF, heart murmur, or abnormal heart rhythm) recalled falls or syncope more often in the past year than those with fewer CVDs. $^{14}$  In addition, cardiovascular drugs, heart murmur, and abnormal heart rhythm were associated with any fall, whereas heart murmur, angina, and HF were associated with recurrent falls. $^{14}$

Older adults have a particularly high risk for falling, and falls are a leading cause of injury among older adults. $^{4}$  Both cardiovascular- related and non- cardiovascular- related risks for falling increase with age. Often, a cardiovascular medication regimen that is well tolerated and effective initially can contribute to falls in the context of concomitant non- CVD conditions or medications that lower blood pressure or impair gait and balance. Likewise, noncardiovascular medications, environmental factors such as changes in ambient temperature, fluid intake, hazards in the home (eg, loose rugs, pets), and cognitive impairment can exacerbate fall risk in older adults with CVD.

Adults with HF and arrhythmias have an especially high risk of falls likely because of diminished cardiac output, polypharmacy, or interaction with other comorbid

conditions.15- 18 Among adults with HF, the fall rate was  $43\%$  compared with fall rates of  $< 30\%$  for people with other chronic diseases.16 A large retrospective analysis of older adults concluded that those with HF had a  $14\%$  greater odds of falling compared with those without HF after adjustment for covariates.15 In another population, an individual with an HF diagnosis had a 4- fold increased risk of sustaining a fracture requiring hospitalization compared with adults with other CVDs.18 Arrhythmias are also associated with high rates of falling. Studies demonstrate a higher prevalence of falls in patients with atrial fibrillation (odds ratio, 1.98) compared with those without atrial fibrillation20 and a higher prevalence of nonaccidental falls (ie, without mitigating circumstances such as environmental hazards resulting in trips or slips;  $26\%$  than accidental falls (with environmental hazards resulting in trips or slips;  $15\%$  21

Although racial, ethnic, and sex differences in fall rates have not been studied in CVD, there are notable differences in the general population that should be considered. The Health and Retirement Study found that Black individuals had significantly lower odds of experiencing a fall than non- Hispanic White individuals, even after controlling for covariates.22 Other studies have also demonstrated racial differences, with White individuals having higher rates of falls outside the home and differences in fall injuries compared with Black individuals.23 Similarly, Asian American individuals are significantly less likely to fall than non- Hispanic White individuals.24 Women have higher rates of falls than men4 and have higher rates of falls that resulted in injury or required a medical visit and hospitalization.25

# RISK FACTORS FOR FALLS IN ADULTS WITH CVD

# Medications Contributing to Fall Risk

The use of multiple medications, or polypharmacy, is common in older patients with CVD, who often have comorbidities. Polypharmacy may or may not be appropriate, depending on the clinical situation, but is associated with increased risk of falls.26 Unfortunately, after a fall resulting in fragility fracture (a fracture resulting from mechanical forces that would not ordinarily result in fracture), drugs that may have potentiated a fall are not reduced, and in many cases, other agents are added, increasing the risk of a future fall and fracture.27

There are numerous fall risk- increasing drugs that may be used in patients with CVD. Agents that have most commonly been associated with falls from formal analyses (eg, meta- analyses, observational studies) are listed in Table 1, along with the mechanism for impaired postural control or falls and considerations for therapy. It is important to note that this list is not all inclusive; other agents can increase falls in individualized patients. Likewise, there may be "therapeutic competition" whereby a medication can be used to treat one condition but contributes to adverse effects in another, resulting in a fall.28

Cardiovascular medications, including antihypertensive agents and loop diuretics, are potential contributors to falls through hypotension and orthostatic hypotension (OH).Nonselective  $\beta$  - blockers and mixed  $\alpha - \beta$  blockade are associated with an increased risk of falls compared with selective  $\beta$  - blockers.29 Environmental factors such as hot weather or physiological factors such as reduced oral intake or diminished thirst can tip a well- tolerated regimen toward hypotension. Other CVD medications such as digoxin, diuretics, and type IA antiarrhythmics may cause dizziness, confusion, muscle weakness, or blurred vision, in turn impairing postural control and leading to a potential fall.30,31

Numerous medications may lead to falls by causing an abnormal gait and poor balance and postural control attributable to confusion, dizziness, or sedation. In addition, medications that may cause dry eyes or blurry vision may lead to problems with balance and coordination, increasing the risk for falls.32 Drug classes with central nervous system effects most commonly associated with increased risk of falls and possibly fractures include antidepressants, antipsychotics, benzodiazepines, and opioids.31,33- 38 Benzodiazepines and other sleep medications contribute to report of falls in older adults with sleep problems in the general population and in nursing facilities.39,40 Anticholinergic agents also lead to or worsen OH and increase fall risk as a result of impaired balance, vision, cognition, neuropsychological performance, and physical functioning.41,41 Drug index burden, a measure of total exposure to anticholinergic and sedative medications, has been associated with increased falls.42 Use of alcohol or other drugs (eg, marijuana) impairs gait and balance and increases fall risk.43

# Orthostatic Hypotension

OH, usually defined as a sustained reduction of systolic blood pressure of  $\geq 20$  mmHg or a reduction of diastolic blood pressure of  $\geq 10$  mmHg within 3 minutes of standing or head- up tilt test to  $\geq 60^{\circ}$  on a tilt table,44,45 is a risk factor for dizziness and falling. The prevalence of OH in older adults is  $\geq 30\%$  and increases with age and with hypertension, antihypertensive treatment, and comorbidities.47

Autonomic nervous system dysfunction leads to neurogenic OH, characterized by inadequate autonomic compensatory postural responses (eg, systemic vasoconstriction, reflex increase in heart rate of  $< 15$  bpm) to maintain blood pressure.44 Arterial stiffness is hypothesized to explain reduced baroreflex response in older adults with OH.48 Autonomic nervous system dysfunction and altered blood pressure control, including OH, are also common in chronic kidney disease,49 often a comorbidity in CVD.

Postprandial hypotension (a  $\geq 20 - \text{mmHg}$  drop in systolic blood pressure in the hour after a meal) occurs in more than one- third of older individuals50 and increases fall risk.

Both cardiovascular (eg, antihypertensives, particularly those with  $\alpha$ - adrenergic action) and noncardiovascular (eg, antidepressants, benzodiazepines) medications may cause or exacerbate OH.51 Several antihypertensives (eg,  $\beta$ - blockers, nondihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, central  $\alpha$ - 2 agonists) may impair the compensatory increase in heart rate when patients rise from a supine or seated position. Paradoxically, although antihypertensive therapy lowers blood pressure and can increase OH in an individual, blood pressure lowering decreases cardiovascular morbidity and mortality but does not increase the risk of

OH or falls across a population.45 In fact, although intensive blood pressure lowering reduces standing blood pressure, it is associated with decreased OH, possibly attributable to improved cardiovascular hemodynamics and adaptations in baroreflex sensitivity and other blood pressure regulation.52 Therefore, the presence of OH in and of itself should not be viewed as a reason to de- escalate treatment of hypertension.52 Rather, older patients at risk for OH and falls require slower dose titrations, careful monitoring, and a team- based approach to assess the risks and benefits of antihypertensive drugs.45 This team might include nurses, physicians, pharmacists, therapists, and caregivers in addition to the patient, all of whom can participate in monitoring blood pressures in the course of medication titration.

# Syncope

Syncope, a transient loss of consciousness attributable to decreased blood flow to the brain (neurally mediated, orthostatic, or cardiac), increases with age and can manifest as falls.53 Neurally mediated syncope, characterized by peripheral vasodilation, hypotension, and bradycardia, can be caused by vasovagal syncope and carotid sinus syndrome, both of which are particularly common in older adults with or without structural heart disease. Cardiovascular and noncardiovascular medications also can contribute to vasovagal syncope.

OH, postprandial hypotension, and dehydration can result in hypoperfusion of the brain and, in turn, syncope. OH and postprandial hypotension are attributable to impaired autonomic reflexes and increase with increasing age.

Cardiac syncope may be the result of bradycardia, conduction disease, tachycardia, or structural heart disease (including aortic stenosis, ischemia, systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve, or other causes of reduced left ventricular outflow).53 Arrhythmias, including bradycardia, tachycardia, and conduction disorders, are common in adults with CVD and become more prevalent with older age. Nonarrhythmic cardiac syncope from reduced cardiac output and structural heart disease, including cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, and ischemia, is an important cause of falls, particularly in older adults. In a retrospective study of patients presenting to the emergency department with syncope, the syncope was attributed to structural heart disease (including ischemia) in  $27\%$  and among these patients, ischemic heart disease was the most common cause.54

# Frailty and Sarcopenia

Frailty, defined as decreased physiological reserves and increased vulnerability to stressors,55 is a significant risk factor for future falls among older adults. Frailty is highly prevalent among adults with CVD, affecting almost half of those with  $\mathsf{HF},^{57}$  individuals with coronary artery disease,58 and those undergoing cardiac surgery. Frailty affects those with CVD across the life span and should be considered a significant risk factor for falls among all adults with CVD. Physical frailty manifests as a phenotype of unintentional weight loss (a proxy for loss of muscle mass), weakness, slowness, physical exhaustion, and low physical activity.55 Each of these manifestations can increase the likelihood that someone may fall (eg, lower extremity weakness predicts falls60), but it is the accumulation of each of these manifestations that likely has the greatest impact on fall risk.55

Sarcopenia, a disorder of decreased skeletal muscle mass, strength, and quality61 that is considered a substrate of frailty,62 also confers a high fall risk.63 Sarcopenia is common among adults with CVD such as those with HF in which skeletal muscle has altered muscle fiber composition and mitochondrial function, as well as intramuscular fat deposition.64

These changes result in lower extremity weakness and gait slowness and are associated with physical frailty and risk for falling.60 Evidence of sarcopenia can often be masked by concurrent obesity, called sarcopenic obesity. Sarcopenic obesity also confers a high risk of falls65 and should be considered a risk factor for obese adults with CVD.

# Sensory Impairment and Musculoskeletal Problems

Sensory Impairment and Musculoskeletal ProblemsPostural control required to maintain an upright position integrates sensory input, central neurological processing, motor coordination, and musculoskeletal function to perceive, interpret, and respond to stimuli in the environment. Sensory deficits (eg, peripheral neuropathy and visual impairments) and musculoskeletal problems (eg, arthritis) can significantly affect postural control and impair gait and balance, which in turn increase the likelihood of falling. Studies among older adults have demonstrated that visual, hearing, and neuromuscular sensory deficits are significant predictors of falls. Many adults with CVD have sensory deficit- related comorbid conditions such as diabetes with neuropathy, stroke, visual and auditory impairments, and musculoskeletal disorders that increase their risk for falls.

# Cognitive Impairment

Cognitive ImpairmentCognitive impairment, particularly deficits in processing speed and executive function, is associated with falls. Cognitive impairment is highly prevalent among adults with CVD; for example,  $>40\%$  of patients with HF are estimated to have some degree of cognitive impairment. The mechanisms contributing to cognitive impairment in CVD are not fully known. A loss of brain volume in the hippocampus, frontal lobes, and parietal lobes attributable to cerebral hypoperfusion or untreated sleep apnea is associated with impairments in several cognitive domains, including memory, executive function, and processing speed. Memory loss and impaired executive function can increase medication errors, especially amid polypharmacy, thus increasing hypotension and other adverse effects.

A brief physical frailty assessment involves asking about unintentional weight loss, assessing weakness with standing from a chair 5 times without using arms, and asking about physical exhaustion (the SOF [Study of Osteoporotic Fractures] frailty index).77 Slow gait speed  $(< 0.8m / s)$  also identifies frail individuals.61 Assessing weakness and gait speed will help identify those at higher risk of falling. Any gait instability, any loss of balance, or a positive frailty screen should prompt physical therapy referral for a more in- depth evaluation of gait and balance, strengthening exercises, and prescription of assistive devices.

Older adults with CVD should be screened for OH. In the ideal screen, blood pressure would be measured supine, seated, and standing (for 3 minutes) at least once in adults with CVD. At a minimum, standing (3 minutes) blood pressure will identify significant hypotension. When hypotension is identified in the standing position, supine blood pressure and heart rate should be measured. Once identified, OH should lead clinicians to

monitor supine and standing blood pressures. Evaluation of OH should be included in the clinical care for all patients with a suspected neurodegenerative disorder associated with autonomic dysfunction (eg, Parkinson disease), individuals taking medications for Parkinson disease or antidepressants, those with unexplained fall or a syncopal episode, patients with peripheral neuropathies associated with autonomic dysfunction (eg, HIV, diabetes), patients with hypertension, those  $\geq 70$  years of age or on multiple medications, and those with postural dizziness or symptoms when standing.44

Supine hypertension should be incorporated into evaluation and treatment strategies for patients with CVD with OH. Nocturnal blood pressure treatment has been suggested to have neurohormonal benefit,78 and treatment of supine hypertension both provides CVD risk reduction and can reduce OH.19 Management of evidence- based medications for CVD requires adjustment and close monitoring to avoid significant hypotension that could lead to falls. The benefits of evidence- based therapies should be balanced with fall risk reduction.

In patients diagnosed with OH, after a detailed medication review and modification of medications that can cause or worsen OH, nonpharmacological measures should be instituted.46 In appropriate patients, strategies include increased fluid and salt intake, use of compression stockings or abdominal binders, Valsalva maneuver on standing, and exercise. Bed rest worsens OH and should be avoided except during sleep. Elevation of the head of the bed, even by a few inches, can decrease supine hypertension and improve OH. Any adult falling after a meal should be evaluated for postprandial hypotension and at a minimum instructed to avoid standing for 30 minutes after a meal.

Medication management of OH includes 3 agents that increase supine hypertension. Volume expansion with fludrocortisone, a mineralocorticoid aldosterone analog, is relatively contraindicated in patients with hypertension and HF. Midodrine is a short- acting  $\alpha - 1$  agonist that raises blood pressure for  $\approx 4$  hours and thus can be dosed when the patient is sitting or standing. Droxidopa increases levels of norepinephrine in the peripheral nervous system and raises blood pressure for  $\approx 4$  hours.44 Prescription of these medications requires ongoing monitoring of both standing and supine blood pressure.

An evaluation for syncope should include an assessment of heart rhythm disturbances, a cardiac examination and echocardiogram to detect structural disease (described above), orthostatic vital signs, carotid sinus massage, and a comprehensive medication review. Heart rhythm evaluation can be enhanced by current ambulatory cardiac monitoring modalities such as topical patches that make long- term arrhythmia monitoring much less cumbersome for both patients and clinicians.80 In extreme cases, implantable loop recorders have the potential to document actionable arrhythmias in the patient with a fall or syncope whose digital literacy or dexterity precludes the use of these newer, less invasive technologies.

Many older adults with CVD may already have pacemakers or defibrillators implanted as primary or secondary prevention devices with built- in arrhythmia detection algorithms.81 All of the diagnostic capabilities of these devices may not have been deployed because of recognition of the detrimental effects of right ventricular pacing on left ventricular function or to increase device battery longevity. In rare cases, the programming may result in pacemaker syndrome (ie, reduced cardiac output attributable to atrioventricular dyssynchrony) with presyncope. Device malfunction or battery depletion may also result in syncope. Thus, the presence of a pacemaker or defibrillator in a patient who falls should prompt an evaluation by a device- experienced clinician to diagnose possible device- related syncope.

Interventions to reduce frailty or the impact of frailty on falls, including exercise, nutrition, and case management, have demonstrated mixed results.82- 84 Supplementation of vitamins  $\mathsf{B}_{12}$  and D has not demonstrated benefits in frailty measures or falls, although vitamin  $\mathsf{B}_{12}$  supplementation to  $>350 \mathrm{pg} / \mathrm{mL}$  improved balance and gait speed in 1 study.85 Multifactorial interventions may reduce fall rates (but not the number of people who fall).86,87 An exercise- based transitional, tailored, progressive physical therapist- based rehabilitation intervention that focused on 4 physical function domains (strength, balance, mobility, and endurance) for older patients with HF beginning during the incident HF hospitalization and continuing for 3 months as outpatient 1- on- 1 training thereafter improved Short Physical Performance Battery scores at 3 months but without significant differences in falls measured over 6 months as a secondary outcome of the study.86 Cardiac rehabilitation is underused for people with CVD for whom it is available, and although cardiac rehabilitation improves multiple domains of physical function, many questions remain about its impact on falling.

Some patients with HF demonstrate improved gait speed with neurohormonal blockade88 or mechanical circulatory support device therapy.89 Angiotensin- converting enzyme inhibitors improve skeletal muscle function,90 but a recent meta- analysis did not show an effect on physical performance.91 Further data are needed to demonstrate that falls in patients with CVD are reduced by exercise, cardiac rehabilitation, nutritional interventions, and guideline- directed therapies that reduce frailty.

Strategies to address sensory impairments, as well as gait and balance issues, are outlined by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention STEADI (Stopping Elderly Accidents, Deaths, and Injuries) initiative toolkit.92 These include a check sheet to address home environmental safety, including trip hazards such as throw rugs and pets, uneven surfaces, and poorly lit areas to ensure a safe home environment, and evaluation for and appropriate use of assistive devices such as walkers and canes. Referral to home health physical therapy or occupational therapy can facilitate home safety evaluations and treatments.

Therapists also evaluate and appropriately dispense assistive devices. Exercise that improves postural stability, gait, and balance such as Tai Chi/Oigong or any exercises that emphasize flexibility and strength training of leg muscles84,93 may help mitigate falls among those with sensory impairments or gait and balance issues.

Screening cognitive function and monitoring cognitive status are important aspects of care for adults with CVD. The Montreal Cognitive Assessment has high sensitivity and specificity to detect cognitive impairment among older adults generally and adults with HF specifically.94 It includes tests of executive and visuospatial function, recall, orientation, and other domains. The Mini- Cog tests executive function and memory, the domains commonly affected in CVD, and requires  $\leq 5$  minutes to administer.94,95 Similar to sensory impairments, home hazard reduction is an effective strategy in preventing falls among those with cognitive impairment.96 In patients with cognitive impairment, involving family or other caregivers in fall prevention is also important.

Strategies to address falls must include close collaboration with the patient and their support network of family and friends. It may be necessary to educate and negotiate for a patient to acknowledge their fall risk and to accept interventions to reduce falling. Specifically, it is important to address misconceptions about fall risk, especially perceived threats to the patient's identity (eg, "I do not want to look old") and their independence. Encouraging safety while not excessively limiting activities may help patients strike a balance between fall risk and maintaining quality of life.2

# CONSIDERATIONS FOR ANTICOAGULATION MANAGEMENT

Atrial fibrillation, which increases with age, with up to 1 in 10 patients  $>80$  years of age affected, can result in strokes that can be especially devastating. Despite anticoagulation therapy being highly effective in reducing stroke risk, many older patients with atrial fibrillation or venous thromboembolic events who sustain a fall or who have high falling risks do not receive such therapy.9,100 Minor and major bleeding, especially intracranial hemorrhage, is a concern when treating older patients at greater risk of falling with anticoagulants.9 However, the risk of bleeding or intracranial hemorrhage is typically outweighed by the benefits of anticoagulation therapy for decreasing the risk of stroke, especially for patients at higher stroke risk.100- 102 The nonvitamin K oral anticoagulants are at least as safe and effective as warfarin in decreasing the risk of stroke and venous thromboembolic events and are associated with a significantly reduced risk of intracranial hemorrhage,100 even in patients with a history of falls.103,104

For patients with atrial fibrillation at risk for falling and in need of anticoagulation therapy, a thorough evaluation of stroke risk (eg,  $\mathsf{CHA}_2\mathsf{DS}_2$ - VASO), bleeding (eg, HASBLED), and falls should be incorporated into shared decision- making on anticoagulation.100 In general, oral anticoagulant therapy should not be withheld in patients at risk of falling, and a non- vitamin K oral anticoagulant should be prescribed preferentially.97,100 Mitigation of risk factors for bleeding and falling is multifactorial, as previously discussed, and should use a team- based approach. Decisions on atrial fibrillation therapy should be based on shared decision- making, taking into account risk of stroke and bleeding and patient preference.97

# GAPS IN KNOWLEDGE AND FUTURE RESEARCH

The absence of prospective studies of falls in adults with CVD required this consensus document to gather information from many different sources to identify risks and management of falls among adults with CVD. We currently lack a thorough understanding of how many adults with CVD are falling, the frequency of their falls, and injuries resulting from falls. In addition, we are limited in our understanding of potential contributing factors to falls, including the impact of cardiovascular targets outlined in Table 2. Evidence is limited on best practices for implementing a fall risk assessment for adults with CVD.

Future research in key areas is needed to understand the intersection of falls and CVD. Falls and functional outcomes need to be included in parallel with other CVD metrics. We need to prospectively quantify the frequency of falls and the associated risk factors as a function of specific cardiovascular conditions. Research is needed to identify the demographic, social, cultural, and other factors that may affect differential fall rates among adults with CVD across racial and ethnic groups, by age, and between sexes. Research should also evaluate falls in specific CVD diagnoses, in varied settings, and in association with comorbidities.

System improvement in terms of falling could link CVD and non- CVD clinicians in caring for older adults with CVD and standardize approaches to recognizing and managing medications and conditions that increase fall risk. Further improvement efforts could include new medication regimens, which are more sophisticated in terms of interactions, as well as the impact of medications on gait, balance, and falling. Data are needed to inform whether to treat CVD according to evidence- based guidelines in someone with a fall.

We need to build evidence for therapies, including different approaches to exercise such as complementary medicine (Oigong and Tai Chi), to reduce fall risk in adults with CVD. Cardiac rehabilitation improves multiple domains of physical function105; however, many questions remain about its impact on falling: Do we use home- based cardiac rehabilitation in a person who falls? What kind of exercise will be effective in preventing falls (strength, balance, aerobic)? Do cardiac rehabilitation and other strategies to prevent or manage frailty and sarcopenia affect fall risk? Further understanding the underpinning of falls

in people with CVD and the impact of interventions can result in the incorporation of new monitoring technologies to detect sway and physical features of falling.

# CONCLUSIONS

Falls and risk for falling are major, yet underrecognized, problems among adults with CVD. The cardiology team should inquire about falls in adults with CVD and conduct basic assessments to identify the causes of falls and risks for falls. Clinicians caring for patients with CVD should carefully review medications that increase fall risk, address cardiovascular risk factors for falling, and develop referral links to physical and occupational therapists and other health care team members who can act on other identified fall risks. Given the increasing numbers of older adults with CVD and the significant CVD and non- CVD risk factors for falling in adults with CVD, falls should be on the radar of all cardiology clinicians.
