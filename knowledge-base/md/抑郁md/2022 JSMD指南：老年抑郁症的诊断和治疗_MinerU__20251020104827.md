# Guidelines for diagnosis and treatment of depression in older adults: A report from the Japanese Society of mood disorders

Hajime Baba, MD, PhD  $^{1,2}$ * Shinsuke Kito, MD, PhD  $^{3,4}$  Kazutaka Nukariya, MD, PhD  $^{4,5,\ast}$  Minoru Takeshima, MD, PhD  $^{6,7}$  Noboru Fujise, MD, PhD  $^{8,\ast}$  Junichi Iga, MD, PhD  $^{9}$  Hidehiro Oshibuchi, MD, PhD, $^{10,11}$  Masahiko Kawano, MD, PhD, $^{12}$  Mahiko Kimura, MD, PhD  $^{13}$  Katsuyoshi Mizukami, MD, PhD, $^{14}$  Masaru Mimura, MD, PhD  $^{15}$  and Committee for Treatment Guidelines of Mood Disorders, Japanese Society of Mood Disorders

The Committee for Treatment Guidelines of Mood Disorders, Japanese Society of Mood Disorders, published a Japanese guideline for the treatment of late-life depression in 2020. Based on that guideline, the present guideline was developed and revised to incorporate the suggestions of global experts and the latest published evidence. In the diagnosis of late-life depression, it is important to carefully differentiate it from bipolar disorders, depressive states caused by physical and organic brain disease, drug effects, and dementia, and to determine the comorbidity between late-life depression and dementia. It is necessary to fully understand the clinical characteristics and psychosocial background of late-life depression, evaluate the patient's condition, and provide basic interventions based on these factors. Problem-solving therapy, reminiscence therapy/life review therapy, and behavioral activation therapy, and other forms of psychotherapy can reduce depressive symptoms. In terms of

pharmacotherapy, newer antidepressants or non-tricyclic antidepressants are recommended for late-life depression, and it is recommended that the efficacy of least the minimal effective dosage should first be determined. Switching antidepressants and aripiprazole augmentation can be used to treatment-resistant therapy. Electroconvulsive therapy and repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation have demonstrated usefulness for late-life depression. Exercise therapy, high-intensity light therapy, and diet therapy also show some effectiveness and are useful for late-life depression. Continuation therapy should be maintained for at least 1 year after remission.

Keywords: antidepressant, depression, elderly, guideline, psychotherapy.

# Progress

With the support of the Japan Agency for Medical Research and Development (AMED), the Committee for Treatment Guidelines of Mood Disorders of the Japanese Society of Mood Disorders developed a guideline for the treatment of late-life depression for the next revision of the Guidelines for Treatment of Depression<sup>1</sup> (July 2018). The development of this guideline was approved and facilitated by the Japanese Psychogeriatric Society. A working group to develop the draft was launched in November 2018, and the first draft was released at the 16th Annual Meeting of the Japanese Society for Mood Disorders (July 2019). Public comments were solicited in January 2020, and the draft was revised. The Japanese version of the draft was

approved by the society's board, and it was published in July 2020. The present guideline was based on the Japanese version. Several revisions were made to this guideline, to incorporate the suggestions of three global experts and evidence that appeared after the completion of the Japanese version of the guideline in 2020.

# Basic principles and disclaimer

The present guideline was created for psychiatric specialists. This guideline was intended to support clinical practice in the treatment of late-life depression; it does not aim to determine treatment. Therefore, clinicians must select appropriate treatment at their own discretion

and not be bound by this guideline. The authors are not liable for any consequences deriving from adherence solely to this guideline.

# Structure and methods

The guideline comprises two parts: General remarks (Sections A and B) and Treatment (Section C). The General remarks part describes diagnosis, clinical features, pharmacokinetics, evaluation of the condition, and basic interventions for late-life depression. Although current research evidence was included as much as possible, the lack of sufficient evidence made it difficult to conduct a systematic review. Therefore, this part is based on a narrative review.

The second part, Treatment, is partly based on the 2017 Minds Manual for Guideline Development. However, the content does not deviate substantially from actual clinical practice. The scope of treatment and key clinical issues are discussed, and clinical questions (CQ) are included for each clinical issue. A systematic review and evaluation of each evidence level was conducted for each CQ. The results were used to examine the strength of each recommendation and to make a final recommendation.

# Systematic review

The literature databases PubMed and Japanese medical journals (Ichushi-Web) were used in the systematic review for this guideline (See File S1). Although the aim was to produce a quantitative review, this review was mainly qualitative owing to insufficient research evidence.

In this guideline, depression is defined according to the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) and International Statistical Classification of Diseases (ICD) criteria, and most studies cited here have used diagnoses based on this definition. However, this systematic review was not limited to such studies, but included other studies if the quality of evidence was high.

"Late-life" is generally defined as 65 years or older. However, studies of late-life depression have used a broad age range (from  $\geq 50$  years to  $\geq 75$  years). Therefore, this guideline did not set clear age criteria for late-life depression, and the systematic review comprised a preliminary screening of articles with terms related to "elderly" or "late-life" in the titles. The PubMed search equations were unified to (elderly [TI] OR late-life [TI] OR geriatric [TI] OR older [TI]).

# Diagnosis of late-life depression Diagnosis

The diagnosis of depression in elderly patients is essentially the same as that in younger patients. The terms "depression" and "late-life" are defined in the "Systematic review" section of the Introduction. Late-life depression includes early-onset depression, characterized by younger onset and recurrence in older age, and late-onset depression, characterized by first onset in older age. There are differences in the etiology, clinical course, and prognosis of early-onset and late-onset depression.[2,3]

# Diseases and states to be considered

# Bipolar disorder

It is important to differentiate bipolar disorder (BD) from major depressive disorder (MDD) because the usefulness of antidepressants for treating patients with major depressive episode (MDE) owing to BD remains to be confirmed. Therefore, it is necessary to ask patients and their significant others about any history of manic/hypomanic episodes when examining elderly patients with MDE. Elderly patients with bipolar II disorder/BD not otherwise specified show significantly higher prevalence of early onset of first MDE (<60 years), frequent MDEs  $(>3)$ , and depressive mixed state (mixed depression) than those with MDD. These features could help to differentiate BD and MDD.

# Dementia, apathy, and delirium

A frequent problem in the diagnosis of late-life depression is differentiating dementia and delirium and determining their comorbidity. In particular, late-onset depressive symptoms may be early manifestations of dementia. Therefore, clinicians need to be able to differentially diagnose depression and dementia. At the same time, a possibility that the patient has both depression and dementia should also be considered.

The early stage of Alzheimer's disease (AD) is characterized by greater encoding impairment, resulting in impairments in recent memory and recognition. Response speed is often slower in depression, but not in early AD.[5]

Depressive symptoms in dementia with Lewy bodies (DLB) often appear prior to core symptoms such as cognitive impairment, visual hallucinations, and Parkinsonism. Rapid eye movement sleep behavior disorder; olfactory dysfunction; autonomic symptoms such as dyshidrosis, orthostatic dysregulation, and dysuria; and hypersensitivity to psychotropic drugs are often observed in early stages of DLB. Psychotic symptoms such as visual hallucinations, pareidolia, and delusional misidentifications such as Capgras syndrome and phantom border syndrome often appear in DLB.

The behavioral variant of frontotemporal dementia is a subtype of frontotemporal lobar degeneration, and is characterized by personality and behavior changes such as disinhibition, apathy (See below), perseveration/stereotypy and eating behavior changes (e.g., extreme preference for sweets and tendency to eat the same foods) are also observed from the early stage.

One meta-analysis $^{10}$  and one large subsequent study $^{11}$  found no evidence of antidepressant efficacy in depressed patients with dementia.

Apathy is often indistinguishable from the depressive state. Although some features of apathy are suggestive of a depressive syndrome, if apathy is present without the features of sad mood or distress, the clinician should be suspicious of the presence of frontal lobe injury, vascular disease, or other degenerative disorders. There is no established treatment for apathy,[12,13] and differential diagnosis is important to avoid unnecessary treatment. The points of clinical differentiation for depression and apathy are shown in Table 3.[14,15]

It may sometimes be necessary to differentiate depression and delirium.[16] Compared with depression, delirium has an acute onset and fluctuating symptoms, and it frequently occurs at night.

# Depressive states caused by physical or organic diseases, drug-induced depressive states

Points of differentiation for primary depressive disorder and depressive states caused by physical/organic disease and drugs include the following: (i) a temporal relationship between depressive states and the development, deterioration, and relief of physical/organic diseases, and drug use; (ii) the presence of unusual characteristics that differ from the characteristics of primary depressive disorders (e.g., age at onset, clinical course, family history); and (iii) research evidence of a direct association between depressive state and physical/organic diseases or drug use.[18]

# Clinical features of late-life depression Clinical symptoms

Although depressive mood and loss of interest are core symptoms of depression in both elderly and younger adults, other core symptoms include death wishes and pessimism in elderly patients,[19] and the core symptoms of fatigue and appetite changes in younger adults.[20,21] A meta-analysis of the phenomenology of depression in elderly adults compared with younger adults showed that elderly patients demonstrate more agitation, hypochondriasis, and somatic-general and somatic-gastrointestinal symptoms, but less guilt and loss of sexual interest.[22] Symptoms of severe guilt or delusions of guilt increase the risk of suicide.[23] Some reports indicate that  $45\%$  of elderly inpatients with depression show psychotic features.[1,24,25]

# Treatment response and remission

Older age, baseline depression severity, baseline anxiety symptoms, current episode duration, physical illness, and executive dysfunction are negative predictors, and early improvement is a positive predictor, of treatment outcomes.[26-28] One study showed that lifetime duration of depression was associated with lower placebo response rate,

resulting in a larger drug-placebo difference.[29] In elderly patients, psychotic depression can be treated with combination therapy (antidepressants and antipsychotics) or electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) as effectively as nonpsychotic depression.[30]

# Relapse and recurrence

The recurrence rate for depression is higher in elderly patients than in nonelderly patients.[31,32] Because subsyndromal symptoms after remission are associated with a higher recurrence rate,[33] sufficient maintenance therapy is important. One study showed that maintenance antidepressant therapy for 12 months prevented recurrence compared with a placebo (number needed to treat  $= 5$ ), and that discontinuation owing to side effects did not lead to a difference.[34]

# Prognosis

If elderly patients with depression are not treated appropriately, depressive symptoms can be prolonged and life threatening.[35] Because depressive symptoms in elderly people may be a prodromal feature of dementia,[36] and depressive episodes may be associated with an increased risk of dementia,[37,38] progression to dementia should be carefully considered. For example, one study showed that the prevalence of depression in patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI) was  $25\%$  to  $40\%$ [39] and depression was associated with an approximately 1.5-fold increased risk of progression to MCI in a population with normal cognition.[40] However, the risks and benefits of using cholinergic agents like donepezil for these patients need to be carefully considered.[41] Patients with late-life depression may have a risk of subsequently developing  $\mathrm{AD}^{38,42}$  and DLB.[43,44]

# Pharmacokinetics in elderly people

# Overview

Changes in pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics with aging Pharmacokinetic effects vary according to age, and drug concentrations in blood tend to increase for various reasons in elderly people. Therefore, adverse events caused by psychotropic agents increase with age (Table 4).<sup>45</sup> When administering psychotropic agents to elderly patients, current overall conditions (hepatic, renal, or cardiac dysfunctions) and concurrent use of other drugs must first be evaluated. Physicians must be careful when they prescribe medications to older patients and adverse events must be routinely evaluated. Checking physical conditions using blood tests and electrocardiogram is necessary.

# Psychotropic agents

# Antidepressants

The following adverse reactions to antidepressants prescribed to elderly patients with depression should be monitored: hyponatremia, falls, bone fractures, reduced bone density, arrhythmia, extrapyramidal symptoms, serotonin syndrome, and hemorrhage of the digestive tract. Because adverse events occur before antidepressant effects appear, early detection is necessary.

Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs), and TCAs are metabolized by the liver via cytochrome P450 (CYP). Therefore, this effect should be monitored, and the hepatic function of patients should be examined. For drugs metabolized by the kidney, renal functions such as creatinine clearance should be examined. Because SSRIs and SNRIs inhibit CYP, drug interactions must be considered. The level of drug interaction with CYP is high for fluvoxamine and paroxetine, moderate for duloxetine and sertraline, and low for escitalopram, mirtazapine, and venlafaxine.[46]

As TCA tolerability is lower than SSRI and SNRI tolerability, TCAs should be used cautiously. TCAs have anticholinergic effects, antihistamine effects, and anti-  $\alpha 1$ -adrenergic receptor actions. Blocking  $\alpha 1$  adrenergic receptors induces orthostatic hypotension and tachycardia, causing staggering and falls.

Antidepressants may cause the drug-induced syndrome of inappropriate secretion of antidiuretic hormone, which has a particularly high incidence in elderly patients.[47-49] TCAs and new antidepressants are risk factors for sudden cardiac death and lethal arrhythmias.[50-53]

In particular, high-dose TCAs must be used with caution.[54] Careful consideration should be given to QT prolongation associated with the TCAs, citalopram and escitalopram.[55,56] Rare but serious adverse reactions to SSRIs include upper gastrointestinal bleeding. The risk increases when nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs or antiplatelet drugs are used in combination, which is more common in elderly people.[57,58]

# Lithium carbonate

Lithium carbonate is generally well tolerated.[59] However, because its effective blood concentration  $(0.4 - 1.2\mathrm{mEq / L})$  is close to toxic concentrations  $(\geq 1.5\mathrm{mEq / L})$ , the possibility of lithium poisoning should be considered. It is contraindicated in patients with significant heart disorders, renal disorders, dehydration, and salt-restricted patients. When combined with drugs that can increase lithium carbonate concentrations in blood (e.g., nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, diuretics, angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin II receptor blockers), careful attention must be paid to blood concentrations.

# Antipsychotics

Notable adverse events of antipsychotics are extrapyramidal symptoms, anticholinergic adverse reactions, serious ventricular arrhythmia, and sudden cardiac death associated with QT prolongation. Elderly patients prescribed antipsychotics, particularly those with history of electrolyte abnormality and arrhythmia, require routine QTc interval measurements using electrocardiogram. Second-generation antipsychotics are recommended, and the initial dose should be reduced for elderly patients.

# Section B. Evaluation of the Condition and Basic Intervention

# Evaluation of the condition

The Geriatric Depression Scale (GDS) is a well-known self-rating scale developed to screen for late-life depression. The 15-item short version is the most widely used and its validity and reliability have been confirmed.[60] The Japanese version of the GDS was developed by Niino et al.,[61] and its validity and reliability have been confirmed.[62] The Patient Health Questionnaire (PHQ-9) is a useful self-rating screening instrument for major depression; the validity of the PHQ-9 and its Japanese version (J-PHQ-9)[63,64] has been confirmed. The Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HAM-D)[65] and the Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale (MADRS)[66] are representative observer rating scales. The HAM-D and the MADRS are used to evaluate the severity of diagnosed depression, not to diagnose depression. The GRID-HAM-D[67,68] and the Structured Interview Guide for the HAM-D (SIGH-D)[69] are revised versions of the HAM-D that use structured questions and anchors. The reliability of both revised versions of the HAM-D has been confirmed. The Japanese version of the SIGH-D was developed by Nakane and colleagues[70] and its validity and reliability have been confirmed.[71] The 17-item version of the SIGH-D also measures depression severity.[72] The Japanese version of the MADRS (MADRS-J) was developed by Kamishima et al., and its reliability and validity have been confirmed.[73] A structured version of the MADRS, the Structured Interview Guide for MADRS (SIGMA), has also been developed and shows higher reliability than the MADRS.[74]

Apathy is an important clinical symptom that can help to differentiate depression and dementia in elderly patients. The Apathy Evaluation Scale[75] and the Apathy Scale[76] are clinician-administered scales that measure apathy. The latter has been translated by Okada et al. to produce a "motivation score" and its validity has been confirmed.[77] The Clinical Assessment for Spontaneity, developed by the Japanese Society for Higher Brain Dysfunction, also measures apathy.[78]

It is important to regularly evaluate the patient's general condition and treatment/care as needed when treating late-life depression. Elderly patients with depression are often debilitated and may be weakened by appetite loss can weaken such patients.

# Basic intervention

Before introducing full-scale treatments such as drug therapy and systematic psychotherapy, psychoeducation and environmental adjustment for the patient, family, and caregivers should be implemented. It is recommended that treatment plans be developed for elderly patients, paying particular attention to their physical condition. Additionally, when treating late-life depression, it is important to show an appropriately receptive and empathic attitude toward older age mental health issues, which must be considered against the background of various loss experiences. In other words, elderly people may experience deterioration in several areas, such as physical function, reduction in social roles, and bereavement of close relatives, in a relatively short period of time.[79,80]

In addition, care is needed when treating late-life depression because it is associated with a high risk of suicide.

# CQ1. Is psychotherapy for late-life depression useful? Recommendation

Psychotherapy (cognitive-behavioral therapy [CBT] (B), problem-solving therapy (B), reminiscence therapy/life review therapy (B), and behavioral activation therapy (C)) can reduce depressive symptoms in elderly patients. These types of therapy are effective and not associated with any obvious adverse events (1B). However, there is no clear evidence of efficacy differences among therapies (D).

# Commentary

Meta-analytic results show that psychotherapy substantially improves depressive symptoms in elderly patients.[81] A meta-analysis of nine CBT studies and one interpersonal psychotherapy study showed a remission rate comparable to drug therapy and a lower dropout rate for psychotherapy, indicating a superior effect.[82]

Psychotherapy is generally administered for mild to moderate depression, but most meta-analyses do not specify the severity of depression, only the phase (acute or chronic) and treatment environment (outpatient or inpatient), although this information is often limited. A study by de Maat et al. showed that psychotherapy was more effective for mild depression than moderate depression but had no statistically significant effect on severe depression.[82] When prescribing psychotherapy, care must be taken to ensure it does not become a psychological burden for patients with severe depression.

There is no meta-analytic evidence of the effectiveness of supportive psychotherapy, owing to the technique's high versatility, nonspecificity, and lack of standardization. However, supportive psychotherapy is primary treatment for depression. In contrast, meta-analytic results show that specific psychotherapy, CBT[83-87] (B), problem-solving therapy,[88,89] reminiscence therapy/life review,[88,90,91] and behavioral activation therapy[92] are valid treatments.

Reminiscence therapy is very similar to life review, but life review is primarily aimed at the integration and acceptance of life through evaluation, a process that gives meaning and value to past events. In contrast, reminiscence therapy also includes the provision of enjoyable experiences that enhance quality of life, and the facilitation of interaction between participants.[93] Both reminiscence therapy and life review are effective treatments for late-life depression.[88] Although the effectiveness of interpersonal psychotherapy has been demonstrated, there are no meta-analytic studies on this therapy. Because elderly patients may show cognitive decline, the effectiveness of psychotherapy for this population is debatable.

There is evidence that the effect of CBT can last for 6 months,[86,87] although one systematic review suggests that its effectiveness is less prolonged.[94] One study showed that the effect of reminiscence therapy persisted for 3 months, shorter than that for CBT, but showed no effect after 6 months.[91]

Although there is evidence that individual CBT has a greater antidepressant effect than group therapy,[83] one report indicated no significant difference between these treatments.[81] The superiority of individual therapy or group therapy remains to be clarified.

Some studies indicate that the appropriate intervention period for psychotherapy is 7-12 weeks, although other studies report no relationship between the number of sessions and the effect, or show inconsistent findings. However, it should be noted that group therapy (vs. individual therapy) and longer interventions are associated with higher dropout rates.[84]

Many studies have found no clear difference in efficacy and tolerability between type of psychotherapy and antidepressants.[81,86,95,96] Additionally, it is difficult to match conditions such as evaluation method, target age, intervention method, and intervention period across intervention studies.

# CQ2. Which antidepressants are recommended for late-life depression?

# Recommendation

The efficacy of antidepressant treatment (for the acute phase and maintenance) for late-life depression has been demonstrated (A), although there is no clear evidence of differences in efficacy among antidepressants classes. The frequency of adverse events tends to be higher for TCAs than for newer antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs, and mirtazapine) and non-TCAs (B). Therefore, newer antidepressants or non-TCAs are recommended for late-life depression (1A). As there is insufficient evidence on differences in efficacy and safety among newer antidepressants, it is not currently possible to rank them (D).

# Commentary

Meta-analytic results for acute phase treatment of late-life depression with antidepressants show a significant effect of antidepressants versus placebo on response rates[26-99] and remission rates.[26,97] Meta-analytic studies on antidepressant maintenance therapy have also shown significant effects on relapse prevention.[99-101] However, in patients aged 65 years or older, some reports show no significant effects compared with placebo.[98,100] It is therefore important to fully assess the risks and benefits of antidepressant medication for elderly patients.

Meta-analyses comparing antidepressant efficacy and safety indicate that many studies report no differences in efficacy among antidepressant classes (SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, and non-TCAs).[26,97,99-104]

One network meta-analysis showed a significant efficacy for SSRIs and SNRIs versus placebos. $^{105}$

Although some studies report no difference in adverse events or discontinuation owing to adverse events among antidepressant classes,[101,102] others report that TCAs show more adverse events than SSRIs and non-TCAs.[103,106]

Although there are no placebo-controlled studies of mirtazapine in elderly patients, randomized comparison trials of late-life depression indicate that mirtazapine is not inferior in efficacy to SSRIs[107] and low doses of TCAs.[108] Mirtazapine also shows comparable or fewer adverse events than SSRIs, and can therefore be considered as effective and safe as other newer antidepressants.

# CQ3. Is a combination of drug therapy and psychotherapy more useful than monotherapy for late-life depression?

# Recommendation

There is no clear evidence that combined psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy for late-life depression is more effective than psychotherapy alone (D). However, it has been suggested that combination therapy may be superior to monotherapy for patients with chronic depression, and a combination of psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy may be useful (2D).

# Commentary

Many studies have compared the effects of monotherapy (pharmacotherapy or psychotherapy) and combination therapy (pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy) on depressive symptoms, but few have focused on late-life depression.[109]

The effects of CBT versus a combination of CBT and TCA on depression do not significantly differ; however, combination therapy is superior to TCAs alone and combination therapy is particularly effective for moderate depression.[110] In addition, some studies show that when combined with pharmacotherapy, CBT and interpersonal therapy are more effective than pharmacotherapy alone.[111-113]

One meta-analysis examined studies comparing the effects of combination therapy and monotherapy; however, it did not include studies after 2004 and presented conclusions about which therapy is superior.[96]

A systematic review by Hollon et al. $^{112}$  of studies of adults (including, but not limited to elderly patients) found that combined therapy was more effective than monotherapy in reducing chronic depression symptoms. There are also reports that combination therapy has a superior effect on remission rate compared with monotherapy, but the difference is not significant. $^{114,115}$

Taken together, these findings indicate that although the evidence is limited, combined pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy may be useful for chronic patients.

# CQ4.Is treatment with low-dose antidepressants useful for late-life depression?

# Recommendation

Evidence for the effectiveness of low-dose antidepressants for late-life depression is limited; however, newer antidepressants show efficacy and fewer adverse events at the minimal effective dose (C). Therefore, it is recommended that the efficacy of a minimal effective dose should first be confirmed when using antidepressants for late-life depression (1D).

# Commentary

For this CQ, studies on newer antidepressants (drugs and doses approved in Japan) were selected in accordance with the recommendations of CQ2.

A randomized controlled trial (RCT) of low-dose antidepressant treatment (10 weeks of paroxetine CR) for late-life depression demonstrated efficacy for both response and remission rates compared with placebo in patients receiving paroxetine CR  $25\mathrm{mg/day}$ , and reported fewer adverse events than for patients receiving  $50\mathrm{mg/day}$ .

Placebo-controlled studies of treatment with newer antidepressants for late-life depression have shown efficacy and safety for paroxetine, $^{117}$  fluvoxamine, $^{118}$  sertraline, $^{119,120}$  and duloxetine $^{121-125}$  versus placebo at usual doses. Escitalopram $^{126}$  and venlafaxine $^{127}$  showed no efficacy against placebos.

Comparative trials with other antidepressants have shown similar efficacy and safety to comparators at low doses (50 mg/day) of sertraline[128,129] and usual doses of milnacipran,[130] venlafaxine,[131-133] and mirtazapine.[107,108]

Evidence for the efficacy and safety of low-dose antidepressants for late-life depression is limited to data on paroxetine; however, owing to their efficacy and greater safety, similar benefits can be expected from other, newer antidepressants. Therefore, it is recommended to first confirm the efficacy of antidepressants at a minimal effective dose. However, because many newer antidepressants have shown efficacy and safety for elderly patients at the usual dose, it is recommended that when the minimal effective dose is insufficiently effective, it should be increased to the maximum dosage, while carefully monitoring adverse events. However, caution should be exercised when increasing the dose of TCAs owing to the appearance of severe adverse events (see Section A-iii and CQ2).

# CQ5. How long should pharmacotherapy for late-life depression be continued once remission has been achieved?

# Recommendation

Owing to the high risk of relapse/recurrence of late-life depression, continuation therapy should be maintained for at least 1 year after remission (1C). Then, the duration of maintenance therapy should be determined based on the risk of recurrence and the preferences of the patient and his or her family (2D).

# Commentary

Although elderly patients with depression have a high risk of relapse/recurrence,[31,32] the risk can be reduced by continuation and maintenance therapy.[34] A meta-analysis of three small RCTs showed that antidepressants can significantly reduce the recurrence rate for 1 year in patients who have achieved 6 months of remission.[101] An expert consensus guideline for late-life depression also recommends that the discontinuation of antidepressants should be considered 1 year after remission in those who have had a single episode of depression. Patients with two episodes of depression should continue antidepressants for 2 years. If the patient has had three or more episodes of depression, they should continue antidepressants for at least 3 years, or indefinitely (Fig. 1).[134] Because predictive factors for recurrence include the severity of the last episode; the number of treatments needed to achieve remission; the number of years between depression episodes; and the presence of risk factors for depression such as chronicity, disability related to medical multimorbidity, and lack of social support,[134] longer maintenance therapy should be considered in patients with these risk factors.

# CQ6. Is switching or combining antidepressants useful for late-life depression that is refractory to initial antidepressant treatment?

# Recommendation

Switching should be tried for refractory late-life depression, with a careful evaluation of both the number and class of previous adequate medication trials (2C). Antidepressant combinations that involve adding mirtazapine to an SSRI should be used with caution (2D).

# Commentary

There is a lack of studies that provide strong evidence for the usefulness of switching or combining antidepressants for refractory late-life depression. One RCT with mixed age patients with refractory MDD, of whom at least half were elderly, compared the relative usefulness of switching (to bupropion), combining antidepressants (adding bupropion), and aripiprazole augmentation strategies.[135] There was no significant difference in remission rate between switching and combining antidepressants, but aripiprazole augmentation modestly outperformed switching. However, the generalizability of the results to other antidepressants is unclear.

Regarding the evidence for the efficacy of the switching strategy for late-life depression, one open trial of extended-release venlafaxine examined the likelihood of antidepressant response as a function of previous antidepressant response.[136] The findings showed that remission rates with venlafaxine were inversely correlated with the number of previous adequate medication trials. If previous treatment trials included an SNRI, participants were even less likely to achieve remission with venlafaxine. One small single-blind RCT and three small open trials examined the usefulness of switching for refractory late-life depression; a response rate of  $20\% - 80\%$ .[133,137-139]

No studies have exclusively examined the usefulness of combining antidepressants for refractory late-life depression. Although studies that examined the usefulness of adding mirtazapine to an SSRI in mixed age patients with MDD have shown mixed results,[140,141] mirtazapine may have beneficial effects on sleep and appetite in elderly patients. Therefore, the addition of mirtazapine to an SSRI, starting at a very low dose (e.g.,  $7.5\mathrm{mg}$ ), may be cautiously considered.

# CQ7. Is augmentation therapy useful for late-life depression that is refractory to initial antidepressant treatment?

# Recommendation

Aripiprazole augmentation is the only strategy that has showed efficacy and a good tolerability profile for refractory late-life depression in an RCT with a reasonably sized sample (A). Because of the lack of sufficient long-term safety information, this regimen is only worth trying if tardive adverse events such as dyskinesia are monitored (2A). Although lithium augmentation has been reported to be effective for refractory late-life depression, its evidence level is lower than that of aripiprazole augmentation (B). Use of this regimen should include monitoring of serious adverse events and lithium plasma concentrations (2B).

# Commentary

In a 12-week double-blind RCT with elderly patients with MDD that was refractory to venlafaxine, aripiprazole augmentation therapy showed significantly higher remission rate.[142] Regarding adverse events, akathisia and Parkinsonism were more frequent in the aripiprazole group; however, the severity of these adverse events was mild. In the additional 12-week observation of the remitters, who continued the study medication under double-blind conditions, the emergence of tardive dyskinesia did not significantly differ between the two groups. However, it should be noted that little is known about the prevalence of tardive dyskinesia in the long-term use of aripiprazole ( $>6$  months). In a post-hoc study of elderly subjects extracted from three identically designed placebo-controlled RCTs for patients with MDD refractory to SSRIs or SNRIs, aripiprazole augmentation showed similar results to the above-mentioned

study. $^{143}$  This study also compared the usefulness of aripiprazole augmentation between elderly and young participants; the efficacy and dropout rate owing to adverse events were equivalent. Importantly, the rate of akathisia in elderly participants receiving aripiprazole augmentation was lower than that in younger participants.

The addition of lithium is the most frequently reported augmentation strategy for refractory late-life depression. However, there are no high-quality studies on this strategy. In a meta-analysis on pharmacotherapy for refractory late-life depression, the results from five case series/open studies of lithium augmentation were synthesized and a response rate of  $42.0\%$  of this regimen was revealed.[144] A small open RCT of lithium versus phenelzine augmentation therapy for late-life depression refractory to TCA or venlafaxine showed that significantly higher remission rate of lithium augmentation group compared to phenelzine augmentation group.[145] Because comorbid physical diseases and prescriptions of medications that potentially increase lithium blood concentration frequently occur in elderly patients, lithium should be administered carefully (see Section A-iii. Pharmacokinetics in elderly people).

There is little evidence on other augmentation strategies focusing on refractory late-life depression. One open study suggested the efficacy of risperidone augmentation.[146]

# CQ8. Is ECT useful compared with drug therapy for late-life depression?

# Recommendation

ECT monotherapy is more effective than drug therapy for late-life depression (C). In addition, the combination of ECT and drug therapy is more effective than drug therapy alone (C). However, ECT tends to cause side effects such as memory impairment, headache, dizziness, nausea, and vomiting in elderly patients (D), although there is no evidence that the effects on cognitive function are long-term (C). The above findings suggest that ECT can be more efficacious than drug therapy in some patients with late-life depression (2C).

# Commentary

ECT is a highly effective treatment for late-life depression,[147] and may have a more rapid therapeutic effect than drug therapy.[148] However, because of the need for general anesthesia and adverse events such as amnesia, ECT is used only when patients have severe suicidal ideation, undernutrition, or difficulties with antidepressant treatment. ECT is often used as a life-saving treatment, so it is difficult to conduct sham controlled RCTs of ECT.

A systematic review of five RCTs of late-life depression[149] reported that two of the three RCTs comparing the efficacy of ECT monotherapy and antidepressants showed that compared with antidepressants, ECT monotherapy was significantly more effective in improving depressive symptoms.[150,151] One RCT showed no significant difference.[152] A comparison of the efficacy of antidepressant and ECT combination therapy and antidepressant drugs alone showed that a combination of antidepressants and ECT is superior to antidepressants alone in improving depressive symptoms.[153,154] However, side effects such as memory impairment, headache, dizziness, nausea, and vomiting are observed with ECT treatment.[150-154] The findings of a systematic review of 121 studies, including observational studies of late-life depression, supports the efficacy and safety of ECT in the acute treatment of depression.[155]

Late-life depression is often accompanied by psychotic symptoms. No RCTs have compared the usefulness of ECT with drug therapy for late-life depression with psychotic symptoms, but a meta-analysis investigating the therapeutic effect of ECT for depression showed that psychotic symptoms and older age were good predictors of remission and response.[156]

Regarding differences in ECT stimulation conditions, there is little evidence for the usefulness of ECT in studies limited to late-life depression. However, a meta-analysis of "general" depression studies comparing a right unilateral brief pulse ECT group and a right

unilateral ultrabrief pulse ECT group found that the former condition was more effective, whereas the latter condition was associated with better cognitive function after treatment.[157]

A systematic review of the effect of ECT on cognitive function in late-life depression reported that ECT worsened cognitive function between and after seizures, but there was no evidence that the cognitive impairment persisted for 6 months or more.[158]

ECT can be a very effective treatment for severely depressed patients, patients with psychotic depression, and some treatment-resistant patients.

# CQ9. Is ECT useful for maintenance therapy for late-life depression in remission following a combination of ECT and drug therapy?

# Recommendation

A maintenance therapy of a combination of ECT and drug therapy is more effective in reducing depressive symptoms than drug therapy (A) for late-life depression in remission following a combination of ECT and drug therapy. However, there were no significant difference between these therapies on the long-term neurocognitive outcomes (A). In summary, a combination of ECT and drug therapy is useful for maintenance therapy (2A).

# Commentary

One high-quality RCT compared the usefulness of ECT plus drug therapy (venlafaxine, lithium carbonate) versus drug therapy alone (venlafaxine, lithium carbonate) as maintenance therapy (24 weeks) for late-life depression in remission following ECT and venlafaxine combination therapy. Patients who received a combination of ECT and drug therapy (venlafaxine, lithium carbonate) had significantly lower HAM-D scores.[159] However, there were no significant differences on all neurocognitive outcomes.[160]

Two RCTs compared the usefulness of nortriptyline plus ECT and nortriptyline maintenance therapy (2 years) in elderly patients with unipolar depression with psychotic symptoms who were in remission following a combination of ECT and nortriptyline. The time to relapse/recurrence was significantly longer in patients who received a combination of nortriptyline and ECT,[161] and the risk of relapse/recurrence was significantly lower.[162]

# CQ10. Is repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) therapy useful for late-life depression?

# Recommendation

rTMS therapy is more effective in reducing depressive symptoms than sham stimulation (B) in elderly patients who showed no therapeutic effect of drug therapy (excluding those with psychotic symptoms). However, patients may experience pain at the stimulation site (B). In addition, rTMS therapy is not associated with cognitive dysfunction (B) and is therefore useful treatment for late-life depression (2B).

# Commentary

rTMS is a minimally invasive technique that directly stimulates the cerebral cortex and modifies cortical and subcortical activity.[163,164] The efficacy and safety of rTMS therapy for depression have been demonstrated in several RCTs with sham stimulation as controls.[165-167] In Japan, the first therapeutic rTMS device was approved in September 2017. Its use is indicated for adults with moderate or severe depression who cannot obtain a therapeutic effect even from antidepressant therapy and who do not have psychotic symptoms. For detailed indications of rTMS therapy, please refer to the guidelines for appropriate use published in April 2018 by the Japanese Society of Psychiatry and Neurology.[168]

One RCT showed that rTMS therapy was significantly superior to sham stimulation in response and remission rates of elderly depressed patients.[169] This RCT used an H1 coil that can stimulate deeper areas of the brain.[167] Stimulation site pain is the most common side effect (Japanese Society of Psychiatry and Neurology, rTMS Proper Use Guidelines). In the above-mentioned trial, four  $(16\%)$

patients experienced stimulation site pain; all these patients had received real stimulation.[169]

Two other RCTs $^{170,171}$  with depressed patients with an average age of 60 years or older were not included in the recommendations because the stimulation intensity and number of stimuli were insufficient according to current standard stimulation conditions. $^{165-167}$

These findings suggest that rTMS is a useful treatment for late-life depression.

# CQ11. Are there other treatments that are useful for late-life depression?

# Recommendation

Exercise therapy (C) is a gentle exercise that has been shown to be effective and safe for late-life depression, and can therefore be recommended (2C). High-intensity light therapy (C) and diet therapy (C) also have some effects and are useful (2C). However, there is no clear evidence of efficacy differences between treatments according to type, intensity, amount, and duration of intervention (D).

# Commentary

Although various treatments for late-life depression have been reported, many studies target relatively mild depression (e.g., studies on depressive state evaluated using the GDS and those on minor depressive disorder and dysthymia) and some studies target MDD. This may be because patients with severe depression or poor general condition cannot participate in exercise therapy. Three meta-analyses of exercise therapy for relatively mildly depressed or depressed patients have been conducted, and all have reported that exercise therapy is safe and effective.[172-174] Regarding the content of exercise, one RCT showed that active exercise was associated with a significantly higher remission rate and shorter time to remission.[175] Therefore, exercise therapy is useful for elderly depressed patients who are able to exercise and are willing to actively participate in exercise therapy.[134] Two meta-analyses have been conducted on high-intensity light therapy for depression (including nonseasonal depression); both reported the effectiveness of this therapy.[176,177] However, one RCT reported that the reduction in depressive symptoms was not significant compared with the control group.[178]

There are several studies on the use of diet therapy for depression. Four meta-analyses examined the effect of omega-3 unsaturated fatty acids (which are abundant in blue fish and nuts) such as eicosapentaenoic acid and docosahexaenoic acid. One study reported that these are effective only for mild to moderate depression,[179] one study found no evidence of effectiveness,[180] and the other two studies reported a limited efficacy owing to large between-study variation.[181,182] A cohort study in Japan found that a high intake of omega-3 unsaturated fatty acids was associated with a low prevalence of major depression.[183]

These findings suggest that there is some evidence that exercise therapy is efficacious and safe and should be recommended for relatively mild depression in elderly patients who are able to exercise and wish to participate in exercise therapy. There is also some evidence for the effectiveness of high-intensity light therapy. Although evidence for the effectiveness of diet therapy varies widely, diet therapy is associated with few adverse events and so is considered useful.

# Conclusion

In the diagnosis and treatment of late-life depression, it is important to first carefully consider the differentiation of depression from BD, dementia, depressive states caused by physical and organic brain disease, and drug effects. However, manic episodes can occur in old age, dementia can be comorbid, and depression can transition to dementia. It is therefore important to keep these patterns in mind when monitoring the progress of patients with depression. It is necessary to fully understand the clinical characteristics of late-life depression, understand the psychosocial background that affects the etiology and psychopathology of each patient, evaluate the patient's condition, and provide basic interventions based on these factors.

Regarding specific treatments, psychotherapy, pharmacotherapy, and ECT/TMS have been shown to be useful for late-life depression. However, particularly when prescribing pharmacotherapy, it is important to understand the effects of pharmacokinetics in elderly patients, and to take extra care in monitoring the appearance of adverse events in elderly patients compared with younger patients.

Owing to the high heterogeneity of late-life depression and the lower number of RCTs conducted with older patients with MDD compared with younger patients with MDD, treatment recommendations for late-life depression can be made with less confidence than treatment recommendations for depression in younger adults. Therefore, when treating late-life depression, it is necessary to take into consideration various conditions and situations, and to provide detailed treatment tailored to each patient.
