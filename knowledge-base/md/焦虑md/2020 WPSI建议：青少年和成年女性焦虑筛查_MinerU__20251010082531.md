# Screening for Anxiety in Adolescent and Adult Women: A Recommendation From the Women's Preventive Services Initiative

Description: The Women's Preventive Services Initiative (WPSI), a national coalition of women's health professional organizations and patient representatives, developed a recommendation on screening for anxiety in adolescent and adult women to improve detection; achieve earlier diagnosis and treatment; and improve health, function, and well-being. The WPSI's recommendations are intended to guide clinical practice and coverage of services for the Health Resources and Services Administration and other stakeholders. The target audience for this recommendation includes all clinicians providing preventive health care to women, particularly in primary care settings. This recommendation applies to women and adolescent girls aged 13 years or older who are not currently diagnosed with anxiety disorders, including pregnant and postpartum women.

Methods: The WPSI developed this recommendation after evaluating results of a systematic review of the effectiveness of screening, accuracy of screening instruments, and benefits and harms of treatments in adolescent girls and adult women. No studies directly evaluated the overall effectiveness or harms of screening for anxiety. Twenty-seven screening instruments and their variations were moderately to highly accurate in identifying anxiety (33 individual studies and 2 systematic reviews; 171 stud

ies total). Symptoms improved and relapse rates decreased with psychological therapies (246 randomized controlled trials [RCTs] in 5 systematic reviews) and with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors or selective serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (126 RCTs in 3 systematic reviews). The WPSI also considered the effect of screening on symptom progression and identification of associated and underlying conditions, as well as implementation factors.

Recommendation: The WPSI recommends screening for anxiety in women and adolescent girls aged 13 years or older who are not currently diagnosed with anxiety disorders, including pregnant and postpartum women. Optimal screening intervals are unknown, and clinical judgment should be used to determine frequency. When screening suggests the presence of anxiety, further evaluation is necessary to establish the diagnosis and determine appropriate treatment and follow-up.

Anxiety disorders comprise several related conditions characterized by excessive, uncontrollable worry (1). These include generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, social or school anxiety disorder, and other specific types (Table 1) (1). The term anxiety disorders is used to refer to all types collectively.

Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent and among the most consequential biological health disorders in the United States (2). Lifetime prevalence of anxiety disorders in women is approximately  $40\%$ , twice that in men (2). Anxiety disorders are associated with biological, sex-specific mechanisms (3, 4) and can be a common manifestation of underlying issues, including posttraumatic stress disorder, sexual harassment and assault, and other experiences common among women. In adolescent girls, worries about school performance, concerns about appearance, earlier sexualization, changing media and consumer culture, and poor self-esteem are important triggers (5). Anxiety disorders can begin in childhood at a median age of 11 years, can develop in mid-adulthood, and often decline beginning at approximately age 60 years (2). During pregnancy and the postpartum period, anxiety disorders increase in both frequency and effects, including effects on the infant and family (6). Studies have shown

a genetic component in the cause of anxiety disorders, with heritability estimates ranging from  $30\%$  to  $50\%$  (7).

Anxiety disorders greatly impair function in school, work, social settings, and personal relationships. Associated somatic symptoms cause a high proportion of health care visits for unspecified conditions that may lead to extensive evaluations. Anxiety disorders occur frequently with comorbid conditions, such as substance use and posttraumatic stress disorder, but most notably with depressive disorders (8-10). The estimated prevalence of anxiety disorders in patients with major depressive disorders is  $56\%$  (11), and the presence of an anxiety disorder was significantly associated with at least 1 suicide attempt in a large-scale epidemiologic study (12). Costs attributable to anxiety disorders, whether for direct health care or indirect expenses, are in the billions of dollars each year in the United States (13).

Screening for anxiety is not currently a routine preventive practice in clinical settings, although the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) recommends screening for depression in adolescents and adults (14). Anxiety screening, like depression screening, can be done quickly and efficiently in an intake encounter at a primary care or obstetric office using a brief screening tool on a touchscreen or in writing. As in screening for depression, the goal of screening for anxiety is to identify persons for further evaluation of the whole spectrum of anxiety disorders and related conditions. Diagnosis of an anxiety disorder requires meeting specific criteria from the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (1) after ruling out alternative causes of anxiety, such as thyroid disease or drug use. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and other forms of psychotherapy (15) are initial treatments for most patients, whereas medications are usually secondary (most commonly selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors [SSRIs] and selective serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors [SNRIs]) (16).

Despite the high prevalence and burden of anxiety disorders in the general population (particularly among women) and the availability of methods for screening (17, 18), diagnosis, and treatment, only an estimated  $20\%$  of affected persons seek care for these conditions (19). The purpose of this recommendation is to increase detection of anxiety disorders in adolescent and adult women through screening to achieve earlier diagnosis of specific types of anxiety and co-occurring conditions; initiate appropriate treatment; and improve health, function, and well-being.

# WOMEN'S PREVENTIVE SERVICES INITIATIVE

The Women's Preventive Services Initiative (WPSI) is a national coalition of 21 health professional organizations and patient representatives that develops, reviews, updates, and disseminates evidence-based clinical recommendations for women's preventive health care services in the United States (20). The WPSI considers conditions that are unique to, more common in, or different in women. The WPSI is supported by the Health Resources and Services Administration of the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services and led by the American College of Obste-

tricians and Gynecologists. Methods and processes of the WPSI and persons involved are detailed on a website (21) and in previous publications (22). The Appendix (available at Annals.org) lists current members and their organizations.

The WPSI focuses on gaps in current recommendations about preventive services for women aged 13 years or older. These include services for which the USPSTF provided recommendations that were indeterminate or had narrow scopes; areas with new research; and topics not addressed in previous recommendations by the USPSTF, Bright Futures, or the Institute of Medicine Committee on Preventive Services for Women (23).

The WPSI bases recommendations on evidence of both benefits and harms of an intervention or service and an assessment of the balance between them (21). Cost is not considered in this assessment because it is outside the scope of the WPSI, which bases its recommendations on clinical benefits and harms. The WPSI recognizes that many of the most important clinical questions about effective use of preventive services are not addressed by research studies, particularly those involving adolescents, pregnant and postpartum women, or older women.

The WPSI based its rationale for anxiety screening on several considerations. Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent mental health disorders in women, and the problems created by an untreated anxiety disorder can impair function in all areas of a woman's life. Screening can identify symptoms in undiagnosed women who may benefit from appropriate evaluation and treatment. Effective screening may lead to earlier or timelier treatment (including behavioral and medical interventions) and result in improved clinical outcomes, such as symptoms, function, and quality of life. Screening may also lead to the detection of associated conditions, such as depression and posttraumatic stress disorder, which may also require treatment.

# RECOMMENDATION FOCUS AND TARGET POPULATION

This is a new recommendation based on evidence of the benefits and harms of screening for anxiety in

women; it includes a new systematic review of studies of the accuracy of screening instruments and recently published systematic reviews on the benefits and harms of treatments (24, 25). No clinical recommendations currently exist for anxiety screening, although several guideline groups (including the USPSTF) recommend screening for depression, which is frequently associated with anxiety (14). The target audience for this recommendation includes all clinicians providing preventive health care to women, particularly in primary care settings, as well as additional stakeholders. This recommendation applies to women and adolescent girls aged 13 years or older who are not currently diagnosed with anxiety disorders, including pregnant and postpartum women.

# METHODS

# WPSI Topic Selection and Recommendation Development

The topic of screening for anxiety was selected by the WPSI Advisory Panel, approved by a vote of members of the Multidisciplinary Steering Committee, and posted on the WPSI website for public comment. The scope and key questions were developed by the Advisory Panel with additional input from subject matter experts on the Multidisciplinary Steering Committee.

A systematic review addressing the key questions was done by investigators at the Pacific Northwest Evidence-based Practice Center using established methods (21, 26), including development of a research protocol, comprehensive searches of the literature, selection of studies on the basis of predetermined eligibility criteria, and abstraction of study data. Investigators applied critical appraisal criteria to independently dual-rate the quality and applicability of each study using USPSTF methods (27) and graded the overall strength of evidence and applicability of each question using criteria from the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality Effective Health Care Program (28). Grading of evidence and applicability was initially assessed by 1 investigator and then reviewed by all investigators for consensus. A report of the evidence review was provided to all members of the WPSI Advisory Panel and Multidisciplinary Steering Committee and presented and discussed at an in-person meeting and during subsequent conference calls.

Members of the Multidisciplinary Steering Committee developed a draft recommendation. In the absence of direct evidence (that is, randomized controlled trials [RCTs] of screening vs. no screening reporting health outcomes), the WPSI considers the strength of the chain of indirect evidence (that is, accuracy of screening to identify individuals for diagnostic evaluation and effectiveness of treatments in improving health outcomes in patients with anxiety), as the USPSTF does for most of its recommendations (27). The draft recommendation was made publicly available online for a 6-week comment period. The WPSI Advisory Panel and Multidisciplinary Steering Committee reviewed comments, and the draft was revised accordingly. A revised

version was adopted by the Health Resources and Services Administration in December 2019; it will be incorporated into the summary of covered benefits for preventive services without cost sharing as required by the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act immediately or no later than 1 January 2021, depending on individual coverage (29). Covered benefits apply to most group health plans and issuers of group and individual health insurance coverage, as well as to persons who qualify for Medicaid on the basis of Medicaid expansion under the Affordable Care Act.

# Evidence Review

The systematic review focused on the following 3 key questions about screening for anxiety in women and adolescent girls aged 13 years or older, including pregnant and postpartum women. First, what is the effectiveness of screening and evaluation for anxiety in improving symptoms, function, and quality of life? Second, what is the accuracy of methods to screen for anxiety, including differences across population groups? And third, what are potential harms of screening? Key questions were addressed by reviewing all relevant individual studies and systematic reviews. Two additional questions about the effectiveness and harms of treatments for anxiety were addressed by reviewing recently published systematic reviews of RCTs relevant to current treatment in the United States. Details of the systematic review are summarized in a technical report (24) and accompanying article (25).

# EFFECTIVENESS AND HARMS OF SCREENING

The systematic review identified no studies that directly evaluated the overall effectiveness or harms of screening.

# Accuracy of Screening Instruments

Thirty-three studies and 2 systematic reviews (171 studies total) evaluated the accuracy of 27 screening instruments and their variations (Appendix Table, available at Annals.org). Of included studies, 5 met criteria for good quality (18, 30-33), 18 for fair (17, 34-50), and 10 for poor (51-60). Studies enrolled mostly women and were conducted in general adult populations (17, 18, 30-32, 34, 36-43, 51-53), older adults (33, 54, 55), pregnant and postpartum women (44-47, 56-59, 61), or adolescents (48-50, 60, 62). Screening instruments were clinician- or self-administered questionnaires that describe symptoms of anxiety and were designed for use in clinical practice. Responses were typically scored using a Likert scale or other point system. Most studies enrolled women in community, primary care, or obstetrics clinics and used criteria from the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders as the reference standard. Studies did not provide information to assess differences across populations by sociodemographic or cultural group, comorbid conditions, or use of additional medications.

In 17 studies of 10 instruments for general adult populations, 3 versions of the Generalized Anxiety Disorder Scale, the anxiety module of the Patient Health

Questionnaire, demonstrated moderate to high accuracy (area under the receiver-operating characteristic curve, 0.77 to 0.94) (17, 18, 31, 35, 38, 40-42). Single studies of additional screening instruments also showed moderate to high performance (30, 32, 34, 36, 37, 39, 52, 53), whereas 2 others indicated lower values on performance measures (43, 51). Three studies evaluating 5 screening instruments in adults aged 60 years or older reported a range of results (area under the receiver-operating characteristic curve, 0.57 to 0.80) (33, 54, 55).

Nine studies and 1 systematic review (61) evaluated 12 screening instruments in pregnant and postpartum women. Studies of the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale, a 10-item, self-reported measure commonly used in the United States to assess pregnant and postpartum women for symptoms of depression and emotional distress, indicated areas under the receiver-operating characteristic curve for anxiety ranging from 0.62 to 0.73 (44, 45, 56, 57, 59). Additional instruments, including those commonly used in general populations (such as the Generalized Anxiety Disorder Scale, State-Trait Anxiety Inventory, General Health Questionnaire, and Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale), demonstrated moderate to high performance (45-47, 56-59, 61), whereas 2 others had lower values on performance measures (45). Four studies (48-50, 60) and 1 systematic review (62) of screening instruments for adolescents, including 4 variations of the Screen for Child Anxiety Related Emotional Disorders scale (48-50), indicated generally moderate-level performance.

# Effectiveness of Treatment

The effectiveness of treatments for anxiety has been evaluated by trials summarized in systematic reviews of psychological (15, 63-66) and pharmacologic therapies (67-75). Psychological therapy, predominantly CBT, is the initial treatment approach for most patients. Five systematic reviews that included 246 RCTs of adolescents and adults generally indicate improved symptoms and decreased relapse rates with psychological therapies compared with waitlist controls, active treatment groups, usual care, or psychological placebos (15, 63-66).

The first-line pharmacologic therapies for anxiety disorders in adults are SSRIs and SNRIs. Although not all drugs in these classes are approved for adolescents by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, SSRIs and SNRIs are first-line treatments for adolescents as well. Improvement in anxiety based on clinician evaluations was statistically significant for all SSRIs and SNRIs evaluated in 126 placebo-controlled RCTs of adolescents and adults in 3 systematic reviews (67-75). Information on older patients was limited, and pregnant and postpartum women were not included in trials, although these medications are widely used in these patient groups.

# Harms of Treatment

In a Cochrane review of 25 studies of psychological therapies for adults, attrition for any reason after treat

ment did not differ between CBT and control groups or between CBT and various active treatment groups (64). Results were similar in a separate Cochrane review of media-delivered CBT (66). Trials reported no other harms due to psychological therapies.

A systematic review of trials of antianxiety medications in adults indicated no differences in discontinuation between treatment and pill placebo groups (75). Common adverse events were diarrhea, dizziness, dry mouth, fatigue, headache, nausea, sexual dysfunction, sweating, tremor, and weight gain (76). In a Cochrane review of SSRIs and SNRs in children and adolescents, dropouts due to adverse effects did not differ between treatment and placebo groups (70, 74). The most frequent adverse effects were abdominal pain and nausea for fluoxetine; abdominal discomfort, increased motor activity, vomiting, tiredness and fatigue, muscle and joint pain, and decreased appetite for fluvoxamine; and anorexia for sertraline. In general, trials were too small or too short to assess more serious or long-term adverse events. Older patients were not specifically studied, and pregnant women were not included.

# Assessment of Benefits and Harms

Table 2 summarizes the evidence. The investigators identified no trials of screening for anxiety that evaluated overall effectiveness and harms. The strength of evidence for the accuracy of brief questionnaires administered by clinicians or patients themselves to identify women with anxiety is high, on the basis of 33 studies and 2 reviews evaluating 27 clinical screening instruments and their variations. Instruments were generally accurate in identifying symptoms of anxiety in studies of general adult populations, older adults, pregnant and postpartum women, and adolescents in predominantly primary care settings. The strength of evidence for the effectiveness of treatment ranges from moderate to high, and that for harms of treatment is low for CBT and moderate for medications.

Like the previous recommendations from the Institute of Medicine on preventive services for women (23), the WPSI recommendations are based on reaching a threshold of supportive evidence. Overall, the WPSI determined that the balance of benefits and harms would likely be favorable on the basis of the high prevalence of anxiety in women; its substantial effect on health, function, and quality of life; and evidence on the accuracy of screening instruments in primary care settings and the effectiveness and harms of treatment.

# RECOMMENDATIONS

The WPSI recommends screening for anxiety in adolescent and adult women aged 13 years or older, including pregnant and postpartum women. Optimal screening intervals are unknown, and clinical judgment should be used to determine screening frequency. The WPSI provides additional recommendations about implementation. Clinicians may consider screening for anxiety in conjunction with screening for depression, which is already recommended by the USPSTF, because of the frequent co-occurrence of anxiety and depressive disorders. Validated instruments that screen for both disorders simultaneously may be clinically efficient in practice settings and can be given via a written

or touchscreen method in a waiting area or by a clinician during an interview. Examples of these instruments include the Patient Health Questionnaire-4 for anxiety and depression and the Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale in adult women, the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale in pregnant and postpartum women, and the Bright Futures Pediatric Symptom Checklist-Youth Report in adolescents and young women (78). Research comparing depression screening using screening tools versus clinical interviews found that the

tools were twice as accurate in detecting depression (79). When screening suggests the presence of anxiety, further evaluation is necessary to establish a clinical diagnosis and determine appropriate treatment. Screening should ideally be implemented in conjunction with collaborative and team-based approaches for effective treatment and appropriate follow-up.

In a previous recommendation adopted by the Health Resources and Services Administration, the WPSI recommended that women receive at least 1 preventive health care visit each year beginning in adolescence and continuing across the lifespan (80). The primary purpose of this visit is the delivery and coordination of recommended preventive services, as determined by age and risk factors. This preventive health visit could be an opportunity to screen for anxiety, potentially by including screening questions on an intake form or in a check-in process.

# FUTURE RESEARCH

No trials of anxiety screening have yet evaluated overall effectiveness or potential harms, including labeling, misdiagnosis, overdiagnosis, and others. Additional studies are needed to determine optimal methods for screening, diagnosis, treatment, and coordination of systems to support all of the steps in the clinical pathway. These include studies comparing routine versus opportunistic screening and different screening intervals. Long-term outcomes are needed to more accurately weight the benefits and harms of screening.

Research to understand changes in the incidence and prevalence of anxiety over time and the influence of specific risk factors, triggers, mediators, and moderators would help focus prevention and screening at optimal times and intervals. This includes quantifying the role of genetic diatheses, studying the natural history of anxiety disorders over the course of the lifetime, and studying the correctable psychosocial stressors unique to women that may contribute to the increased prevalence of anxiety disorders in women. For example, how do major life events, such as marriage, divorce, pregnancy, care-taking roles, loss of a loved one, and change in employment, affect the course of anxiety over a person's lifetime? In addition, for coexisting conditions, such as depression and anxiety, does treatment of 1 condition affect the manifestation of the other? More trials (including head-to-head trials) comparing the effectiveness and harms of various prevention and treatment approaches, as well as combinations and sequences of treatments, are needed to improve decision making by patients and clinicians.

Studies on the differences in screening, clinical presentation, and treatment of depression based on ethnic and racial differences have produced important results. A study of young, Spanish-speaking women in the perinatal period indicated that their cutoff score for depression on the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale was significantly lower than that of white or African American women, which affects the instrument's

accuracy in this population (81). Because disparities have been documented in screening, diagnosis, and treatment between racial/ethnic groups (82), further study of this effect in anxiety screening is necessary to understand the most effective approaches to screening specific populations. Future research should also examine the influence of various social determinants of health, and ascertain the differences between adolescent and adult anxiety to determine whether screening and treatment approaches should differ by age or life stage.

# CONCLUSION

Anxiety disorders adversely affect the health, function, and quality of life of nearly half of American women at some point in their lives, but at this time, the disorders often go unrecognized and untreated. Although routine screening for depression is currently a standard of care in clinical practice, no recommendation for anxiety screening has been issued by guideline groups. The WPSI recommendation fills a long-standing gap in screening for women. Standardized screening for anxiety in routine clinical practice, particularly as part of a preventive health care visit in combination with depression screening, could identify adolescent and adult women with anxiety and initiate diagnostic evaluations and treatment. Because anxiety disorders can be successfully treated, early detection through the use of a brief questionnaire could prevent years of symptoms and impairment in the lives of women at every stage of life.
