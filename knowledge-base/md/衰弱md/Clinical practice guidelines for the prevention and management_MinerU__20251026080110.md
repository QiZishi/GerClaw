# Abstract

Aims: To systematically search for clinical practice guidelines focusing on the prevention and management of frailty, to evaluate their methodological quality and to synthesize the consensus recommendations.

Design: A systematic review.

Data Sources: Guideline websites, related professional association websites and electronic databases were systematically searched through 4 November 2020.

Review Methods: We evaluated the methodological quality of the eligible guidelines using the Appraisal of Guidelines Research and Evaluation II (AGREE II). Two reviewers synthesized the consensus recommendations proposed by at least two guidelines.

Results: Eight guidelines met the eligibility criteria and were included in the review. The mean scores of the six domains were as follows: the 'scope and purpose' domain scored  $88.0\%$ , the 'clarity of presentation' domain scored  $81.9\%$ , the 'stakeholder involvement' domain scored  $63.4\%$ , the 'editorial independence' domain scored  $62.2\%$ , the 'rigour of development' domain scored  $61.1\%$  and the 'applicability' domain scored  $57.8\%$ . In total, we synthesized 23 recommendations for the prevention and management of frailty that are consistent among the included guidelines.

Conclusions: The number of clinical practice guidelines for the prevention and management of frailty is limited. The methodological quality of existing guidelines needs to be improved. Our synthesized findings provide an intuitive, convenient and summative reference resource for frailty prevention and management. It' is worth noting that recommendations described in the included guidelines require additional detail.

Impact: Although the prevention and management of frailty is urgent, there is currently a lack of evidences guiding these processes, especially in the prevention. The methodological quality of existing guidelines is insufficient, and the recommendations described in the guidelines require additional detail. Therefore, users of these guidelines, especially nurses, should make a careful decision according to the specific situation when using. Nurses also have a key role in providing more clinical evidences for the improvement of the quality of the guidelines.

# 1 | INTRODUCTION

Frailty is a complex geriatric syndrome resulting from the decline of multiple organ system function characterized by decreasing muscle strength, endurance and physiological function (Fried et al., 2004). The incidence rate of frailty varies due to inconsistency in the measuring tools (Sternberg et al., 2011); the pooled prevalence was  $12\%$  using physical frailty measures and  $24\%$  using the deficit accumulation model (O'Caoimh et al., 2020). The central problem with frailty is the potential for serious adverse outcomes, such as falls, hospitalization, disability and even death after a seemingly minor stressor event or change (Fried et al., 2001; Ng et al., 2015), threatening the quality of life in older adults. Frailty is also costly, and results from a recent meta-analysis indicated that the mean total healthcare cost for frail women was $10,755 every year, approximately three times that of non-frail older females (Ensrud et al., 2018).

# 1.1 Background

As one can imagine, frailty is considered the most serious manifestation of ageing, the most important problem affecting the health of older adults and also represents an important source of medical care and economic burden (Dedeyne et al., 2017). Therefore, effective strategies that target the prevention and management of frailty will likely reduce these burdens. However, due to its high prevalence, there will be more older people suffering from frailty due to the increased ageing population (Walston et al., 2018) from a macroscopic view. In addition, there is heterogeneity in the clinical manifestations of frailty (Dent, Martin, et al., 2019; Dent, Morley, et al., 2019) from the perspective of individuality. As a result, the prevention and management of frailty is challenging.

CPGs were defined by the Institute of Medicine (IOM) as statements that include recommendations intended to optimize patient care for a specific clinical situation in 1990 (Institute of Medicine, 1990). In 2011, IOM updated the definition of CPGs to optimal recommendations that are informed by a systematic review of evidence and an assessment of the benefits and harms of alternative care options, emphasizing the importance of evidence-based methodology (Graham et al., 2011). To date, multiple versions of CPGs in the same or different fields have gradually emerged, which were developed by different institutions and organizations worldwide with various specific clinical problems, resulting in inconsistent development processes and methods and leading to uneven quality of the CPGs (Deng et al., 2016). Although it is generally accepted that the appropriate use of these guidelines will improve clinical practice, the significance of the guidelines also depends on their quality of methodology (Cook & Giacomini, 1999; Woolf et al., 1999). Therefore, the methodological quality of these CPGs should be evaluated before their implementation (Zeng et al., 2015). The Appraisal of Guidelines Research and Evaluation II (AGREE II; AGREE Next Steps Consortium, 2009), a revised version of the AGREE, is widely used to evaluate the methodological quality of CPGs and was initially

developed and published by the AGREE collaboration. A systematic review showed that AGREE is the most effective guideline assessment system among 24 different tools (Vlayen et al., 2005).

To promote frailty management, CPGs for frailty have emerged in recent years, such as CPGs developed by the British Geriatrics Society (BGS; British Geriatrics Society, 2014), the National Institute of Clinical Evidence (NICE; National Institute for Health & Clinical Excellence, 2015, 2016), and the Clinical Practice Guidelines and Protocols in British Columbia (BC; Clinical Practice Guidelines & Protocols in British Columbia, 2017). CPGs can provide professional guidance for the clinical practice of frailty prevention and management, making effective management of frailty possible. However, to date, no study has evaluated the quality of these guidelines, so we do not know the significance of these guidelines in clinical application, nor do we understand the current status of frailty management guidelines, which has become one of the barriers to the application of existing guidelines to manage frailty and the development of new guidelines. In addition, there is a lack of specific standards for the prevention and management of frailty at present. Therefore, it is necessary to work out consistent recommendations across the guidelines to provide more explicit guidance and suggestions for clinical decision making.

# 2 THE REVIEW

# 2.1 Aims

The purpose of this current systematic review was to systematically search for CPGs focusing on the prevention and/or management of frailty, to evaluate their methodological quality using AGREE II and to synthesize the consensus recommendations to inform decision making by health practitioners.

# 2.2 Design

The current systematic review was conducted and reported in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Review and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines (Moher et al., 2009).

# 2.3 Search methods

# 2.3.1 | Search strategy

Two well-trained researchers conducted a systematic retrieval of guideline websites, related professional association websites and electronic databases on 4 November 2020. Guideline websites included the National Institute of Clinical Evidence (NICE), Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network (SIGN), Registered Nurses Association of Ontario (RNAO) and ClinicalKey. Professional association websites included the British Geriatrics Society (BGS) and the

American Geriatrics Society (AGS). Electronic databases included PubMed, Embase and three Chinese databases (CNKI, WanFang Data and VIP). To ensure the comprehensiveness of the retrieval, we also tracked relevant references, citations and articles identified from the first search.

The combination of free-text and subject terms was used in the search strategy with Boolean conjunction (OR/AND) as the connection of search terms. The complete search formula was as follows: ("Frailty" [Mesh]) OR ("Frail Elderly" [Mesh])) OR (Frailty [Title/Abstract])) OR (Frail Elderly [Title/Abstract])) AND ((guideline [Title/Abstract]) OR (guidance [Title/Abstract])).

# 2.3.2 | Eligibility criteria

According to the eligibility criteria, screening of the desired CPGs was independently conducted by two reviewers. Only CPGs published in English and Chinese were used. The eligibility criteria were as follows: (1) published clinical practice guidelines on the prevention or/and management of frail older adults, or related. Management here meant the identification, screening, assessment, interventions and comprehensive care or management of frailty; (2) completely reported information, including title, catalog, recommendations, evidence, references, etc.; (3) for the revised guidelines, we only included the latest version. The exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) the protocol, draft, interpretation or excerpt of the guidelines; and (2) duplicate guidelines in different databases or different languages.

# 2.4 Data abstraction

Two reviewers independently conducted a three-step article selection by reviewing the title, abstract and full text in turn, according to the inclusion and exclusion criteria. When the two reviewers encountered divergences or doubts during the screening process, the third reviewer was consulted to negotiate a final result.

Extracted information from the eligible guidelines was conducted by two independent reviewers using a unified table. The extracted information included guideline name, country or region, year of publication, author or development institution, focus (frailty prevention or management), target users, whether guidelines were based on a systematic review, whether grade of recommendation was given, whether guidelines updated and recommendations. The method to address disagreements was the same as above.

# 2.5 Synthesis

There were no data that could be synthesized in this study, so we did not conduct a meta-analysis. We reviewed the methodological quality of the included guidelines, extracted recommendations from the included guidelines and synthesized the consensus recommendations solving the same clinical problem. Recommendations

proposed by two or more guidelines were included, while the recommendations only given by one guideline were excluded from our synthesis. Two independent reviewers synthesized the consensus recommendations.

# 2.6 Quality appraisal

AGREE II (AGREE Next Steps Consortium, 2009) was used to assess the methodological quality of eligible CPGs. The instrument contains six domains with a total of 23 items: scope and purpose (3 items), stakeholder involvement (3 items), rigour of development (8 items), clarity of presentation (3 items), applicability (4 items) and editorial independence (2 items). The score of each item ranges from 1 to 7, where 1 point indicates no information related to AGREE II or very poor reporting, while 7 point indicates high report quality, meeting all the criteria and conditions required by AGREE II.

Three well-trained researchers assessed the quality of all eligible CPGs in strict accordance with the AGREE II criteria. First, the three researchers reviewed the AGREE II manual to ensure that their understanding of each item was consistent with that of the assessment method. Then, an independent trial assessment was performed to guarantee the efficacy of training together. After ensuring consistency among the three researchers, independent quality assessment began. If there is a large difference in the assessment results of a certain item, it was resolved by negotiation.

# 2.7 Statistical analysis

A quality score is calculated for each of the six AGREE II domains. Score of each domain of the CPGs = [(obtained score of each domain - minimum possible score)/(maximum possible score of each domain - minimum possible score)] × 100% (AGREE Next Steps Consortium, 2009). As defined by AGREE II, we considered a CPG satisfactory if it scored at least 50% on all six domains (Deng et al., 2016). Interrater reliability within each CPG was examined using the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) with a 95% confidence interval. The degree of agreement was as follows: ICC<0.4 indicates poor consistency; 0.4–0.74 indicates general consistency and ≥0.75 indicates good consistency (Li et al., 2000). Data were analysed using SPSS version 23.0.

# 3.3 Recommendations of CPGs

We extracted a total of 60 recommendations from the eight guidelines (Appendix S1).

There was no recommendation for the prevention of frailty that could be synthesized, therefore six independent recommendations were included (Table 4), which fell into two main aspects:

healthy promotion lifestyles (2 recommendations) and interventions (4 recommendations). The guidelines developed by NICE (National Institute for Health & Clinical Excellence, 2015) provide recommendations on healthy promotion lifestyles, such as raising awareness of risk of frailty, preventing tobacco use, reducing alcohol-related risk, etc. In addition, service organizations and delivery are recommended to deliver services to promote healthy lifestyles, such as providing accessible services, providing advice on reducing the risks of frailty and providing physical activity opportunities, etc. Interventions to prevent frailty proposed in the guidelines include physical interventions, interventions based on tailored care and/or geriatric evaluation and management and interventions based on cognitive training (Marcucci et al., 2019). Physical interventions include physical activity/exercise, nutritional interventions and a combination of exercise and nutritional interventions. Interventions based on cognitive training can play a role in frailty prevention alone or in combination with exercise and nutritional supplementation. However, interventions based on hormone therapy or on problem-solving therapy are not recommended.

For the management of frailty, we synthesized 17 consensus recommendations concerning 13 clinical problems (Table 5), including frailty screening/identification, frailty assessment, comprehensive management, social support, personalized care, physical activity/exercise, nutrition intervention, cognitive intervention, hormone therapy, multidomain intervention, medication management, Reversible conditions and referral. # 4 | DISCUSSION

In the current review, we systematically retrieved guidelines for the prevention and management of frailty and assessed their methodological quality using the AGREE II instrument. Overall, we found that the methodological quality of the included CPGs was insufficient. Measured by the mean score, the lowest score was obtained in the 'applicability' domain, followed by the 'rigour of development' domain, 'editorial independence' domain, 'stakeholder involvement' domain, the 'clarity of presentation' domain and the 'scope and purpose' domain. Additionally, we synthesized 23 consensus recommendations for the prevention and management of frailty. Consequently, our results provide a more intuitive, convenient and summative reference resource for frailty prevention and management in frail older adults and health practitioners.

# 4.1 | Methodological quality of the included CPGs

Although the mean score of all six domains exceeded the standard of  $50\%$ , the four domains of 'applicability', 'editorial independence', 'rigour of development' and 'stakeholder involvement' were just slightly more than the standard, barely reaching the satisfactory level. Among the six domains of the AGREE II evaluation criteria, the lowest score fell on the 'applicability' domain, which is crucial to the overall quality because guidelines with high applicability more easily translate evidence into clinical practice. However, if guidelines are not clinically applicable, they are unlikely to be implemented; therefore, healthcare practices will not improve (Sabharwal et al., 2014). However, this factor was found to be poor among the included guidelines. This domain of AGREE II includes four items related to guideline implementation. In the current study, most guidelines failed to report facilitators or barriers to application, failed to take the potential resource implications of applying recommendations into account, and failed to

provide monitoring or auditing criteria to measure application of the recommendations. In terms of implementation advice or tools, four guidelines provided tools (British Geriatrics Society, 2014; Clinical Practice Guidelines & Protocols in British Columbia, 2017; Dent, Martin, et al., 2019; Dent, Morley, et al., 2019) or resources (National Institute for Health & Clinical Excellence, 2016) to help healthcare practice, such as a management plan or pathway for frailty (British Geriatrics Society, 2014; Clinical Practice Guidelines & Protocols in British Columbia, 2017) or a user-friendly patient information guide (Dent, Martin, et al., 2019; Dent, Morley, et al., 2019). The remaining four guidelines did not provide the corresponding content. In summary, the guidelines included in this study had a limited effect on promoting healthcare practice from the perspective of applicability. Users will encounter barriers in practice (Sabharwal et al., 2014).

The 'editorial independence' domain also received a poor score. Such a result has also been found in other studies (Deng et al., 2016; Sabharwal et al., 2013; Sanclemente et al., 2014) using AGREE II to evaluate the quality of guidelines, indicating that the importance of editorial independence is often ignored by guideline developers. There may be an interesting relationship between guideline sponsors or developers and the content of the guideline, especially in the pharmaceutical and medical device industries (Krimsky et al., 1998). Such interactions may introduce bias into the guidelines, whether conscious or not (Sabharwal et al., 2013). Therefore, independence is also very important to the quality of the guidelines. AGREE requires that any conflicts of interest be stated in the main body or appendix of the guideline to avoid bias. There are two items in this domain. In the one item, guidelines are required to report the name of the funding and to state that sponsors' views do not affect the guidelines. In the other item, it is required that publication of the guidelines be accompanied by the disclosure of interest statement. The primary reason for the poor score in this domain was that the majority of guidelines did not state the independence of the sponsor's view. As a result, the independence of guideline editing has been weakened, indicating that it is uncertain whether the formulation of recommendations has been biased with competing interests.

There are eight items in the 'rigour of development' domain, primarily involving the methods of collecting and synthesizing evidence, the process of forming recommendations and the steps to update guidelines, which is considered the strongest indicator of quality of all the domains because it evaluates the integrity of the guideline development process (Deng et al., 2016). However, our results showed that the included guidelines performed poorly in this domain, primarily due to a lack of external review procedures and guideline update information. The latest definition of guidelines by the IOM stated that for a guideline to be considered trustworthy, it should be based on a systematic review (Graham et al., 2011), indicating that systematic reviews might become the gold standard for guidelines (Sabharwal et al., 2013). However, the included guidelines did not perform well in evidence retrieval methods, evidence selection criteria or evidence strength, including incomplete description, inaccurate description and a lack of evidence-based methods, which reduced the trustworthiness of the guidelines.

Compared with the above three domains, the 'stakeholder involvement' domain scored better. All development groups of the guidelines included relevant professionals with or without detailed descriptions, and almost all guidelines reported target users. However, most of the guidelines lacked target population preferences and views, leading to a reduced score in this domain. The scores obtained in the 'scope and purpose' domain and 'clarity of presentation' domain were more satisfactory. Each guideline reported all items in the AGREE II, but individual guidelines reported incompletely or vaguely on these items.

# 4.2 Recommendations of the included CPGs

# 4.2.1 | Content of the recommendations

The synthesized recommendations primarily include frailty prevention and frailty management, where management refers to frailty screening/identification, frailty assessment, comprehensive management, social support, personalized care, physical activity/ exercise, nutrition intervention, cognitive intervention, hormone therapy, multidomain intervention, medication management, reversible conditions and referral in the review.

Our results indicate that there is a lack of clinical evidences guiding the frailty prevention processes. Only two guidelines (Marcucci et al., 2019; National Institute for Health & Clinical Excellence, 2015) for the prevention of frailty have been retrieved in the study. The prevention strategies of frailty proposed in the two guidelines mainly focus on two aspects, respectively, one is to standardize health behaviours and to raise awareness, the other is targeted interventions. Therefore, there is no consensus recommendation can be synthesized. The target users of NICE guideline are commissioners, local authorities, providers of NHS services, which is different from other guidelines (patients and healthcare practitioners). Recommendations from different perspectives can complement and promote each other, which is very necessary for the prevention of frailty, because the prevention work should not only be the duties of medical staff, but also need the attention and joint efforts of the whole society. In the future, more scientific practical evidences are needed to perfect and expand the frailty prevention system.

Recommendations on the same clinical problems for the management of frailty are generally consistent. However, the necessity of screening for frailty in older adults remains controversial. Guidelines developed by EAST (Crandall et al., 2016) and ICFSR (Dent, Martin, et al., 2019; Dent, Morley, et al., 2019) recommend screening for frailty, but the guidelines developed by BGS (British Geriatrics Society, 2014) and BC (Clinical Practice Guidelines & Protocols in British Columbia, 2017) did not recommend offering routine population frailty screening. The reason for the opposite recommendation may be that the focus of each guideline is different when forming these recommendations. The BGS guideline (British Geriatrics Society, 2014) considered that systematic screening for frailty would be expensive, and there was currently no evidence

for improved outcomes (Morley et al., 2013). It was shown that in a series of filmed case studies of 'frailty' in Age UK research, none of the participants classified themselves as 'frail'. Therefore, routine screening for frailty may also cause a degree of 'public unacceptability'; for example people may be fearful of being diagnosed with frailty and therefore be reluctant to accept frailty screening. The ICFSR guideline (Dent, Martin, et al., 2019; Dent, Morley, et al., 2019) also mentioned the lack of evidence, but they were more concerned that if not screened and intervened, the risk of developing frailty would increase, thus increasing the global burden. Therefore, use of recommendations in the guidelines should be based on the purpose and specific circumstances.

# 4.2.2 | The rigour of the recommendations

Half of the eight guidelines (Crandall et al., 2016; Dent et al., 2017; Dent, Martin, et al., 2019; Dent, Morley, et al., 2019; Marcucci et al., 2019) are based on systematic reviews with the grade of recommendation. Two guidelines (British Geriatrics Society, 2014; Clinical Practice Guidelines & Protocols in British Columbia, 2017) are neither based on a systematic review nor give the grade of recommendation. According to the standards of IOM for clinical practice guidelines, recommendations of these guidelines are not rigorous enough. The two NICE guidelines (National Institute for Health & Clinical Excellence, 2015, 2016) are based on system reviews, but do not give the grade of recommendation. In addition to the grade, the ICFSR guideline also presents the certainty of evidence of each recommendation, making its recommendations more rigorous.

The contribution of each guideline to the synthesis findings can be seen from Table 5 clearly. The ICFSR guideline provides the greatest number of consensus recommendations for the results, and it has a higher quality and the latest publication years, so it is the most recommended guideline to use among the eight included guidelines.

# 4.3 Limitations

In general, there are several limitations in the current study. First, this study only evaluated the quality of guidelines for frailty prevention and management from the perspective of methodology. In addition, the quality of reporting is also an aspect to measure the quality of the guidelines. However, because it was not within the scope of our research purpose, we did not evaluate this aspect. Second, only eight guidelines met the criteria and were included in this review. Although the prevention and management of frailty is urgent, there is currently a lack of clinical evidences guiding these processes. With the emergence of new evidence, more relevant guidelines will be developed and published, and this study will be updated in a timely manner. Third, due to the language limitation, we only searched the guidelines published in English and Chinese. Therefore, it is likely that the guidelines published in other languages have been omitted in this study.

# 4.4 | Implications for practice and research

We put forward the following suggestions on improving guideline quality and on developing guidelines in future research. First, guideline developers should pay special attention to improving the methodological quality of the 'applicability', 'editorial independence', 'rigour of development' and 'stakeholder involvement' domains. For example more attention should be paid to researching the facilitators and barriers to application and reporting in detail, clearly reporting conflicts of interest, describing the method of evidence retrieval, joining external review and collecting the choices and preferences of the target population. Second, we suggest that the applicable setting of these guidelines should be further specified in the future, such as the guidelines used in hospitals, the guidelines used in communities and the guidelines used in nursing facilities. Most existing guidelines apply to all settings where frailty can be encountered. However, due to differing infrastructure and medical conditions, different clinical manifestations of frailty and different goals for frailty management in different settings, the existing guidelines are not very instructive. Therefore, it is necessary to distinguish the settings in which the guidelines apply. Third, in the process of composing the recommendation text content, based on the full consideration of the evidence from the literature, cost-effectiveness and patient preferences, the content of the recommendations should be described in detail as much as possible, such as the time, frequency and intensity of exercise required for the specific implementation of an exercise program, to enhance its operability. Finally, frailty, as a type of geriatric syndrome, is usually accompanied by a series of chronic diseases, such as diabetes. In addition to inpatients, most older adults remain at home for long periods of time. Therefore, in addition to being managed by healthcare providers, frail older adults also need to be self-managed to a large extent. However, current guidelines do not address frailty self-management, which may be an important direction for future research.

# 5 CONCLUSIONS

The number of clinical practice guidelines for the prevention and management of frailty is limited. Methodological quality of the existing guidelines for frailty prevention and management needs to be improved, especially in domains of 'applicability', 'editorial independence', 'rigour of development' and "stakeholder involvement". Our findings provide a more intuitive, convenient and summative reference for frailty prevention and management. It is worth noting that the recommendations described in guidelines require additional detail. Users of clinical practice guidelines, especially nurses, should make careful decisions according to the actual clinical situation when using these guidelines. Nurses also have a key role in providing more clinical evidences for the improvement of the quality of the guidelines.
