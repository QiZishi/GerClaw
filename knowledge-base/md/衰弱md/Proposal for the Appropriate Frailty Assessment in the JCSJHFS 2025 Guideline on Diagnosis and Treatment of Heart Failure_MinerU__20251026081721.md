# Proposal for the Appropriate Frailty Assessment in the JCS/JHFS 2025 Guideline on Diagnosis and Treatment of Heart Failure

# To the Editor:

We read the article by the JCS/JHFS 2025 Guideline on diagnosis and treatment of heart failure<sup>1</sup> with interest. We are grateful to know that the authors have given frailty a solid significance in the management of heart failure. However, we have some serious concerns about how frailty is assessed.

The Cardiovascular Health Study (CHS) criteria are the most representative diagnostic method for physical frailty. The Japanese version of the CHS criteria is "the J-CHS criteria", but from 2020 "the revised J-CHS criteria" have been used, in which the cutoff of grip strength has been changed. In addition, the questionnaire for the medical checkup of the old-old,² which has been used in the frailty health checkup for the old-old since 2020, is reportedly being used to evaluate frailty.³

Meanwhile, the Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) clearly has a different meaning from the concept that "frailty is a state preceding disability", at least in Japan, and the concept must be distinguished from the diagnostic and assessment tools for frailty as indicated by the Japan Geriatrics Society (https://www.jpn-geriat-soc.or.jp/tool/pdf/tool_14.pdf). Therefore, the CFS should not be classified as "Multidimensional frailty assessment methods" as indicated in Table 9.

Additionally, the concept of cognitive frailty is "a combination of decline in physical function and mild cognitive decline equivalent to mild cognitive impairment (MCI)".4 Because a diagnosis is required in the pre-dementia stage, the clinical dementia rating (CDR) and the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) are used. In general, the Mini-Cognitive Assessment Instrument (Mini-Cog) is not used for cognitive frailty. Therefore, "Cognitive" and Mini-Cog should be removed from Table 9.

We do hope that the authors correctly address how to assess frailty in patients with heart failure.
