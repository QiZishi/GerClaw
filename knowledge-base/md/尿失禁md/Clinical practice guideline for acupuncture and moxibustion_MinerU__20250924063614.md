# ABSTRACT

Urinary incontinence (UI) is a common problem worldwide. It has a major impact on physical and social activities and interpersonal relationships. UI is common in women, but is under- reported and undertreated. It affects the quality of life of female patients severely. Acupuncture and moxibustion have been proposed as potentially effective interventions for female UI. Hence, for the benefit of acupuncture practitioners around the world, the World Federation of Acupuncture- moxibustion Societies initiated a project to develop a clinical practice guideline (CPG) for the use of acupuncture and moxibustion to treat female UI. This CPG was developed according to the Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) methodology, referring to the principles of the World Health Organization Handbook for Guideline Development. During the development of the CPG, the guideline development group (GDG) played an important role. The clinical questions, recommendations and therapeutic protocols were all formulated by GDG using the modified Delphi method. This CPG contains ten recommendations about the use of acupuncture and moxibustion interventions for ten clinical questions, which include nine conditional recommendations for the intervention and one conditional recommendation for either the intervention or the comparison. # 1. Introduction

The International Continence Society defines urinary incontinence (UI) as involuntary loss of urine [1]. European Association of Urology classifies UI into stress UI (SUI), mixed UI (MUI) and urge UI (UUI) [2]. SUI refers to involuntary leakage of urine due to increased intra- abdominal pressure (coughing, laughing, sneez ing, and exercising). In UUI, there is involuntary leakage of urine after an intense and sudden urge to urinate. MUI refers to concomitant SUI and UUI [3], accompanied by dysfunction of bladder sphincter.

According to the severity of subjective symptoms, SUI can be classified as follows [4]: (1) mild, leakage of urine only occurs during coughing or sneezing, and does not need to use a urine pad; (2) moderate, leakage of urine occurs during daily activities such as running, jumping, fast walking etc., and needs to use a urine pad; (3) severe, leakage of urine occurs even with slight movement and change of body position, and needs to use a urine pad.

The main symptoms of UUI manifest as urgent, and/or frequent urination, nocturia, and inability to voluntarily control urination [5]. Involuntary leakage of urine occurs immediately after the sensation of urination, which is often induced by activities and environment, such as the sound of running water, cold stimulation, etc.

An epidemiological survey in Australia [6] found that  $36.2\%$  of Australian women suffered from UI, in which MUI was the most common with a prevalence of  $14.6\%$  followed by UUI  $(11.3\%)$  and SUI  $(10.4\%)$ . In China, the female UI prevalence was  $18.9\%$  in 2015, with an increasing trend with age, in which 50 to 59 years old were the peak ages for getting SUI  $(28.2\%)$  [7]. In the United States, the prevalence of UI in elderly women significantly increased in recent years, and the annual medical cost for UI was over USD 12 billion [8,9].

Although UI is not a life- threatening disease, it has a major impact on physical and social activities and interpersonal relationships. UI is common in women, but is under- reported and undertreated. It affects the quality of life in female patients severely [9].

At present, the main treatments for female UI include both conservative treatment and surgical treatment [10]. After patients were evaluated for incontinence, treatment plans should begin with conservative treatment, including pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT), medication therapy, and changes in behavior and lifestyle [2,10]. For patients with poor response to conservative treatment or with severe UI, anti- UI surgery can be undertaken, such as urethral injection of fillers and retropubic sacral vaginal suspension [11].

PFMT could be used as an effective first- line treatment for SUI, UUI, and MUI [12- 15]. At present, rehabilitation training for pelvic floor muscles includes Kegel exercise, pelvic floor muscle electrical stimulation therapy, biofeedback therapy, etc. [14]. Among them, Kegel exercise is most commonly used in clinical practice. The main operations include continuous pelvic floor muscle contraction (anal constriction movement) for no less than  $3\mathrm{~s}$ , relaxation for  $2 - 6\mathrm{~s}$ , repeating the above movement for  $15 - 30\mathrm{~min}$ , or doing 150- 200 times of anal constriction exercises every day [16,17]. Evaluation was performed after 3- months of training in an outpatient clinic. Current evidence- based medication therapy is primarily used as an alternative treatment for UUI [2]. Medications commonly used in the treatment of UUI include anticholinergic agents (e.g., solifenacin) and  $\beta 3$  adrenergic receptor agonists (e.g., mirabelon) [18,19].

Clinical practice guidelines (CPG) are critical in guiding clinical decisions. The rigorous development process set out by Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) can effectively improve the methodological quality of the CPG [20,21]. In recent years, acupuncture and moxibustion have been proposed as potentially effective interventions for female UI [22- 25]. Hence, the World Federation of Acupuncture- Moxibustion Societies initiated a project to develop a CPG on acupuncture and moxibustion for female UI towards global target users (WFAS 007.6- 2023). This guideline provides recommendations for acupuncture and moxibustion for female UI to meet the needs of an international population of clinicians and patients based on current clinical evidence. The international applicability, and patients' values and wishes are also taken into account. This guideline intends to improve the effectiveness, safety and standardization of acupuncture and moxibustion treatment for female UI and promote international application of acupuncture and moxibustion for female UI in clinical practice.

# 2. Methods

A registry has been made on the Practice guideline REgistration for the transPAREncy (PREPAB) platform with the registration number IPGRP- 2021CN037. The development of this CPG refers to the GRADE methodology and the World Health Organization guideline development manual [26]. The four key steps of developing this CPG are shown in Fig. 1, including forming clinical questions and selecting outcomes, constructing an evidence body, developing recommendations, and formulating treatment protocols. The guideline development group (GDG) played critical roles in the whole process.

# 2.1. Framing key clinical questions and outcomes

Firstly, a patient, intervention, comparison, and outcome (PICO)- structured clinical concern survey questionnaire was used to conduct an international survey of the intended users, and 114 questionnaires were collected [27]. The survey results suggested that the most type of female UI that received the most interest was SUI. The interventions that received the most interest were filiform needle therapy, electroacupuncture therapy, and moxibustion therapy. The outcomes that received the most interest were frequency of UI, quality of life score of female UI patients, and International Consultation on Incontinence Questionnaire- Short Form (ICIQ- SF) score. A preliminary list of clinical questions and outcomes was developed based on the result of the survey. Through two rounds of modified Delphi GDG consensus, this guideline incorporated 10 clinical questions covering the elements of PICO that received the most interest [28]. Through three rounds of modified Delphi GDG consensus, 11 outcomes have been included in this guideline, and in order of importance, they are frequency of UI in  $24\mathrm{~h}$ , quality of life score, validation of the Urge Incontinence Impact Questionnaire (U- IIQ)/Urge- Urinary Distress Inventory (U- UI) score, ICIQ- SF score, patient satisfaction score, number of urine pads used, adverse event rate, clinical economic indicators, urine leakage in  $24\mathrm{~h} / 72\mathrm{~h}$ ,  $1\mathrm{~h}$  pad test leakage, and ancillary tests for the evaluation of urinary tract problems.

# 2.2. Collecting and evaluating the evidence for PICO questions

A systematic search was carried out in four major English databases (PubMed, Embase, Cochrane, and Web of Science) and four major Chinese databases (China National Knowledge Infrastructure, Wanfang Data, China Science and Technology Journal Database, and China Biomedical Literature Database). The retrieval included literature published from the establishment of the databases up to May 15, 2022, and used the keywords "female urinary incontinence," "acupuncture," and "moxibustion." A search was also conducted on the international clinical trial register platform (https://trialsearch.who.int/Default.aspx), the American clinical trial register platform (https://www.clinicaltrials.gov/), and the Chinese clinical trial registry (https://www.chicetr.org.cn). EndNote software (Version X9.3.3, Clarivate Analytics, Pennsylvania, USA) was used for literature management.

The inclusion and exclusion criteria were formulated according to the PICO element characteristics of the ten clinical questions. The data were extracted from the reports and the risk of bias was analyzed. Meta- analyses were conducted for each clinical question. Review Manager 5.4.1 (the Nordic Cochrane Centre, the Cochrane Collaboration, Copenhagen, Denmark) and Stata 12.0 (StataCorp LP, College Station, TX, USA) were used for statistical analysis. The quality of evidence (risk of bias, inconsistency, imprecision, indirection, and publication bias) was assessed using the GRADE methodology. The guideline development tool GRADEpro (McMaster University and Evidence Prime Inc., Hamilton, Canada) was used to generate the summary of findings (SoF) and the evidence to decision (EtD) framework.

# 2.3. Formulating the recommendations

The recommendations were formulated by the GDG members using the modified Delphi method [29]. First of all, after reading the evidence body of each clinical question and the consensus rules, GDG members made judgments about the twelve dimensions of the EtD framework (problem, desirable effects, undesirable effects, certainty of evidence, values, balance of effects, resources required, certainty of evidence of required resources, cost- effectiveness, equity, acceptability, and feasibility), and gave the direction and intensity of recommendations for each clinical question. The working group made statistical summaries of the first round modified Delphi surveys of the EtD framework and recommendations. Then a GDG consensus meeting was held to introduce the body of evidence, consensus rules and statistical results from the first round of modified Delphi voting, and the GDG members discussed the EtD framework for each question. After extensive discussion, GDG members voted on the EtD framework and recommendations again. According to the results of this second round of modified Delphi survey and GRADE grid consensus rules, final recommendations were made.

# 2.4. Formulating the therapeutic protocols

The recommended therapeutic protocols were generated by the modified Delphi consensus after GDG members evaluated the effective protocols from the included systematic reviews. Firstly, the working group analyzed the systematic reviews and retrieved the therapeutic protocols from the randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that had positive results. The patients' characteristics and the details of interventions were extracted and then categorized into multiple filiform needle and moxibustion therapeutic protocols. The first round of the modified Delphi survey was conducted among the acupuncture experts of the GDG, and the importance

score and coefficient of variation of different protocols were calculated. An expert consensus meeting on acupuncture and moxibustion was held by the GDG, at which each of the recommended therapeutic protocols were fully discussed, and then the second round of the modified Delphi survey was conducted. The therapeutic protocols were refined and finalized according to the modified Delphi consensus rules [30].

# 3. Clinical questions and recommendations

The terminologies in the clinical practice guideline are summarized in Table 1.

The recommendations for ten clinical questions are summarized in Table 2, including nine conditional recommendations for the intervention, and one conditional recommendation for either the intervention or the comparison.

3.1. Compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture, can female UI patients benefit from conventional filiform needle therapy?

# 3.1.1. Recommendation

For female UI, conventional filiform needle therapy may be recommended rather than no treatment or sham acupuncture (conditional recommendation).

# 3.1.2. SoF

Two RCTs addressed this clinical question and included four outcomes. Among these, frequency of UI in  $24\mathrm{h}$  [32] was supported by very low- quality evidence. The incidence of adverse events [31], urodynamic examination- maximum bladder manometric volume [32], and urodynamic examination- initial sensation of bladder filling [32] were supported by low- quality evidence.

Compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture, conventional filiform needle therapy showed moderate beneficial effects and trivial adverse effects in female UI patients. Compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture, conventional filiform needle therapy may be more cost- effective and may increase health equity. In addition, the conventional filiform needle therapy was acceptable to patients and doctors, and had good feasibility. The certainty of the evidence body was low.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that conventional filiform needle therapy should be recommended for the treatment of female UI, compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture.

# 3.1.3. Application of recommendation

Conventional filiform needle therapy applies to all types and degrees of adult female UI patients. Conventional filiform needle therapy may be recommended for: (1) female UI patients whose life quality is affected seriously by UI; (2) female UI patients who are not opposed to conventional filiform needle treatment.

# 3.2. Compared with PFMT or medication treatment, can female UI patients benefit equally or more from conventional filiform needle therapy?

# 3.2.1. Recommendation

For female patients with UI, conventional filiform needle therapy may be recommended rather than PFMT or oral medication (conditional recommendation).

# 3.2.2. SoF

Five RCTs addressed this clinical question. Among them, were three RCTs of conventional filiform needle therapy with a PFMT control. Seven outcomes were included. Among these, the incidence of adverse events [31] and urodynamic examination- detrusor pressure [34] were supported by low- quality evidence; ICIQ- SF score [33], urine leakage in  $24\mathrm{h}$  [33], urodynamic examination- maximum urine flow rate [34], urodynamic examination- mean urine flow rate [34], and urodynamic examination- maximum urethral closure pressure [34] were supported by very low- quality evidence. One RCT [35] studied the effects of conventional filiform needle therapy combined with PFMT and compared it with oral tolterodine tartrate. Three outcomes were included in this study. Among them, 1- hour pad test leakage [35] and the incidence of adverse events [35] were supported by low- quality evidence; while the ICIQ- SF score [35] was supported by very low- quality evidence. One RCT with conventional filiform needle therapy and oral tolterodine hydrochloride as control included two outcomes. Among them, the incidence of adverse events [36] was supported by low- quality evidence, and frequency of UI in  $24\mathrm{h}$  [36] was supported by very low- quality evidence.

Compared with PFMT or oral medication, the conventional filiform needle therapy showed small beneficial effects and trivial adverse effects in female UI patients. Compared with PFMT or oral medication, conventional filiform needle therapy may be cost- effective and increase health equity. In addition, the conventional filiform needle therapy was acceptable for patients and doctors, and had good feasibility. The certainty of the evidence body was very low.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that conventional filiform needle therapy should be recommended for the treatment of female UI, compared with PFMT or oral medication.

# 3.2.3.Application of recommendation

Conventional filiform needle therapy may be recommended for female UI patients who: (1) are not opposed to conventional filiform needle therapy; (2) are experiencing little symptom relief from PFMT; (3) are benefited little from oral medication; (4) who are concerned about side effects of medication.

3.3. Can the addition of conventional filiform needle therapy to PFMT or medication increase the benefit for moderate to severe female UI patients?

# 3.3.1.Recommendation

For female patients with moderate to severe UI, PFMT or oral medication combined with conventional filiform needle therapy may be recommended (conditional recommendation).

# 3.3.2.SoF

This clinical question included five RCTs, which compared conventional filiform needle therapy combined with PFMT to PFMT alone and included five outcomes. Among them, ICIQ- SF score [37- 41] and incontinence- quality of life (IQoL) score [40] were supported by low- quality evidence. Frequency of UI in  $24\mathrm{h}$  [37,38], 1- hour pad test leakage [39- 41], and urodynamic examination- pelvic floor muscle potential [37,38] were supported by very low- quality evidence.

No RCT was found to compare conventional filiform needle therapy in combination with oral medication therapy against oral medication therapy alone.

Compared with PFMT, conventional filiform needle therapy combined with PFMT showed moderate beneficial effects and trivial adverse effects in the treatment of moderate to severe female UI. Compared with PFMT or oral medication alone, combination therapy adding conventional filiform needle therapy may be cost- effective, accepted by patients and doctors, have good feasibility, and increase health equity.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that PFMT or oral medication combined with conventional filiform needle therapy should be recommended for the treatment of moderate to severe female UI.

# 3.3.3.Application of recommendation

PFMT or oral medication combined with conventional filiform needle therapy may be recommended for female patients with moderate to severe UI who: (1) do not decline conventional filiform needle therapy; (2) are experiencing little symptom relief from PFMT; (3) are experiencing little symptom relief from oral medication.

For female UI patients whose condition has been assessed by a urologist and requires surgery, acupuncture should be used with caution, in accordance with the treatment recommendations of specialists.

# 3.4. Compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture, can female UI patients benefit from the DPSLAT?

# 3.4.1.Recommendation

For female patients with UI, deep puncturing stimulation on lumbosacral acupoints therapy (DPSLAT) may be recommended rather than no treatment or sham acupuncture (conditional recommendation).

# 3.4.2.SoF

Six RCTs addressed this clinical question and included twelve outcomes. Among them, frequency of UI (3- 6 months of followup) [41,42], ICIQ- SF score (after treatment) [41- 44], urine leakage in 1- hour pad test [41- 45], and incidence of adverse events [41- 44] were supported by moderate- quality evidence; frequency of

UI after treatment [41,21,44,45], frequency of UI at 3- month follow- up [41,42,45], incontinence quality of life score after treatment [45], incontinence quality of life score at follow- up within 3 months [45], ICIQ- SF score at follow- up within 3 months [41,42,46], ICIQ- SF score at 3- 6 months of follow- up [41,42,46], urine leakage in 1- hour pad test (follow- up within 3 months) [45], and quantity of pads consumed in  $72\mathrm{h}$  [44] were supported by low- quality evidence.

Compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture, DPSLAT showed moderate beneficial effects and trivial adverse effects in female UI treatment. Compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture, DPSLAT may be cost- effective and increase health equity in female UI patients. In addition, DPSLAT is acceptable to patients and doctors, and has good feasibility. The certainty of the evidence body is moderate.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that DPSLAT should be recommended for the treatment of female UI, compared with no treatment or sham acupuncture.

# 3.4.3. Application of recommendation

DPSLAT may be used to different types of adult female UI patients with different severities of condition and may be recommended to female UI patients: (1) who do not decline the treatment of DPSLAT; (2) whose daily life is impacted by symptoms of UI.

# 3.5. Compared with PFMT or medication treatment, can female UI patients benefit equally or more from the DPSLAT?

# 3.5.1. Recommendation

For female UI patients, DPSLAT may be recommended rather than PFMT or oral medication (conditional recommendation).

# 3.5.2. SoF

Nine RCTs addressed this clinical question. There are three RCTs of DPSLAT with a PFMT control, including eight outcomes. Urine leakage amount in 1- hour pad test (after treatment, and follow- up within 3 months and 3- 6 months) [47- 49] were supported by low- quality evidence; frequency of UI (after treatment) [47- 49], ICIQ- SF score (after treatment, within 3 months and 3- 6 months of follow- up) [48,49], and incidence of adverse events [47] were supported by very low- quality evidence. Six RCTs of DPSLAT compared to PFMT in combination with oral administration of solifenacin included thirteen outcomes. Frequency of UI (after treatment, and within 3 months and 3- 6 months of follow- up) [50- 55], ICIQ- SF score (after treatment, and follow- up within 3 months and 3- 6 months) [50- 55], urine leakage amount in 1- hour pad test (after treatment) [51- 55], number of patients in satisfaction (after treatment) [55], and incidence of adverse events were supported by low- quality evidence [50- 53,55]; incontinence impact questionnaire- 7 (IQoL7) [54], patient satisfaction score (after treatment and 3- 6 months of follow- up) [52], and number of satisfied patients (follow- up of 3- 6 months) [55] were supported by very low- quality evidence.

We found no RCTs that compared DPSLAT against oral medication.

Compared with PFMT or PFMT in combination with oral medication, DPSLAT showed moderate beneficial effects and trivial adverse effects in female UI patients. Compared with PFMT or oral conventional medication, DPSLAT for female UI may be cost- effective and may increase health equity. In addition, DPSLAT is acceptable to patients and doctors, and has good feasibility. The certainty of the evidence body is graded as low.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that DPSLAT should be recommended for the treatment of female UI, compared with PFMT or oral medication.

# 3.5.3. Application of recommendation

DPSLAT may be recommended to female UI patients who: (1) do not decline the treatment; (2) are experiencing little symptom relief from PFMT; (3) are experiencing little symptom relief from oral medication; (4) are concerned about drug side effects.

3.6. Can the addition of the DPSLAT to PFMT or medication increase the benefit for moderate to severe female UI patients?

# 3.6.1. Recommendation

For moderate to severe female UI, PFMT or oral medication combined with DPSLAT may be recommended (conditional recommendation).

# 3.6.2. SoF

Three RCTs addressed this clinical question. All of them studied DPSLAT combined with PMFT vs only PFMT control and included four outcomes. Among them, IIQ- 7 [56], ICIQ- SF score [56- 58], urine leakage amount in 1- hour pad test [57,58], and incidence of adverse events [56] were supported by very low- quality evidence. There was no RCT that studied DPSLAT combined with oral administration of medication vs simple oral administration of medication control.

Compared with simple PFMT, DPSLAT combined with PFMT showed moderate beneficial effects and trivial adverse effects on moderate to severe female UI. Compared with PFMT or oral medication alone, combination with DPSLAT may be cost- effective, acceptable to patients and doctors, have good feasibility, and increase health equity. The certainty of the evidence body is graded very low.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that PFMT or oral medication combined with DPSLAT should be recommended for the treatment of moderate to severe female UI.

# 3.6.3. Application of recommendation

PFMT or oral medication combined with DPSLAT may be recommended to moderate to severe female UI patients who: (1) do not decline to DPSLAT; (2) are experiencing little symptom relief from PFMT; (3) are experiencing little symptom relief from oral medication.

For female UI patients whose condition is assessed by urologists and requires surgery, acupuncture should be used in accordance with the treatment recommendations of specialists.

# 3.7. Compared with no treatment, can female UI patients benefit from moxibustion therapy?

# 3.7.1. Recommendation

For female patients with UI, moxibustion therapy may be recommended rather than no treatment (conditional recommendation).

# 3.7.2. SoF

Four series of case studies [59- 62] of moxibustion for female UI and nine RCTs [63- 71] of moxibustion for post- stroke UI were included as supplementary evidence for this clinical question, and include a total of seven outcomes. Among them, the frequency of UI in  $24~\mathrm{h}$  [59,63], IQoL score [64,65], ICIQ- SF score [59], 1- hour pad test leakage amount [59], and incidence of adverse events [64,65] were supported by very low- quality evidence.

Compared with no treatment, moxibustion therapy for female UI showed a moderate beneficial effect and a trivial adverse effect. Compared with no treatment, moxibustion therapy for female UI may be cost- effective, and increase health equity. In addition, moxibustion therapy is acceptable for patients and doctors, and has good feasibility. The certainty of the evidence is graded very low.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that moxibustion therapy can be recommended in the treatment of female UI, compared with the blank control.

# 3.7.3. Application of recommendation

Moxibustion therapy is suitable for different types of adult female UI patients with different severities of condition. Moxibustion therapy may be recommended to female UI patients: (1) who do not decline moxibustion treatment; (2) whose quality of life is impacted by symptoms of UI; (3) who are elderly or experiencing postpartum UI; (4) who are diagnosed with "deficiency syndrome" by traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) syndrome differentiation.

3.8. Compared with PFMT or medication treatment, can female UI patients benefit equally or more from moxibustion therapy?

# 3.8.1. Recommendation

For female UI patients, moxibustion therapy, PFMT or oral medication may be recommended (conditional recommendation for either intervention or comparison).

# 3.8.2. SoF

There is one RCT [72] of moxibustion treatment vs PFMT control, involving three outcomes. All three, ICIQ- SF score after treatment, ICIQ- SF score at 3- 6 months of follow- up, and incidence of adverse events (important outcomes), were supported by very low- quality evidence.

No RCT of moxibustion treatment vs an oral conventional medication control was found.

Compared with PFMT, moxibustion therapy for female UI showed a trivial beneficial effect and a trivial adverse effect. Compared with PFMT or oral conventional medication, moxibustion for female UI may have equal cost- effectiveness and may increase health equity. In addition, moxibustion treatment is more easily accepted by patients and physicians and has good feasibility. The certainty of the evidence is graded as very low.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that moxibustion therapy, PFMT or oral medication can be recommended in the treatment of female UI patients.

# 3.8.3. Application of recommendation

Moxibustion therapy may be recommended to female UI patients: (1) who wish to receive moxibustion treatment; (2) who have postpartum UI or are elderly; (3) who have been diagnosed with "deficiency syndrome" by TCM syndrome differentiation.

PMFT may be recommended to female UI patients after an assessment of their condition by a urologist.

Oral medication may be recommended to female UI patients after assessment of their condition by a urologist.

3.9. Can the addition of moxibustion therapy to PFMT or medication increase the benefit for moderate to severe female UI patients?

# 3.9.1. Recommendation

For moderate to severe female UI, PFMT or oral medication combined with moxibustion therapy may be recommended (Conditional recommendation).

# 3.9.2. SoF

Eighteen RCTs [73- 90] addressed this clinical question. There were 17 RCTs of moxibustion therapy combined with PMFT compared to a control of PMFT alone. The RCTs involved ten outcomes. Among them, the frequency of UI (after treatment, and follow- up within 3 months) [86,88- 90], quality of life score (IQoL or GQOLI- 74) [74,89], ICIQ- SF score (after treatment, and follow- up within 3 months) [75,77- 85,88,89], urine leakage in  $24\mathrm{h}$  (after treatment, and follow- up within 3 months) (important outcomes) [84], 1- hour pad test leakage (after treatment, and follow- up within 3 months) (important outcomes) [74,75,77,80- 85,87- 89], urodynamic examination- average flow rate (important outcomes) [81], and incidence of adverse events (important outcomes) [84,86,88,90] were supported by very low- quality evidence.

There was one RCT [91] of moxibustion therapy combined with oral medication compared to an oral medication control. All involved three outcomes, including the frequency of UI (after treatment), urodynamic examination- maximum flow rate, and average flow rate, which were supported by very low- quality evidence.

Compared with PFMT or oral medication alone, the combined application of moxibustion for moderate to severe female UI showed a moderate beneficial effect and a trivial adverse effect. Compared with PFMT or oral medication treatment alone, the combined application of moxibustion for the treatment of moderate to severe female UI may be more cost- effective, more acceptable to patients and physicians, and feasible, and may increase health equity. The certainty of the evidence is graded as very low.

Based on these justifications, the GDG agreed that the combination of moxibustion with PFMT or oral medication should be recommended in the treatment of moderate to severe female UI, compared to PFMT or oral conventional medication alone.

# 3.9.3. Application of recommendation

PFMT or oral medication combined with moxibustion therapy may be recommended to female patients with moderate to severe who: (1) do not decline moxibustion therapy; (2) are experiencing little symptom relief from PFMT; (3) are experiencing little symptom relief from oral medication.

For female UI patients whose condition is assessed by urologists and requires surgery, moxibustion should be used in accordance with the treatment recommendations of specialists.

3.10. Compared with filiform needle therapy or moxibustion therapy alone, does the combination of filiform needle and moxibustion therapy increase the benefit for female UI patients?

# 3.10.1. Recommendation

For female UI patients, the combination of filiform needle and moxibustion therapies may be recommended (conditional recommendation).

# 3.10.2. SoF

Six RCTs addressed this clinical question and included seven outcomes. Among them, the frequency of UI [92,93] and urodynamic examination- residual urine ultrasound (important outcomes) [94] were supported by low- quality evidence, while ICIQ- SF [93,95,96] and 1- hour pad test leakage (important outcomes) [93,95,96] were supported by very low- quality evidence.

Compared with filiform needle or moxibustion therapy alone, the combination of the two therapies showed a moderate beneficial effect and a trivial adverse effect. Acupuncture combined with moxibustion for female UI may have better cost- effectiveness and increase health equity. In addition, acupuncture combined with moxibustion is easily accepted by patients and physicians and has good feasibility. The certainty of the evidence is graded as very low.

# 3.10.3. Application of recommendation

For female UI patients with poor effects from filiform needle therapy or moxibustion alone, the filiform needle combined with moxibustion therapy may be recommended.

# 4. Therapeutic protocols

Therapeutic protocols were extracted from the original studies, and GDG acupuncture experts reached a consensus on including seven key acupuncture and moxibustion protocols.

# 4.1. Conventional filiform needle therapy

# 4.1.1. Acupoint selection

Abdomen: Zhongji (CV4), Guanyuan (CV3), Qihai (CV6) and Dahe (KL12).

Lumbosacral region: Shenshu (BL23), Pangguangshu (BL28) and Mingmen (GV4).

Lower limbs: Sanyinjiao (SP6), Taixi (KI3), Weizhong (BL40) and Fuliu (KI7).

According to the patient's condition, the above acupoints are selected as appropriate for the patient. It is suggested that the abdominal acupoints should be combined with the lower limb acupoints, the lumbosacral acupoints should be combined with the lower limb acupoints, or the abdominal and lumbosacral acupoints should be combined with the lower limb acupoints.

# 4.1.2. Acupuncture method

Needling method: Acupuncture with filiform needle at abdomen points (Zhongji, Guanyuan, Qihai and Dahe) obliquely,  $30-$ $40~\mathrm{mm}$  in depth toward the pubic symphysis; acupuncture at lumbosacral region perpendicular to the skin,  $20–30\mathrm{mm}$  in depth; at the remaining points needle insertion should reach conventional acupuncture depths. When needling the abdomen, ideally the needling sensation should travel to the perineum and urethra. The other points may induce needling sensations such as soreness, numbness, distention, and heaviness.

Reinforcing and reducing manipulations are chosen based on the principle of "reducing method treats excess syndrome, reinforcing method treats deficiency syndrome."

# 4.1.3. Retention time of the needles

The retention time of  $20–30\mathrm{min}$  is determined according to the patient's condition and acceptability, as appropriate.

# 4.1.4. Frequency of treatment

The frequency of treatment is selected depending on the patient's condition, preference, and acceptability. A minimum of one session per week is acceptable, and a frequency of 2- 3 times per week is recommended.

# 4.1.5. Course of treatment

The course of treatment is selected depending on patient's condition, preference, and acceptability, and the feasibility of scheduling with the acupuncture provider, at least 4 weeks for one treatment course, and 2- 3 courses are recommended.

# 4.1.6. Other considerations

According to the patients' TCM syndrome differentiation and classification, appropriate points should be added.

# 4.2. DPSLAT protocol I

4.2.1. Acupoint selection Zhongliao (BL33) and Huiyang (BL35).

# 4.2.2. Acupuncture method

Body position: The patient is in a prone position, and the lumbosacral region should be exposed adequately.

Filiform needles:  $0.3\mathrm{mm}\times 75\mathrm{mm}$  disposable acupuncture needles.

Needling method: Zhongliao, needling at an medial and inferior angle of  $30^{\circ} - 45^{\circ}$  with  $50\mathrm{- }60\mathrm{mm}$  depth; Huiyang, needling lateral and superior obliquely, with  $50\mathrm{- }60\mathrm{mm}$  depth. Needling manipulation of lifting, inserting and turning is needed to obtain a Deqi sensation such as soreness, numbness, distention and heaviness. Electroacupuncture electrodes are transversely linked to the needles at Zhongliao and Huiyang on both sides, with continuous waves, frequency of  $50\mathrm{Hz}$  and current of  $1 - 5\mathrm{mA}$

Needle retention time:  $30\mathrm{min}$

# 4.2.3. Frequency of treatment

Three times per week.

# 4.2.4. Course of treatment

For mild SUI and MUI: 6- 8 weeks; for moderate to severe SUI and MUI: 8- 12 weeks.

# 4.2.5. Other considerations for patients

This protocol is applicable to adult female patients with MUI and SUI. This recommended protocol may also be considered for the treatment of adult female patients with UUI.

This protocol may cause strong needle sensation, and should be fully explained to patients prior to beginning a session. Patients who are unable to tolerate strong needle sensation should be cautious.

# 4.2.6. Other considerations for operation

Acupuncture practitioners should receive relevant training in this treatment and be familiar with the relevant anatomical structure before clinical application of this guideline due to the operational difficulty in this protocol.

Needle standard: Disposable acupuncture needles with specifications of  $0.25\mathrm{- }0.3\mathrm{mm}$  in diameter and  $60 - 75\mathrm{mm}$  in length, according to the female UI patients' the body shape and tolerance, etc.

Precautions of acupuncture angles: The acupuncture angle can be adjusted according to specific anatomical conditions of female UI patients in different population and races in order to make sure that the needle is inserted into the posterior sacral foramen.

Application of electroacupuncture: Choose electroacupuncture and its parameters according to conditions and acceptance of patients.

Precautions of treatment frequency: There are differences in the frequency of acupuncture treatment internationally. Treatment frequency is recommended as at least once a week, and a frequency of 2- 3 times a week are recommended, according to patient's condition and acceptability as appropriate.

Precautions of course of treatment: There are differences in the course of acupuncture treatment internationally. Treatment plan needs a minimum of 6 weeks, and 6- 12 weeks are recommended, depending on the patient's condition, and acceptability as appropriate.

# 4.3. DPSLAT protocol II

# 4.3.1. Acupoint selection

Zhibian (BL54).

# 4.3.2. Acupuncture method

Body position: The patient is asked lie in a prone position, and the lumbosacral region is exposed.

Needles:  $0.25\mathrm{mm}\times 125\mathrm{mm}$  disposable acupuncture needles.

Needling method: The needle inserts into into Zhibian and penetrates  $110\mathrm{mm}$  anteriorly toward Shuibao (ST28) point. In the pro

cess of inserting, the needle body should keep an angle of  $20^{\circ}$  to the sagittal plane of the patient's trunk and at the same time parallel to the horizontal plane of the patient's trunk. Needling sensation of expansion, warmth, and relaxation should reach the lower abdomen, anterior genital and perineum.

Needle retention time:  $30\mathrm{min}$

# 4.3.3. Frequency of treatment

The frequency of treatment is selected depending on the patient's condition, preference and acceptability, and feasibility of scheduling with the acupuncture practitioner. Patients should receive treatment at least once a week, and the frequency of 2- 3 times per week is recommended.

# 4.3.4. Course of treatment

The course of treatment is selected depending on patient's condition, preference and acceptability, and feasibility of scheduling with the acupuncture practitioner; a treatment course of at least 2 weeks is recommended, and a course of 2- 4 weeks is preferred.

# 4.3.5. Other considerations for patients

This protocol is applicable to adult female patients with SUI. This protocol may also be considered for adult female patients with MUI or UUI.

This protocol may cause strong needle sensation, and should be used with caution for patients who are unable to tolerate strong needle sensation. Acupuncture practitioners should fully explain the procedure to patients before clinical practice.

# 4.3.6. Other considerations for operation

Acupuncture practitioners should receive relevant training for this treatment and be familiar with the relevant anatomical structures before clinical application of this guideline due to the operational difficulty in this protocol.

Precautions of acupuncture depth: The acupuncture depth can be adjusted according to structure and body type of female UI patients. Needling sensation should reach the lower abdomen, anterior genital, and perineum.

# 4.4. Moxibustion therapy

# 4.4.1. Moxibustion protocol I: Suspended moxibustion

Acupoint selection: Guanyuan (CV4), Qihai (CV6), Shenshu (BL23), and Ciliao (BL32) are selected acupoints according to the patient's condition.

Moxibustion method: Suspension moxibustion method is used to create a sensation of warmth and heat at the treatment point, but without burning or pain. The duration of moxibustion is determined by patient's condition and tolerance to heat. In clinical treatment, moxibustion can be combined with moxa burners when it is necessary. Conducting moxibustion on points in the abdomen and lumbosacral area simultaneously may shorten the treatment duration and save human resources.

# 4.4.2. Moxibustion protocol II: Warming needle moxibustion

Acupoint selection: Acupoints should be selected from among Zhongji (CV3), Qihai (CV6), Guanyuan (CV4), Shenshu (BL23), Ciliao (BL32) and Zusanli (ST36) according to the patient's condition.

Moxibustion method: The duration of warming needle moxibustion is determined according to the patient's condition and tolerance as appropriate.

# 4.4.3. Moxibustion protocol III: Indirect moxibustion

Acupoint selection: Acupoints are selected from among Zhongji (CV3), Guanyuan (CV4), Qihai (CV6), Shenque (CV8), Shenshu

(BL23), Pangguangshu (BL28) and Guanyuanshu (BL26), according to the patient's condition.

Intermediate drugs: Ginger slices,  $2 - 3\mathrm{cm}$  in diameter and  $0.2-$ $0.3\mathrm{cm}$  thick, with several holes poiked in the middle; or herbal cake, prepared from one or more kinds of Chinese herbs with the property of warming yang (e.g., Aconiti Lateralis Radix Praeparata [Fuzi], Cinnamomi Cortex [Rougui], Cinnamomi Ramulus [Guizhi]) that have been ground to fine powder and mixed with ginger juice.

Moxibustion method: The duration of indirect moxibustion is determined according to the patient's condition and tolerance as appropriate.

# 4.4.4. Moxibustion protocol IV: Heat-sensitive moxibustion

Acupoint selection: Look for heat- sensitive points around the Qihai (CV6), Zhongji (CV3), Guanyuan (CV4), Shenshu (BL23), Pangguangshu (BL28), Ciliao (BL32), Sanyhijiao (SP6) and Baihui (GV20).

Moxibustion method: The duration of heat- sensitive moxibustion is determined according to the patient's condition and tolerance as appropriate.

# 4.4.5. Frequency of treatment

The frequency of treatment is selected depending on the patient's condition, preference, and acceptability, and feasibility of scheduling with the acupuncture practitioner. The patients should receive treatment at least once a week, and the frequency of 2- 3 times per week is recommended.

# 4.4.6. Course of treatment

The course of treatment is selected depending on a patient's condition, preference, and acceptability, and feasibility of scheduling with the acupuncture practitioner. A course of treatment should last at least 4 weeks, and a 4- 8 week- long course is recommended.

# 4.4.7. Other considerations of moxibustion treatment

Moxibustion therapy is prohibited for those who are allergic to moxibustion materials. Moxibustion therapy should not be used for those who are unable to tolerate the smoke produced during the application of moxibustion.

Warming needle moxibustion, indirect moxibustion, and heatsensitive moxibustion have certain operational requirements, and the practitioners should receive relevant training.

# 5. Notes for the implementation of this CPG

# 5.1. Notes on laws and regulations

Users of this CPG must practice acupuncture and moxibustion therapy for female UI in compliance with the local laws and regulations.

# 5.2. Notes for medical institutions

(1) Medical institutions should be qualified to practice acupuncture and moxibustion therapy. 
(2) Medical institutions should have rescue facilities and drugs that can address any adverse events related to acupuncture and moxibustion. 
(3) It is suggested that medical institutions should set up special moxibustion rooms, equipped with exhaust fans and fire prevention facilities.

# 5.3. Notes for practitioners

(1) Those practicing acupuncture and moxibustion should be trained and appropriately licensed. 
(2) Before acupuncture treatment, practitioners should fully explain the possible benefits and

potential risks of acupuncture and moxibustion treatment protocols to patients and carry out treatment only after obtaining the consent of patients. (3) Practitioners should strictly follow the guidelines of the therapeutic protocol to prevent the occurrence of adverse events (such as needle fainting, traumatic pneumothorax, stabbing organs, and burning) and must deal with acupuncture- related adverse events.

# 5.4. What needs to be clear to the patients

(1) Acupuncture is not suitable for patients who are fasting or have overeating, extreme fatigue or fear of acupuncture therapy. For weak patients, acupuncture manipulation should not be too strong. 
(2) For pregnant women, it is not appropriate to stimulate acupoints in the abdominal and lumbosacral regions, or to treat acupoints with strong functions of dredging meridians and activating blood circulation (such as Hegu and Sanyinjiao). 
(3) For patients with bleeding tendency, acupuncture is not recommended. 
(4) For patients with skin infection, ulcer or tumor at acupoints, acupuncture is not recommended. 
(5) Moxibustion should be carried out in a ventilated environment and precautions should be taken to prevent burning moxa floss from falling off and burning skin and clothing.

# 6. Conclusion and discussion

This CPG provides recommendations according to the GRADE methodology. The recommendations address the clinical questions that attracted most interest from acupuncture and moxibustion practitioners. All the recommendations and remarks were provided by the GDC via the modified Delphi consensus method and were based on the summary of current clinical evidence. From our knowledge, this is the first acupuncture and moxibustion CPG on female UI for international practitioners. There are several limitations. The clinical questions and recommendations of this guideline mainly focus on the interventions (filiform needle acupuncture and moxibustion) that received the most interest in the survey of target users. Other types of acupuncture therapies for female UI (e.g., acupoint application therapy, auricular point stimulation) were not addressed in this CPG.
