# ABSTRACT

Objective: The aim of this study was to develop consensus definitions and diagnostic criteria for sarcopenic obesity tailored to Asia- Oceania populations, a condition characterized by reduced skeletal muscle mass and function with excess adipose tissue.

Methods: Experts from various fields formed a working group under the Asia- Oceania Association for the Study of Obesity (AOASO) and the International Association of Gerontology and Geriatrics Asia/Oceania Region (IAGG- AOR). The group convened four meetings, employing the Delphi technique to achieve consensus on diagnostic criteria and management strategies for sarcopenic obesity.

Results: The consensus defines sarcopenic obesity with a 3- step algorithm, including screening, diagnosis, and intervention. The screening criteria included BMI and waist circumference for obesity and calf circumference, the SARC- F, and the Finger Ring test for sarcopenia. The diagnostic criteria for sarcopenia include assessments of muscle mass (using dual- energy X- ray absorptiometry or bioelectrical impedance analysis), muscle strength (handgrip strength), and physical performance (gait speed, Short Physical Performance Battery, or the 5- Times Sit- to- Stand Test). Central obesity is the main concern for the diagnosis of obesity. The working group recommends a combination of dietary consultations, increased physical activity, resistance training, and the potential use of oral nutritional supplements and medications for managing sarcopenic obesity. The primary objective is to increase skeletal muscle mass and reduce adipose tissue mass.

# Introduction

Sarcopenic obesity is a clinical and functional condition characterized by the simultaneous presence of decreased skeletal muscle mass and function, alongside an excessive accumulation of adipose tissue [1,2]. This dual condition poses significant health risks, particularly in older adults, as it is linked to increased frailty, cardiovascular diseases, fractures, and higher mortality rates [1- 3]. The global prevalence of sarcopenic obesity among adults is rising rapidly, driven by demographic shifts and lifestyle changes [4].Sarcopenic obesity exacerbates with aging population both globally and in the Asia- Oceania region [4,5]. However, the lack of a universally accepted definition for sarcopenic obesity complicates comparability across studies and hinders effective clinical management [5]. Although sarcopenia and obesity are two distinct conditions, they share overlapping pathophysiological characteristics and risk factors, including lifestyle influences, aging, the production of inflammatory cytokines and reactive oxygen species, and endocrine changes [5- 7].

The European Association for the Study of Obesity (EAsO) and the European Society for Clinical Nutrition and Metabolism (ESPEN) recently proposed criteria for sarcopenic obesity [8], but these may not suit Asia- Oceania populations due to differences in body composition, genetics, and muscle mass. The region's fast aging and uneven healthcare access also require more adaptable criteria. In the Asia- Oceania region, the epidemiology of sarcopenic obesity reflects the broader trends observed, with increasing prevalence due to rapid demographic shifts and lifestyle changes [4,9- 12]. In the Asia- Oceania region, the prevalence of sarcopenic obesity reflects broader global trends, with estimates ranging from  $8\%$  to  $12\%$  based on various studies conducted primarily in Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and China [4,13]. This region is experiencing one of the fastest rates of population aging, which significantly contributes to the rising incidence of sarcopenic obesity. The clinical significance of sarcopenic obesity in Asia- Oceania is important, as it poses unique challenges compared to other parts of the world. Factors such as ethnic diversity, varying body compositions, and genetic predispositions influence the manifestation and outcomes of the condition. Moreover, the Asia- Oceania region encompasses a large and diverse population with varying levels of access to medical resources. This diversity requires more appropriate approaches to effectively address sarcopenic obesity.

The significance of addressing sarcopenic obesity lies in its multifaceted impact on health, mobility, and quality of life. This finding underscores the importance of integrated approaches considering both muscle and fat in managing older adults and those at risk of developing obesity and experiencing muscle loss. Understanding sarcopenic obesity is crucial for developing targeted interventions to improve muscle strength, reduce fat mass, enhance physical function, reduce disease risk, and improve outcomes for affected individuals [7].

# The process of developing a consensus

# Working group formation

Nineteen experts, including a broad range of specialists in obesity, geriatrics, endocrinology, and dietetics from Asia- Oceania countries, convened a working group organized by the Asia- Oceania Association for the Study of Obesity (AOASO) and the International Association of Gerontology and Geriatrics Asia/Oceania Region (IAGG- AOR) to address sarcopenic obesity in October 2023. The working group retains complete autonomy and is purposefully diverse, incorporating members with varying viewpoints and geographical backgrounds to guarantee a multifaceted perspective on sarcopenic obesity.

# Delphi process results

The group convened four face- to- face meetings between October 2023 and March 2024 to discuss and document their recommendations. The expert panel included 15 professionals with diverse expertise in geriatrics, endocrinology, obesity medicine, nutrition, family medicine, and public health. Panelists were selected to ensure broad geographic representation from across the Asia- Oceania region, including experts from Australia, Hong Kong, India, Indonesia, Japan, Korea, Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore and Taiwan. The panel consisted of senior clinicians, academic researchers, and leaders in relevant medical societies, ensuring multidisciplinary input and contextual relevance to the region's populations and healthcare systems.

Existing definitions of obesity and sarcopenia were revisited in light of differences in population composition, culture, and economic development. An expert consensus was reached regarding the criteria for screening, diagnosis, and intervention in clinical patients with sarcopenic obesity.

The process of addressing sarcopenic obesity involves a multistage, collaborative approach, and it was initiated with an online survey distributed to panel experts from diverse fields and regions. The aim of this survey was to pinpoint critical questions and areas of contention in diagnosing and managing sarcopenic obesity. Following this, a meeting was held to discuss survey outcomes, leading to a consensus on prioritizing the development of definitive diagnostic criteria and evidencebased management approaches. Utilizing the Delphi technique over several webinars, the panel meticulously reviewed existing evidence, engaging in rounds of anonymous ratings on proposed criteria and strategies.

Throughout all phases, participants individually ranked each statement on a 5- point Likert scale (ranging from "strongly disagree" to "strongly agree") and provided justifications for their choices. Statements reaching predetermined consensus thresholds- defined as  $>70\%$  of respondents selecting either "agree" or "strongly agree"- were advanced to the next consensus stage.

The Delphi process was conducted in three iterative rounds between January and March 2024. In the first round, a total of 27 items were distributed to 11 experts, primarily to gather baseline information on the definitions, policies, and diagnostic practices related to sarcopenic obesity in each participating country. Among these, 6 items (questions 18- 23) were quantitatively scored using a 5- point Likert scale. Of these, 4 statements  $(66.6\%)$  reached the predefined consensus threshold of  $\geq 70\%$  agreement and were retained, while the remaining items were revised for the second round.

In Round 2, 10 experts assessed 11 revised or newly proposed statements. 11 items  $(100\%)$  reached consensus and were accepted, while 2 items were further refined and moved to the third round. In Round 3, 11 experts evaluated 11 remaining statements, and all 11 items  $(100\%)$  reached consensus, with agreement scores ranging from  $90.9\%$  to  $100\%$  . A complete summary of all evaluated statements, along with agreement percentages and consensus outcomes, is provided in Supplementary Table 1.

After three Delphi rounds, a consensus emerged on specific diagnostic criteria incorporating measurements of muscle mass, strength, and body fat. Management strategies for which there was greater than  $90\%$  agreement included an emphasis on nutritional interventions, physical activity, and patient education.

While patient perspectives were not directly gathered, the panel considered feedback from regional geriatric and obesity patient advocacy groups via prior AOASO/HAGG- AOR consultations to ensure clinical relevance.

# Issues addressed by the working group

One major obstacle to determining accurate prevalence rates of sarcopenic obesity is the absence of uniform definitions for both sarcopenia and obesity. Current standards for diagnosing sarcopenic obesity, including measures of muscle mass, strength, and fat distribution, are not well unified.

The working group acknowledged that sarcopenic obesity is a complex syndrome that is currently lacking a widely accepted and validated diagnostic algorithm available for use in Asia- Oceania region. This lack of consensus on diagnostic criteria has resulted in variations in prevalence across different reports (see later discussion). The working group determined that important features and measurements were crucial for identifying patients with sarcopenic obesity. Therefore, these clinical screening and diagnostic criteria should encompass all aspects and ensure ease of use and cost- effectiveness. Additionally, this clinical criterion should be applicable for gathering extensive clinical datasets and screening for sarcopenic obesity in out- of- hospital settings, such as community- based environments.

# Identified challenges and opportunities

# Age-related inconsistencies in sarcopenia

Sarcopenia is an age- related loss of muscle mass, in addition to low muscle strength. Sarcopenia leads to an increased risk of physical disability, poor quality of life, and mortality [14- 16]. Sarcopenia predominantly impacts older individuals, although a gradual decline in muscle mass begins at 40 years of age [17,18]. Beyond the age of 50 years, muscle mass decreases annually by  $1 - 2\%$  [19,20]. The prevalence of sarcopenia significantly varies due to differences in definitions and cutoff values. For individuals over 65 years of age, rates range from  $3\%$  to  $24\%$  and increase with advancing age [21]. One study revealed an incidence rate of  $1.6\%$  for sarcopenia among European men and women aged 40- 79 years, based on the definition provided by the European Working Group on Sarcopenia in Older People (EWGSOP) [22]. Utilizing the definition from the Asian Working Group for Sarcopenia (AWGS), another study reported a  $3.4\%$  incidence of sarcopenia in a cohort of Chinese men and women, with a mean age of 72 years [23]. Aging disrupts skeletal muscle homeostasis through complex but not fully understood mechanisms and pathways [14]. Due to varying definitions, inconsistencies in sarcopenia studies have created a significant gap in understanding and comparability. The Global Leadership Initiative in Sarcopenia (GLIS) is actively working to establish a universal definition of sarcopenia [24]. This initiative aims to harmonize various existing definitions, facilitating consistent diagnosis and management across diverse populations.

# Escalating obesity in aging population in Asia-Oceania region at present

Obesity is characterized by an excessive accumulation of body fat, leading to adverse effects on health [25]. The cause of obesity is a calorie intake that exceeds caloric expenditure, which is often related to a combination of unhealthy sedentary lifestyle factors, genetic predispositions, socioeconomic factors, and certain medical conditions or medications [26]. With respect to chronic conditions, the prevalence of overweight and obesity has increased to pandemic levels in recent decades and has more than doubled globally since 1980 [26]. Currently, approximately  $30\%$  of the world's population has either overweight or obesity. This increase has affected all age groups and both sexes, with a notably sharper increase among older adults and women [27]. The

Asia- Oceania region is undergoing one of the fastest rates of population aging globally. This demographic shift results in a higher prevalence of age- related conditions, including obesity and sarcopenic obesity.

# Assessing the prevalence of sarcopenic obesity without standardized diagnostic criteria

The convergence of these two conditions—sarcopenic obesity—presents unique challenges for diagnosis, treatment, and prevention, making it a critical area of focus for healthcare providers and researchers [28]. Assessing the prevalence of sarcopenic obesity in the Asia- Oceania region is challenging due to the absence of standardized diagnostic criteria, which leads to variability in reporting and understanding of this condition. In a meta- analysis of 50 studies involving 86,285 individuals aged 60 years and older, sarcopenic obesity was shown to have a prevalence of  $11\%$  among the Asian- Oceanic population [4]. No significant differences were observed in the prevalence of sarcopenic obesity among studies, whether using body fat percentage  $(10\%)$ , body mass index (BMI)  $(13\%)$ , or waist circumference  $(16\%)$  to diagnose obesity or between female  $(14\%)$  and male  $(10\%)$  patients [4]. In the Korean Sarcopenic Obesity Study, a prospective, ongoing epidemiological cohort from South Korea consisting of healthy volunteers aged 20- 80 years, the prevalence of sarcopenic obesity varied, ranging from  $1.3\%$  to  $15.4\%$  among men and  $0.8 - 22.3\%$  among women [9]. Data from the Dutch Lifelines cohort study, which included individuals aged 18- 90 years, revealed a global prevalence of sarcopenic obesity of  $1.4\%$  in women and  $0.9\%$  in men. Notably, the prevalence begins to increase at age 50, peaking at  $16.7\%$  among those in the 80- to 89- year- old age group [29]. Using data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Surveys (NHANES) conducted between 1988 and 1994, researchers examined 1526 women and 1,391 men, all aged 70 years and older. Individuals in the highest fat mass quartile and lowest muscle mass quartile were classified as having sarcopenic obesity. The findings revealed a sarcopenic obesity prevalence of  $7.4\%$  among women and  $9.6\%$  among men [30]. In a 14- year prospective study (NHANES III) involving 4652 older adults older than 60 years, the prevalence of sarcopenic obesity was  $18.1\%$  in women and  $42.9\%$  in men [31]. The prevalence of sarcopenic obesity is greater in patients with diabetes [32]. The variation in reported rates highlights the need for standardized diagnostic criteria to accurately assess and compare the prevalence of sarcopenic obesity across different demographic groups and regions.

# Standardized diagnostic criteria for sarcopenic obesity are essential in Asia-Oceania region with diverse ethnicities

Sarcopenic obesity, characterized by shared pathological factors such as aging, hormonal imbalances, and immunological changes, presents a greater risk for adverse health outcomes than either sarcopenia or obesity alone. Increasing evidence reveals associations of sarcopenic obesity with an increased risk of developing disability [33], mortality [34], metabolic disorders [35], cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) [36- 38], falls [39], osteoporosis [40,41] and other comorbidities [42], outweighing the risks associated with sarcopenia or obesity alone. The increasing prevalence and complex mechanism contributing to sarcopenic obesity highlight the need for increased awareness and research in this area. The development of effective strategies for prevention, early detection, and intervention is essential for managing the growing burden of sarcopenic obesity on individuals and healthcare systems worldwide.

Recently, European Association for the Study of Obesity (EASO) and European Society for Clinical Nutrition and Metabolism (ESPEN) proposed diagnostic criteria for sarcopenic obesity [8]. However, Asia- Oceania region's ethnic diversity results in varying body compositions and genetic predispositions, complicating the identification and management of sarcopenic obesity. Additionally, the significant variability in medical resources and accessibility across different countries

in the region further complicates accurate assessment and reporting. These differences necessitate tailored diagnostic criteria and treatment protocols.

# Results/recommendations

The collaborative initiative established a consensus on defining sarcopenic obesity and introduced a structured three- step algorithm for patient care, including screening, diagnosis, and intervention (Fig. 1). This approach aids healthcare providers in effectively identifying and managing sarcopenic obesity and enhancing patient care and outcomes.

Sarcopenic obesity is defined by the coexistence of central obesity and sarcopenia, which are characterized by low skeletal muscle mass and function, according to the AwGs definition. It is essential to recognize sarcopenic obesity as a distinct clinical condition, separate from either obesity or sarcopenia when considered individually. This distinction underscores the need for unique diagnostic and management strategies tailored to the specific challenges of sarcopenic obesity.

Central obesity is identified by an inappropriate accumulation of fat in the abdominal area, a critical factor distinguishing sarcopenic obesity. Sarcopenia, defined by the AwGs, involves reduced skeletal muscle mass and function. Sarcopenic obesity, therefore, uniquely integrates the health risks and challenges of both central fat accumulation and muscle depletion, necessitating clinical approaches distinct from those used for obesity or sarcopenia alone.

# Clinical considerations

The working group recommends initiating screening for sarcopenic obesity in individuals aged 40 years and older. However, for those aged 75 years and older, interventions should be approached with greater caution. This age- specific strategy acknowledges the increased prevalence of sarcopenic obesity with age while also recognizing the need for more careful consideration of interventions in older adults due to potential health vulnerabilities and not enough evidences for advantage of weight reduction in this age group.

Sarcopenia is diagnosed through a combination of criteria that assess muscle mass, muscle strength, and physical performance. Muscle Mass: The working group recommends the use of dual- energy X- ray absorptiometry (DXA) or bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA) to measure muscle mass. The diagnosis of sarcopenia considers appendicular skeletal muscle mass (ASM) adjusted for height squared (ASM/height2), with thresholds varying by guideline and population. Muscle Strength: The working group recommends handgrip strength measurement using the AWGS thresholds for sarcopenia:  $< 28\mathrm{kg}$  for men and  $< 18\mathrm{kg}$  for women. Physical Performance: This component is assessed by tests such as the gait speed test, with a threshold of  $\leq 1\mathrm{m / s}$  indicating sarcopenia; the Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB), with scores  $\leq 9$  suggesting poor physical performance; or the 5- Times Sit- to- Stand Test, with  $\geq 12s$  as an indicator of poor physical performance.

Obesity is traditionally diagnosed based on BMI. A BMI of  $\geq 30\mathrm{kg}/$ $\mathrm{m^2}$  is considered to indicate the presence of obesity unless the definitions/cut- offs are adapted to a specific country or population. However, for the diagnosis of sarcopenic obesity, it is crucial to assess body composition more precisely, focusing on fat distribution and percentage. The working group recommends waist circumference and waist- to- hip ratio as obesity measures. A high waist circumference or waist- to- hip ratio indicates central obesity, which is associated with metabolic risk. We recommend measuring the visceral fat area (VFA) through technical modalities such as DXA, computed tomography or magnetic resonance imaging, and body fat percentage via DXA or BIA.

# Step 1. Screening

In the screening phase for obesity, the working group recommends using BMI or waist circumference as a tool. Individuals categorized as overweight or obese based on BMI are recommended to proceed to the diagnostic phase. Similarly, those with a waist circumference exceeding the normal range should also be diagnosed. The cutoff values for BMI and waist circumference should align with the criteria set by each country. Tools with higher sensitivity for possible cases are recommended in the screening phase.

Furthermore, the easy use of these tools makes them universally applicable in various medical situations. A high BMI can indicate a high body fat percentage but does not specifically indicate central obesity. Moreover, an individual can have a normal BMI while still having central obesity. Thus, the working group recommends the inclusion of BMIdiagnosed overweight and obesity status instead of obesity alone. Additionally, those with abnormal waist circumference should also be assessed for sarcopenic obesity.

In the screening phase for sarcopenia, the working group recommends the use of screening tools, including calf circumference, the SARC- F, the SARC- CalF, and the Finger ring test [15]. The SARC- F is a questionnaire that comprises five items: Strength (S), Assistance in walking (A), Rising from a chair (R), Climbing stairs (C), and Falls (F). The SARC- CalF is based on the SARC- F with the additional measurement of calf circumference. Furthermore, medical staff who clinically suspect sarcopenia are encouraged to implement further surveys. The working group follows the recommendation of the AwGs 2019, with calf circumference  $< 34~\mathrm{cm}$  for men and  $< 33~\mathrm{cm}$  for women for screening or case- finding. In the finger- ring test, individuals use both their hands index fingers and thumbs to encircle the thickest part of their nondominant calf. An increased risk for sarcopenia is considered if their calf circumference fits within or is smaller than the circle formed by their fingers. A SARC- F score equal to or greater than 4 is suggested for diagnosis. The SARC- F is more useful for facilitating referrals of at- risk individuals to receive a definitive diagnosis because of its low sensitivity and high specificity. The sensitivity of the SARC- F is enhanced by adding calf circumference to the SARC- CalF [43], where a score equal to or greater than 11 is suggested for confirming a diagnosis of sarcopenia.

Positive screenings for both obesity and sarcopenia should be followed by further investigation to diagnose sarcopenic obesity. Additionally, upon detecting a positive screening for either condition, practitioners should adhere to the recommendations in the respective guidelines for each condition.

# Step 2. Diagnosis

In the diagnostic phase for obesity, the working group recommends focusing on central obesity. Central obesity is considered a more significant predictor of health risks than BMI alone [44]. Fat that accumulates in the abdomen is metabolically active and is linked to increased risks of metabolic and cardiovascular diseases [45,46]. Since sarcopenia and sarcopenic obesity become more prevalent with increasing age, this raises concerns about the obesity paradox. The obesity paradox refers to the observation that, in specific populations, overweight or obese status is associated with a lower risk of mortality compared to normal- weight status [47]. This phenomenon has been particularly noted in the older population. In older individuals, having extra weight may provide nutritional reserves during illness. Thus, fat mass distribution is an essential issue in older individuals. Fat distribution may play a role in the protective effect observed in the obesity paradox. Compared with visceral fat, peripheral fat (around the hips and thighs) is thought to have different metabolic effects.

The working group advocates for the inclusion of central or abdominal obesity as a critical criterion in the diagnosis of sarcopenic obesity. This approach recognizes the significant role that fat accumulation in the abdominal region plays in the condition's health implications, distinguishing it from general obesity by its metabolic effects and association with increased health risks. By focusing on central obesity, the working group can more accurately identify individuals at risk and tailor interventions to address the unique challenges presented by the co- occurrence of sarcopenia and central obesity. The working group recommends using visceral fat area, body fat percentage, BMI, waist circumference, or waist- to- hip ratio in the diagnosis phase of obesity. We recommend measuring visceral fat area or body fat percentage as the first option. However, these measurements may not be feasible in all settings due to variations in medical resources across countries or regions. In such cases, waist circumference, waist- to- hip ratio, or BMI can be used for the diagnosis of obesity.

In the diagnosis phase of sarcopenia, the working group recommends combining muscle strength and physical performance with the ASM index. The working group supports the AwGs recommendation for the use of handgrip strength for these assessments. In Asia, the most commonly used devices for measuring handgrip strength are spring- type dynamometers (Smedley) and hydraulic- type devices (Jamar). According to Asian population studies, low muscle strength diagnostic cutoffs of handgrip strength  $< 28.0\mathrm{kg}$  for men and  $< 18.0\mathrm{kg}$  for women are recommended [48]. Published research has utilized a comprehensive range of physical performance tests, including the SPPB, usual gait speed test, 6- min walk test, stair- climb power test, timed- up- and- go test, and 5- times chair stand test. According to the recommendations of the AWGS 2019, an SPPB score below 9 is considered the cutoff for low physical performance. Additionally, a 5- times chair stand time of 12 s or longer has been proposed as an alternative measure for diagnosing sarcopenia, serving as a surrogate for gait speed. A slow gait speed, defined as a gait speed less than  $1\mathrm{m / s}$  indicates reduced physical performance.

The working group categorized three groups in the diagnosis phase

of sarcopenic obesity: normal, possible sarcopenic obesity and sarco- penic obesity. Individuals who meet the criteria for obesity and exhibit either low physical performance or reduced muscle strength- but not decreased muscle mass- are categorized as having possible sarcopenic obesity. Conversely, those who meet the criteria for both obesity and sarcopenia are classified as having sarcopenic obesity. Individuals who meet the criteria for sarcopenia but not obesity should be categorized according to the recommendations of the AWGS 2019.

Considering areas with limited resources, we suggest that individuals with obesity who have either low muscle strength or poor physical performance in the community but do not have access to DXA or BIA should be categorized as having possible sarcopenic obesity.

# Step 3.Intervention

The primary objective of interventions targeting sarcopenic obesity is to simultaneously increase skeletal muscle mass and decrease adipose tissue mass [1,2]. Lifestyle modifications, particularly diet consultation, and increased physical activity are identified as the key strategies for combating sarcopenic obesity [49]. The success of these interventions requires attention to the detailed assessment of changes in body composition and functional parameters, avoiding the narrow focus of solely measuring changes in overall body weight [6].

Managing weight in older adults is complex [50,51]. Implementing an energy- restricted, hypocaloric diet can lead to a loss of approximately  $25\%$  of skeletal muscle mass, thereby intensifying sarcopenia [52,53]. Strategies designed to enhance muscle protein synthesis during weight loss interventions- such as integrating diets with resistance training, timing food consumption proximate to exercise sessions, or evenly spreading protein intake throughout the day- can help mitigate the sarcopenic effects of weight reduction [51,54- 56]. For individuals with sarcopenic obesity, a combination of diet- induced weight loss and consistent exercise enhances physical function and reduces frailty more effectively than either intervention in isolation [56- 58].

Elevating dietary protein intake is crucial during weight loss to activate skeletal muscle protein synthesis [59,60]. Due to their anabolic resistance, older adults require more protein to stimulate protein synthesis effectively. For maintaining optimal muscle function, a dietary protein intake of  $1 - 1.2\mathrm{g}$  per kilogram per day is advised, with recommendations for an even greater intake for older individuals affected by sarcopenia or other chronic conditions.

Exercise training can significantly improve aspects of physical fitness, such as muscle strength and power, but does not necessarily lead to reductions in body fat or gains in skeletal muscle or lean mass [61]. Resistance training is a potent intervention for managing sarcopenic obesity [62,63]. Combining increased protein intake with exercise could enhance the reduction in fat mass [50,64]. Oral nutritional supplements (ONSs) play a role in managing and preventing sarcopenia, which is the age- related loss of muscle mass, strength, and function [65]. The working group suggested the use of ONSs in individuals with sarcopenic obesity. Protein is vital for muscle repair and growth. ONSs often contain high levels of protein or amino acids (such as leucine) that are beneficial for maintaining and increasing muscle mass. In addition,  $\beta$  - hydroxy-  $\beta$  methylbutyrate (HMB) is a metabolite of the amino acid leucine and has been studied for its potential benefits in muscle health, especially in combination with resistance training [66,67]. Increasing protein intake through diet or supplements can significantly help counteract muscle loss in older adults. Micronutrients, including vitamins and minerals, play critical roles in muscle function and overall health. For example, vitamin D and calcium are essential for bone health and support muscular function [68,69]. Other nutrients, such as magnesium [70], vitamin B [71], and omega- 3 fatty acids [72], have been shown to support muscle health and function [73].

The consensus panel suggested that for patients with possible sarcopenic obesity, a combination of exercise, dietary consultation, and lifestyle modifications should be used for treatment. Additionally, for individuals with confirmed sarcopenic obesity, the panel recommends considering ONSs and medical interventions targeting either sarcopenia or obesity.

Integrating resistance training and dietary modifications is the best approach for managing sarcopenic obesity. This finding aligns with existing evidence suggesting significant benefits for muscle mass and strength [1,2]. However, the panel detected discrepancies in dietary recommendations, underscoring the need for personalized nutrition plans based on individual patient profiles. For individuals at risk of or experiencing sarcopenic obesity, a personalized nutrition plan that includes adequate protein, calories, and essential nutrients, possibly supplemented by ONSs, is often recommended. Moreover, there are currently no approved medications for sarcopenic obesity, as they are still under investigation.

No medications are currently approved for sarcopenic obesity. However, promising treatments under investigation include myostatin inhibitors [74] to boost muscle mass and GLP- 1 receptor agonists [75] to reduce fat while preserving lean tissue. The panel recommends integrating such agents, once approved, with lifestyle modifications, pending phase III trial outcomes expected within 5- 10 years. The working group recommends considering future medication use in addition to lifestyle modifications for managing sarcopenic obesity. These strategies should be tailored to individual health conditions and needs, ideally under healthcare professionals' guidance.

Effective management of sarcopenic obesity, particularly when complications arise, necessitates a multidisciplinary approach involving various healthcare specialists. Collaboration with orthopedic surgeons is important when patients with sarcopenic obesity also suffer from osteoarthritis. For adults aged equal or older than 75, interventions like increased dietary protein and resistance training carry potential limitations. High protein intake may strain renal function in those with preexisting kidney disease, while resistance training could exacerbate joint issues or increase fall risk if not supervised. The panel recommends cautious implementation, with renal function monitoring and tailored exercise plans by physiotherapists, especially given limited evidence on weight loss benefits in this age group. Future research should focus on the long- term outcomes of these interventions across diverse populations to validate and refine our recommendations.

# Controversies and limitations

The panel identified several areas needing further research, including the exploration of pharmacological treatments for sarcopenic obesity, the development of more accessible diagnostic tools, and longitudinal studies to assess the long- term effects of various treatment modalities. The consensus depends on the available evidence, which limits the quality, quantity, and extent of research on sarcopenic obesity. Areas with insufficient high- quality research can lead to less robust or evidence- based recommendations, potentially affecting the effectiveness of the proposed strategies.

The consensus on sarcopenic obesity represents a critical step forward in the unified understanding and management of this complex condition. Through collaborative efforts during a 6- month period, the working group has successfully delineated key diagnostic criteria, effective management strategies, and essential preventive measures. These efforts underscore the importance of a multidisciplinary approach to tackle the dual challenges of muscle loss and obesity, particularly in aging populations.

The consensus highlights the need for standardized diagnostic tools that are accessible and practical across various clinical settings, ensuring that sarcopenic obesity can be identified and treated effectively. The strong emphasis on lifestyle interventions, such as improved nutrition and regular physical activity, particularly resistance training, reflects the consensus's commitment to addressing the root causes of sarcopenic obesity in a sustainable manner.

However, the consensus also acknowledges the inherent limitations

of the current evidence base and the variability in expert opinions. Ethnic diversity within Asia- Oceania poses a limitation to the generalizability of these criteria. Cutofs for muscle mass and waist circumference may not uniformly apply across all ethnic groups, potentially under- or over- diagnosing sarcopenic obesity in certain populations. Resource disparities (e.g., DXA availability) further complicate uniform application. Future research should validate these criteria in underrepresented ethnic groups to refine cutoffs and enhance applicability. There is a need for ongoing research to fill existing knowledge gaps, particularly in areas such as the long- term efficacy of interventions and the development of pharmacological options. Future research should also be aimed at understanding the impacts of sarcopenic obesity on diverse populations and to update the consensus as new evidence emerges.

# Conclusions

The updated definitions and clinical diagnostic criteria for sarcopenic obesity presented in this consensus are designed to enhance early recognition and management of the conditions. This consensus highlights the significance of sarcopenic obesity as a distinct clinical entity, underscoring the need for targeted interventions. By standardizing diagnostic approaches, healthcare professionals can improve diagnostic accuracy and tailor treatment strategies more effectively.
