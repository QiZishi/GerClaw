# ABSTRACT

Background: Hearing loss poses a significant public health challenge as a common chronic condition with many known side effects that are often worse when left untreated. Aural rehabilitation (AR) is an umbrella term that encompasses a range of interventions (e.g., informational counseling and perceptual training) designed to reduce deficits related to hearing loss that may stand alone or be used in combination as part of a holistic plan.

Purpose: This evidence- based clinical practice guideline is intended to inform the implementation of person- centered AR to adults with hearing loss. Given the well- known benefits of sensory management, it was not included within the scope of this guideline. The recommendations aim to help clinicians, individuals with hearing loss, and other stakeholders make evidence- informed treatment decisions and improve clinical outcomes, as well as provide payers and policy- makers with information detailing a comprehensive approach to AR.

Method: The American Speech- Language- Hearing Association (ASHA) and a multidisciplinary panel of subject matter experts prioritized key clinical questions and outcomes that served as the foundation of the guideline. The clinical recommendations were based on a comprehensive systematic review and a meta- analysis of 85 studies published between 1978 and 2021.

Recommendations: Given the current state of the evidence, resource considerations, patient acceptability, clinical feasibility, and the overall balance of benefits and harms, ASHA developed several evidence- based recommendations for the provision of AR to adults with hearing loss. Each recommendation is accompanied by a series of key practice points to support its implementation within a person- centered framework.

# Executive Summary:

Hearing loss is the third most common chronic condition affecting  $20\%$  of the world's global population (Blackwell et al., 2014; Chadha et al., 2021). The known side effects of hearing loss are damaging and costly not only to those with hearing loss but also to the health care sector and society overall (Chadha et al.,

2021). Therefore, the American Speech-LanguageHearing Association (ASHA), together with a multidisciplinary panel of subject matter experts, has developed this evidence-based clinical practice guideline for the provision of aural rehabilitation (AR) services to adults with hearing loss based on a meta-analysis of 85 studies published between 1978 and 2021.

To investigate the effects of AR in adults with hearing loss, the Panel defined clinical questions isolating the distinct interventions of AR (i.e., informational counseling, personal adjustment counseling, perceptual training, and multicomponent AR) and examined aspects of service delivery. Due to the known benefits of sensory management, it was excluded from the scope of this guideline, as the goal was to understand the value of each component of AR on key patient outcomes above and beyond amplification. Given the current state of the evidence, resource considerations, patient acceptability, clinical feasibility, and the overall balance of benefits and harms, in addition to appropriate sensory management, ASHA recommends that adults with hearing loss should receive AR within a person- centered framework to include at least one of the following four approaches: informational counseling, personal adjustment counseling, perceptual training, or multicomponent AR.

The Panel additionally developed a series of key practice points to detail and support the clinical implementation and service delivery aspects of each clinical recommendation. Clinicians should use the recommendations in combination with the intervention- specific key practice points to aid in the development and implementation of person- centered AR.

# Introduction

This evidence- based clinical practice guideline is an official statement of ASHA, providing evidence- based recommendations pertaining to AR in adults with hearing loss. The guideline was developed by a multidisciplinary panel made up of subject matter experts involved in the delivery of services to adults with hearing loss and methodologists from ASHA's National Center for Evidence- Based Practice in Communication Disorders (N- CEP). In line with current practices in guideline development, ASHA adopted the Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation (GRADE) methodology (Guyatt et al., 2008). Recommendations were formulated based on a comprehensive systematic review of the evidence and the overall quality and estimated effects of the evidence. These findings, along with other considerations such as the balance of benefits versus harms of AR, patient/caregiver values and preferences, feasibility, acceptability, and resource implications, were carefully considered by the Panel to determine recommendations.

These guidelines and recommendations are not intended to serve as a standard of care. Clinicians must determine the applicability of these guidelines based on expertise, knowledge, and the unique clinical status of the individual patient through a collaborative process that incorporates the patient's values and preferences and, as appropriate, those of their families and/or caregivers. The recommendations should be considered, along with the justification, summary of findings, and key practice points, to facilitate understanding and clinical implementation of the guideline. Potential barriers to the implementation of these guidelines may include resources and reimbursement, institutional policies, and/or the limitations of specific clinical settings.

# Purpose and Scope

# Aim

The recommendations made in this guideline are intended to inform the implementation of person- centered AR services to adults with hearing loss supported by scientific evidence to help clinicians, individuals with hearing loss, their families or caregivers, and patient advocates make evidence- informed treatment decisions and improve clinical outcomes. Additional aims include providing payers and policymakers with information detailing a comprehensive approach to AR in terms of effectiveness, content, and service delivery and identifying future research needs.

# Intended Audience

The target audience of this guideline is clinicians (audiologists, speech- language pathologists, otolaryngologists, and other rehabilitation specialists), adults with hearing loss and their families or caregivers, payers, and policymakers. By providing key stakeholders with information on the available evidence and evidence- based recommendations, these guidelines strive to provide support for joint decision making that culminates in effective treatment for adults with hearing loss.

# Scope

This guideline provides recommendations for the provision of AR services to adults (ages 18 years or older) with hearing loss across all health care settings. It applies to those with all types (e.g., sensorineural, permanent conductive, single- sided deafness, sudden onset, and early or late onset) and degrees of hearing loss, including those with mild (26- 40 dB HL), moderate (41- 70 dB HL), severe (71- 90 dB HL), or profound (91+ dB HL) losses. The guideline pertains to users of amplification (e.g., hearing aids, cochlear implants, and assistive listening devices) and nonusers. Although this guideline covers most key aspects of AR, it does not directly address sensory management (e.g., hearing aids, cochlear implants, and assistive listening devices) of hearing loss or the provision of amplification.

# Description of the Problem

# Hearing Loss

Hearing LossHearing loss in adults is a major public health issue as the third most common chronic physical condition in the United States (Blackwell et al., 2014) and in most cases is not treatable with medical or surgical intervention. According to estimates from the World Health Organization, there are 1.5 billion people in the world with hearing loss, which is  $20\%$  of the world's population. Although the majority have mild hearing loss, 430 million  $(5.5\%)$  have moderate- to- profound degrees of hearing loss. Hearing loss prevalence is slightly higher among men and men with age, increasing from  $12.7\%$  at 60 years of age to  $58\%$  at 90 years of age (Chadha et al., 2021). Due to an aging population in the United States, hearing loss is expected to grow from  $15\%$  (44.1 million) in 2020 to  $22.6\%$  (73.5 million) by 2060 among adults (20 years or older), with an even steeper increase in prevalence for those who are 70 years of age or older (Gorman et al., 2017).

# Sequelae

SequelaeThere are many known side effects of hearing loss, especially when untreated, that can impact the person with hearing loss, as well as significant others, family, and friends. Increased rates of depression (Lawrence et al., 2020), reduced quality of life (Ciorba et al., 2012), and increased social isolation (Nachtegaal et al., 2009) have been identified in individuals with hearing loss. More recently, age- related hearing loss has been linked to cognitive decline, cognitive impairment, and incident dementia (Lin et al., 2011; Loughrey et al., 2018). Additionally, those with hearing loss are at a significantly higher risk of falls, with older adults with hearing loss being 2.39 times more likely to fall than those with normal hearing (Jiam et al., 2016). The associated sequelae of hearing loss vary depending on not only the severity of the individual's hearing loss but also whether it has been managed with rehabilitation, any co- occurring conditions (e.g., vision impairment and dementia), and how supportive the environment is to those with hearing loss.

# Societal Costs

Societal CostsUntreated hearing loss is costly to the health care sector and to society overall. The estimated global cost of untreated hearing loss is over  $880 billion annually (Chadha et al., 2021). This includes the direct costs of unaddressed hearing loss to the health care ($ 314 billion) and education sectors, as well as the loss of productivity ( $182.5 billion) and societal costs ($ 456.5 billion). In the United States, unemployment rates are nearly twice as high for people with severe untreated hearing loss (15.6%) compared to those who use amplification (8.3%; Kochkin, 2010). Additionally, untreated hearing loss has been associated with 46% higher total health care costs of $22,434 over a 10- year period when compared to total costs of adults without hearing loss. The increased health care costs of individuals with untreated hearing loss stem from higher health care utilization with more inpatient stays, longer hospital stays, an increased risk of hospital readmissions, and more outpatient visits than those without hearing loss (Reed et al., 2019).

# Amplification Costs and Use

Amplification Costs and UseAs there is no cure for many types of hearing loss, one of the key components of management is the use of amplification (e.g., hearing aids and cochlear implants). Hearing aids range in price depending on several different factors including style and technology level, but the average out- of- pocket cost for one hearing aid is \(1,601, and most wearers utilize two aids (Kochkin, 2009). Despite the known benefits of hearing aids, there is a significant gap of 9 years, on average, between hearing aid candidacy and adoption (Simpson et al., 2019), and only about \(30\%\) of those with reported hearing difficulty own a hearing aid (Abrams & Kihm, 2015). Furthermore, of those who own hearing aids, regular usage varies widely. Only \(69.1\%\) and \(87\%\) of new and experienced hearing aid users, respectively, report wearing their hearing aids more than \(4 \mathrm{hr}\) per day, whereas \(23.4\%\) and \(8.5\%\) use them less than \(2 \mathrm{hr}\) per day and \(12.9\%\) and \(3.5\%\), respectively, do not use them at all (Kochkin et al., 2010). Given that many who own hearing aids do not use them regularly, the costs of nonutilization can be very high. As part of a publicly funded, device- centered program in Australia, Hogan et al. (2020) estimated that at a cost of \(1,829 AUD (about\)1,400 USD) per client, the annual costs of nonutilization of hearing devices are approximately \(87.4 million AUD (roughly\)67.4 million USD). In addition, of those fitted with hearing aids, device satisfaction rates are often only in the range of \(50\% - 70\%\) (Kochkin, 1999) but are improving up to \(77\% - 91\%\) for aids obtained between 2009 and 2014 (Abrams & Kihm, 2015). For this reason, it is important to understand how other interventions may support the use of amplification, supplement it, or be used in its absence to improve patient- important outcomes of interest.

# Person-Centered Care

Person- Centered CareDelivery of health care services using a person- centered care (PCC) model has become the gold standard (M. H. H. Kramer et al., 2014) and has been highly advocated within many health professions due to its known benefits in treatment adherence and health outcomes (Michie et al., 2003; Stewart, 1995). In a person- centered approach, health professionals respect the interests and autonomy of patients and encourage them to be active participants in their health care (M. H. H. Kramer et al., 2014). Research has shown that  $69\%$  of patients prefer a

person- centered communication style to a direct, biomedical style (Swenson et al., 2004). The needs and preferences of patients are an integral component of treatment and rehabilitation planning when using PCC. Discussing the person's beliefs and values, involving the patient in shared decision making, and using PCC have been associated with better treatment compliance, patient health outcomes, and patient satisfaction for those with chronic health conditions (Michie et al., 2003). Moreover, studies have shown that using person- centered methods is cost effective and often provides substantial returns on investment, which could help reassure hesitant clinicians and payers that this is a feasible option (Gyllensten et al., 2017; Pirhonen et al., 2020).

Considering hearing loss in adults is a chronic condition, a PCC approach should be fundamental when developing a plan to address each person's unique hearing needs and preferences. PCC helps establish individuals as equal partners in the management of their hearing loss and incorporates empathy, active listening, and reflective conversations (Ida Institute, n.d.). It has been shown that, in terms of help seeking, rehabilitation of hearing loss, and hearing aid adoption, patients value a person- centered approach and want their unique needs to be understood, to be supported in shared decision making, and to feel empowered through provision of information about their intervention options (Laplante- Levesque et al., 2010, 2012; Poost- Foroosh et al., 2011). Involving the patient, family members, and communication partners as active participants in the goal- setting and decision- making processes and tailoring information according to the patient's needs set the foundation for a successful rehabilitation experience.

# AR

AR is a large umbrella term encompassing a wide range of interventions. Although many definitions of AR are available, we have structured our review around the following definition: "the reduction of hearing- loss- induced deficits of function, activity, participation, and quality of life through sensory management, instruction, perceptual training, and counseling" (Boothroyd, 2007). Other terms such as audiological and auditory rehabilitation are also used to refer to a similar set of subset of interventions. Hearing loss affects people differently, depending on not only the severity but also the person's lifestyle, communicative needs, and environments.

# Components of AR

To best support adults with hearing loss, the following components are used to mitigate and reduce the deficits of function, activity, and participation:

Sensory management: directly targets and aims to improve the auditory signal/sensory input. This typically involves the use of amplification devices, including, but not limited to, hearing aids, cochlear implants, bone anchored hearing aids, personal sound amplification products, FM systems, or assistive listening devices. This category includes not only the devices but also the fitting, programming, and fine- tuning adjustments.

Informational counseling (instruction): focuses on providing education regarding hearing loss, amplification, available tools/resources, prevention and conservation, associated symptoms, communication strategies training, and the range of possible treatments. This also includes providing demonstration and instruction in the use, care, and management of sensory devices. An informed patient is better prepared to actively participate in the establishment of a care plan and self- manage a chronic condition such as hearing loss.

Perceptual training: directly targets the ability to process incoming auditory or visual signals. The goal of this training is to enhance auditory or auditory- visual abilities. Different types of perceptual training include auditory training, lipreading/speechreading training, and speech tracking. Speech perception is a common complaint among adults with hearing loss, and perceptual training can serve as listening practice and support.

Personal adjustment counseling: focuses on the psychological, social, and emotional acceptance of the hearing loss and/or related disorders. It may also address an individual's readiness to engage in the rehabilitation process. This includes prefitting engagement activities, motivational interviewing, prefitting and postfitting counseling, peer support systems, and other interventions that aim to develop and support psychosocial management skills. Personal adjustment counseling can facilitate acceptance to allow for appropriate management of the stressors commonly associated with hearing loss.

Each of the components of AR serves as support for individuals with hearing loss in a unique way and can aid in the management of hearing loss as a chronic condition. Although sometimes only a single component of AR may be warranted, many AR plans are holistic, multifaceted programs that incorporate more than one component of management (e.g., informational counseling and perceptual training). Understanding the potential contribution of each intervention in isolation and in combination is crucial so that adults with hearing loss and their provider can collaborate in the development of a rehabilitation plan. This review sought to examine the effect of these components, in combination with or in the absence of amplification, on the key critical outcomes to support and inform the implementation of AR within a person- centered framework.

# AR Service Delivery

The development of a comprehensive AR program considers not only what components should be provided but

also other key variables such as the timing (i.e., early vs. later), the format (e.g., direct vs. indirect, in- person vs. remote), and who should be directly involved in the AR plan. The timing aspect relates to when AR services are provided relative to an initial diagnosis, a decline in hearing ability over time, or the fitting of amplification. When hearing loss is present, the brain undergoes cortical changes that are attributed to sensory deprivation. Due to neuroplasticity, cortical reorganization has been observed with treatments such as amplification (Glick & Sharma, 2020). Because the timing of intervention can make a difference on both cortical reorganization and patient outcomes, we want to investigate if there is an ideal time for AR, specifically if the provision of services earlier versus later impacts patient outcomes.

Services may be rendered directly by a clinician or indirectly through written or online formats. Historically, the predominant service delivery model included a direct format often accompanied with indirect materials for support, but with the recent widespread surge in technology availability and use, many rehabilitative services are now delivered completely in an indirect format. As such, it is important to understand if the format in which AR is delivered influences the effectiveness of the intervention.

Another aspect to consider is the impact of including the patient's partner or family in the AR process. The presence and involvement of a communication partner (e.g., spouse, child, and caregiver) may affect not only the person with hearing loss but also the partner, as it has been found that significant others may experience decreased quality of life and social functioning (Kamil & Lin, 2015). Within a person- centered approach, communication partners are often involved in key activities (e.g., providing information for the needs assessment, contributing to shared decision making, and assisting with goal setting); however, they may not necessarily always be included as active participants in the AR interventions (e.g., perceptual training and personal adjustment counseling). By examining the direct participation of communication partners in AR, we can determine how that expanded role affects both them and the individual with hearing loss. Utilizing a variety of service delivery techniques allows for flexibility of approach while managing hearing loss as a chronic condition, but it is important to be aware of the impact of such models on the outcomes of adults with hearing loss.

# Exclusion of Sensory Management

Although sensory management is one of the key components of AR for adults with hearing loss and is used to improve hearing and mitigate its side effects, we have excluded it from the scope of this guideline. Several systematic reviews have already documented the positive effects of sensory management, both hearing aids and cochlear implants, on a number of key outcomes. Hearing aid use was associated with significant positive effects on generic and hearing- specific quality of life measures, participation restrictions, as well as speech perception and listening ability (Chisolm et al., 2007; Ferguson et al., 2017; Johnson et al., 2016). Reviews of cochlear implants reveal similar findings. Cochlear implantation resulted in positive gains in speech perception and substantial improvements in quality of life (Berrettini et al., 2011; Gaylor et al., 2013; McRackan, Bauschard, Hatch, Franko- Tobin, Droghini, Nguyen, & Dubno, 2018; McRackan, Bauschard, Hatch, Franko- Tobin, Droghini, Velozo, et al., 2018). Because the current evidence highlights the benefit and utility of sensory devices and management, it was not directly addressed in this guideline. We have excluded studies that examine solely the benefit of amplification devices. If additional AR services were provided in conjunction with the devices and we were able to isolate the effect of the services, those studies were included. Although sensory management was not included as an intervention, the effects of AR on device- related outcomes (e.g., device use and device benefit) were considered.

# Person-Centered Approach to AR

Because two individuals with the same audiogram can experience very different challenges, a person's needs cannot simply be defined by the audiological profile. As such, the development of a personalized AR plan utilizing a PCC approach is important for all adults with hearing loss. PCC reflects the manner and style of engagement and interaction between the clinician and the person with hearing loss during the intervention process (e.g., active listening, reflective conversations, establishing a therapeutic alliance, and understanding of individual needs and preferences), but it is not an AR intervention. Therefore, the effects of PCC were not directly examined or considered to be within the scope of this document. In this review and guideline, our aim was to determine the effectiveness of the various AR interventions, in combination with or in lieu of amplification, to provide decisional support when developing an AR plan and to aid in the clinical implementation of such interventions through the provision of key practice points. To inform the discussions between the patient and the provider, the scientific evidence, patient preferences/goals, and clinical expertise of the provider should be used in combination with these guidelines.

# Method

The development of this clinical practice guideline was supported by a comprehensive systematic review of the scientific evidence. Although the review protocol was not preregistered on a publicly available site, all key aspects (e.g., clinical questions, outcomes, inclusion/exclusion criteria, and quality appraisals) of the systematic review and guideline

process, as outlined below, were established a priori and were strictly followed to minimize potential bias.

# Panel Composition and Conflicts of Interest

A multidisciplinary volunteer panel composed of researchers, clinicians, and lay people with expertise in audiology, speech- language pathology, otolaryngology, or patient advocacy developed these guidelines. The ASHA staff coordinated the work of the Panel and provided methodological support. ASHA fully funded the development of these guidelines, but neither the organization nor its staff had any role or influence in selecting the questions or determining the recommendations. All panel members disclosed conflicts of interest prior to the initiation of this guideline, and none was conflicted out of participation (see Appendix A).

# Formulating Clinical Questions

The objective of this guideline was to determine the effectiveness of and provide actionable guidance on the provision of AR to adults with hearing loss. Given how multifaceted and diverse the treatments included as a part of AR are, the Panel chose to address this broad topic through a series of questions targeting the individual aspects of AR and key implementation features.

The Panel generated an a priori list of potential clinical questions using a PICO (population, intervention, comparison, outcome) framework to serve as the foundation of the guideline and the key questions for the systematic review. As part of this process, the Panel defined each AR component and provided a nonexhaustive list of the types of interventions contained within each. Each panel member rated all questions for relative importance. Questions deemed to be critical formed the basis of this guideline (see Table 1).

# Determining Outcomes

The Panel considered a broad array of outcomes based on the key characteristics and consequences of hearing loss. Outcomes, as well as examples of common outcome measures included under each domain, were identified for each clinical question a priori and assessed for their relative importance for clinical decision making by each member of the Panel. Those deemed critical or important were analyzed as part of this review (see Table 2).

To examine the self- perceived difficulties brought about by hearing loss, the hearing disability and perceived disability outcome domains were included. Due to the known sequelae of untreated hearing loss, the Panel selected cognition, communication, hearing satisfaction, mental health, mood/well- being, quality of life, and social engagement as outcomes. Self- efficacy was included to explore the impact of AR on one's ability to advocate and manage the hearing loss. Moreover, since it is well known that hearing loss affects more than just the person with hearing difficulties, third- party disability was identified to examine the impact on spouses, significant others, communication partners, and so forth. Although the anticipated adverse effects of AR are minimal overall, the Panel found it important to include this as an outcome to be able to weigh the benefits in relation to the harms. Adverse effects of interest included sound aversiveness, hearing aid complaints, pain, patient concerns regarding the intervention, or ongoing difficulties with the operation and management of the hearing aid or device.

# Selection Criteria

Randomized controlled trials and nonrandomized controlled studies were selected for inclusion into the review and subsequent guideline if they met the following a priori selection criteria:

The study reported original data and was published in English. The study included adult participants aged 18 years or older presenting with hearing loss greater than 25 dB HL in at least one ear averaged across a minimum of three frequencies in the range of 250- 8000 Hz OR fitted with any type of amplification (including unilateral) with any type of hearing loss (e.g., sensorineural, conductive, or mixed). If a study did not give details of the level of hearing loss for participants, we assumed those fitted with hearing aids or other amplification met these criteria. Studies with participants under the age of 18 years were included if the data for the adults could be accessed separately or if the average age of the participants was at least 18 years. The study involved an AR intervention, as defined by the Panel, as the independent variable and directly addressed one or more clinical questions. The study used at least one validated outcome measure for one or more of the included outcome domain(s).

Studies were excluded if they met one of the following criteria:

They evaluated different amplification technologies or device features (e.g., directional microphones and frequency- lowering technology). They were observational or uncontrolled studies. They did not use any validated outcome measures or if the measures were modified by the study authors. They used all relevant outcome measures as trained tasks in the intervention. They examined the comparative effectiveness of two approaches within the same AR component (e.g., synthetic perceptual training vs. analytic perceptual training).

# Publication Screening

Two reviewers independently screened the titles and abstracts of all potentially relevant articles identified by the systematic search and made a binary judgment as to the eligibility of a study (i.e., eligible vs. ineligible). For all disagreements, the full- text article was retrieved to determine inclusion. Similar to title and abstract screening, the same two reviewers independently assessed the full text of the selected abstracts to determine study eligibility. To assess agreement between reviewers, kappa coefficients were calculated and evaluated using the parameters outlined by Landis and Koch (1977), where a value of  $< 0$  indicates no agreement, 0.01- 0.20 is slight, 0.21- 0.40 is fair, 0.41- 0.60 is moderate, 0.61- 0.80 is substantial, and 0.81- 1.00 is almost perfect agreement. Reviewer agreement was considered substantial  $(\kappa = .77)$  for title and abstract screening and almost perfect  $(\kappa = .89)$  for full- text sifting. Blinded title, abstract, and full- text sifting was managed using Covidence systematic review software (Veritas Health Innovation; Covidence, n.d.). All disagreements were resolved through discussion until consensus was reached.

# Quality Assessment

The same two masked reviewers assessed each study and the relevant outcomes from those studies using Cochrane's risk of bias tool across the following six domains: sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of participants and personnel, blinding of outcome assessors, incomplete outcome data, and selective outcome reporting (Higgins et al., 2011). Each domain was rated as low risk, high risk, or unclear risk of bias. To determine risk of bias for incomplete outcome data, we used the What Works Clearinghouse (WWC) standards (WWC, 2017). To be considered low risk of bias, a study had to meet WWC's optimistic threshold using a combination of overall attrition and differential attrition between groups.

The level of agreement between raters was calculated for all quality indicators. Of the six indicators, five (sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of participants and personnel, incomplete outcome data, and selective outcome reporting) had substantial agreement and one (blinding of assessors) had almost perfect agreement. Inconsistencies between reviewers were documented and resolved by discussion until consensus was reached.

# Data Extraction and Management

One reviewer extracted data from the full text of included studies using a predesigned data extraction form. The data extraction form underwent pilot testing prior to use, and no changes were made during the review. Extracted data included participant (e.g., age, diagnosis, and severity) and intervention (e.g., type and dosage)

characteristics and outcomes of interest. Each study was also assigned to one or more clinical questions. A second reviewer checked the data extraction elements and assigned clinical question(s) for accuracy.

If data were missing or not reported in a usable format for the analysis (i.e., end point means and standard deviations) and the study was published after 1999, we contacted the corresponding author to obtain relevant data. If we were unable to obtain the data from the author and the information was presented in a graphical format, numerical values were extracted from images using WebPlotDigitizer Version 4.3 (Rohatgi, 2020). The WebPlotDigitizer software was chosen based on high levels of intercoder agreement (Drevon et al., 2017).

Additionally, when possible, data were imputed from other formats such as medians and ranges, mean quotients, mean  $t$  scores, or  $95\%$  confidence interval (CI) to estimated mean and standard deviation values using the methods described by Follman et al. (1992), Hozo et al. (2005), and Wan et al. (2014). The overall summary of these methods is described in Webe et al. (2006). If standard error of the mean (SEM) was reported, the standard deviation was calculated by multiplying the SEM by the square root of the sample size.

For multiarm studies, when appropriate, we combined data from two arms (e.g., no- treatment group and passive control group) into a single group to prevent double counting of participants or arbitrarily excluding pertinent data (Higgins et al., 2020). For consistency in reporting and to aid in the interpretation of results, when necessary, we converted the direction of scores such that an increasing score would reflect improvement.

# Study Outcome Management

Based on the Panel's expertise, we anticipated multiplicity (e.g., studies that report more than one outcome within a single domain or use multiple measures of the same outcome) within studies would pose a challenge to data analysis. Some likely cases of multiplicity were foreseen, such as objective and subjective measures of device use (e.g., data logging vs. self- report), as well as subtest scores and total scores from a single outcome measure falling within the same outcome domain. To minimize selection bias, the Panel made a priori decisions as to how to handle these scenarios. If a study reported both data logging from a device and self- report of device use, we would select the objective measure of data logging. For all outcome measures, we placed each individual subtest and the total score (if provided) from each study into the relevant outcome domain(s). If all subscales ended up in the same outcome domain or only a total score was provided, we would use the total score.

Other cases of multiplicity, however, were not anticipated ahead of time. Therefore, subsequent to the search and prior to any data extraction or analysis, we developed a multistep process of decision rules to reduce multiplicity. When an individual and their communication partner both assessed an outcome domain (e.g., hearing disability as assessed by the individual with hearing loss and by their communication partner), we included the self- assessed measure. In cases where one subtest and the total score fell in the same outcome domain, we used the subtest score and excluded the total. Finally, for studies in which multiplicity issues remained (e.g., Digit Span- Forward and Digit Span- Backward subscales for the cognitive function outcome domain), we calculated an average effect size for the outcome domain (Lopez- Lopez et al., 2018).

# Data Synthesis and Analysis

Results from the included studies were pooled for the meta- analyses using Cochrane Review Manager 5.3 and analyzed using a random effects model when there were multiple studies in the analysis or a fixed effects model when there was only a single study. The effect size and corresponding  $95\%$  CI was reported as a relative risk for dichotomous outcomes and as a standardized mean difference (SMD) for continuous outcomes. SMDs were classified using Cohen's (1988) conventions of small (.2), medium (.5), and large (.8). Additionally, pooled effect sizes were considered to be statistically significant if the  $95\%$  CI did not cross the null value (i.e., 0). Main findings were grouped by clinical question and outcome into summary of findings tables (see Appendixes B through H). Additional predefined analyses were investigated based on participant characteristics (e.g., hearing loss severity, amplification type and experience, age, and special populations), treatment characteristics, and treatment format (e.g., remote and in- person).

The certainty of evidence for each effect was classified as high, moderate, low, or very low. These classifications were determined by assessing if there were any serious or very serious limitations in the risk of bias (e.g., methodological limitations in study design and/or execution), imprecision (e.g., the body of evidence has a small number of participants/events or a wide CI), inconsistency (e.g., unexplained heterogeneity,  $F$ ), or indirectness (e.g., differences in population, interventions, and/or outcome measures) of the evidence base. Consideration was also given to publication bias (e.g., selective publication of studies that may result in an over- or underestimation of effect) and magnitude of effect of the findings. As part of the GRADE protocol, the certainty of evidence for an outcome was downgraded for each designation of serious (downgraded one level) or very serious (downgraded two levels) limitations (Guyatt et al., 2008).

# Development of Clinical Recommendations

Tables summarizing the findings were provided to the Panel. GRADE profiles containing detailed information about the evidence including study design and judgments on risk of bias, imprecision, inconsistency, indirectness, magnitude of effect, and level of certainty were used by the Panel when making recommendations.

Each clinical question relating to the individual components of AR (i.e., informational, personal adjustment, perceptual training, and multicomponent) was examined by the Panel to determine if a clinical recommendation would be made. To aid in decision making, the GRADE evidence- to- decision framework was used to present information, facilitate discussion, and ensure that key factors were considered when making a recommendation such as the balance of benefits and harms of the various treatment options, resources required for the intervention, patient acceptability, clinical feasibility, and cost- effectiveness. The Panel also considered the overall certainty of evidence as an indication of their confidence in the overall effects (i.e., very low, low, moderate, and high). Preliminary judgments for each of the factors, the recommendations, and overall certainty were drafted during group phone calls, and then each panel member voted privately to provide consent or dissent and any comments. # Recommendations and Supporting Evidence

# Question 1: Should Informational Counseling AR Interventions Versus Control/Alternative Be Used for Adults With Hearing Loss?

# Summary of the Evidence: Informational Counseling AR

Twenty- three studies evaluated informational counseling (see Appendix B1). Most trials (15 studies) examined the effects of counseling on unaided or new hearing aid users and the remaining included experienced users (six studies) or a combination of new and experienced users (two studies). When reported, all included participants had, on average, mild or moderate hearing loss. Interventions varied, but common elements included instruction on communication strategies, hearing aid handling and care, environmental modifications, assistive technology, and information about the consequences of hearing loss and hearing aid use. Most provided informational counseling via an in- person format (17 studies), and some employed group counseling (five studies). In six trials, informational counseling was delivered remotely, with half using an indirect format (e.g., interactive videos, booklets, web- based tools, and e- mails). Informational counseling was typically delivered at standard audiological visits such as the assessment, hearing aid fitting, or follow- up consultation. Most were short term, with only three studies including interventions that consisted of more than five sessions.

Pooled analysis of five trials (663 participants) showed a small significant improvement in communication skills with informational counseling  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.36}$ $95\%$  CI [0.03, 0.69]) This effect was somewhat larger  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.67}$ $95\%$  CI [0.29,

1.04]) for those with mild hearing loss (Ferguson, Brandreth, et al., 2016; Gomez & Ferguson, 2020). Informational counseling also resulted in greater amplification adherence (i.e., number of those using their hearing aids relative to the number of those fitted; risk ratio  $\mathrm{[RR]} = 1.06$ $95\%$  CI [1, 1.12]). Although counseling had no overall effect on amplification use (e.g., hours per day), there was a small significant increase in use  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.22}$ $95\%$  CI [0.06, 0.39]) noted for individuals who received informational counseling through an in- person format as opposed to remote (Alice, 2018; Brooks, 1979, 1981; Ismail, 2019; Kemker & Holmes, 2004; Norman et al., 1994; Saunders et al., 2009; Ward, 1981; Ward et al., 1978).

In the combined results of seven trials (385 participants), informational counseling led to fewer difficulties with social engagement  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.29}$ $95\%$  CI [0.04, 0.53]). Despite no overall effect, counseling had a small positive effect on mood  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.4}$ $95\%$  CI [0.14, 0.67]) for experienced hearing aid users (Jennings, 2005; Kricos & Holmes, 1996; Lundberg et al., 2011; Thoren et al., 2011) and a large benefit on self- efficacy  $\mathrm{(SMD = 1.09}$ $95\%$  CI [0.47, 1.71]) when delivered prior to hearing aid fitting (Gomez & Ferguson, 2020). One study with 100 participants (Cherry & Rubinstein, 1994) reported that individuals receiving postfitting consultations to troubleshoot and counsel on any issues with hearing aids had significantly fewer adverse events in the form of hearing aid complaints than those receiving standard care  $\mathrm{RR} = 0.61$ $95\%$  CI [0.40, 0.93]).

Four outcomes had statistically significant results with effect sizes that approached but did not meet the threshold of a small effect (i.e.,  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.2$ ). These include device benefit  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.13}$ $95\%$  CI [0, 0.27]), device satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.19}$ $95\%$  CI [0.03, 0.35]), device use  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.14}$ $95\%$  CI [0.03, 0.25]), and mood  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.18}$ $95\%$  CI [0.02, 0.35]). There was either no overall effect or no statistically significant benefit of informational counseling on hearing satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = - 0.03}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.26, 0.2]), hearing disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.1}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.09, 0.29]), speech perception  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.55, 0.54]), quality of life  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.07}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.07, 0.21]), self- efficacy  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.33}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.29, 0.95]), perceived disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.04}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.12, 0.21]), or mental health  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.04}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.19, 0.27]). No included informational counseling studies assessed cognitive function outcomes (see Appendix B2 for informational counseling AR evidence tables).

# Evidence-Based Recommendation:

ASHA recommends that clinicians make informational counseling available for adults with hearing loss as part of a person- centered approach to aural rehabilitation (low certainty).

# Justification and Key Practice Points: Informational Counseling AR

The Panel considered the importance of education and information in the self- management of any chronic condition as the cornerstone of a person- centered AR program. They discussed the ramifications of not providing instruction or providing unsuitable or inadequate instruction and acknowledged that this could lead to patient misunderstanding and confusion and result in costly, ineffective, or inefficient care.

Informational counseling yielded benefits on outcomes that are important to individuals with hearing loss such as communication, social engagement, and fewer hearing aid complaints. Although the magnitude of these benefits was small, the improvements were above and beyond any benefit provided by the amplification alone. Moreover, the available, albeit limited, evidence on resources suggested that this was a cost- effective option when delivered either in person (Abrams et al., 2002) or remotely (Ferguson, Brandreth, et al., 2016), resulting in small cost savings in terms of hearing- related quality of life adjusted life years compared to hearing aids alone.

Results from clinical questions relevant to informational counseling or its implementation (i.e., Clinical Questions 1 and 6) indicate that counseling was effective with individuals across the patient continuum (e.g., unaided individuals and experienced hearing aid users) and when provided in a variety of formats. Limited evidence also suggests that informational counseling provided in person or with direct contact from an audiologist may yield additional benefits on key outcomes such as mood, social engagement, or hearing disability (Lundberg et al., 2011).

The flexibility to deliver effective informational counseling in a variety of formats is an important positive feature of this intervention for both clinicians and patients. The Panel considered this intervention to be practical in a clinical setting as many of the interventions were delivered during usual audiology appointments (e.g., fitting) or via a remote or indirect format that could be accessed by the patient as needed. Evidence from the review revealed that remote delivery was acceptable to patients and that most adhered to the treatment program (Ferguson, Brandreth, et al., 2016; Gomez & Ferguson, 2020). When appropriate for the patient's needs and circumstances, remote delivery of informational counseling could be used to supplement or improve access to services.

The available evidence centered on individuals with mild or moderate hearing loss and those who were unaided or had hearing aids. Although we do not know if these benefits would differ or extend to individuals with more severe hearing loss or to those with cochlear implants, the Panel felt most people would need and benefit from education or information on their hearing status, appropriate strategies to maximize their communication abilities, and how to manage their amplification device or other assistive technologies. The Panel voted unanimously to recommend informational counseling with an overall low certainty of effect due to serious or very serious limitations in the imprecision of the results (e.g., small number of participants and wide CI) and risk of bias of the evidence base for many of the included outcomes (see Appendix B).

# Question 2: Should Personal Adjustment Counseling-Infrared AR Interventions Versus Control/Alternative Be Used for Adults With Hearing Loss?

# Summary of the Evidence: Personal Adjustment Counseling AR

Personal adjustment counseling was investigated in 12 trials from 13 publications (see Appendix C1). Nine reported the amplification status of the participants. Most (five studies) evaluated personal adjustment counseling with new hearing aid users or those awaiting hearing aid fitting; three included experienced hearing aid users; and one examined a combination of hearing aid users, cochlear implant users, and nonusers. Four of the studies only included individuals with psychological distress, anxiety, or at least mild- to- moderate hearing- related difficulties in social or emotional functioning (Gamefski & Kraaij, 2012; Manchaiah et al., 2014; Molander et al., 2018; Preminger & Ziegler, 2008). Ten trials provided data on participant hearing loss severity, with most (seven studies) including individuals with mild or moderate hearing loss. The remaining focused on participants described as deaf or with severe- to- profound loss.

The studies examined a variety of personal adjustment interventions such as motivational tools and interviewing, emotional regulation training, counseling focused on the lived experience of hearing loss, acceptance and commitment treatment, self- affirmations, programs based on building resiliency skills or the principles of cognitive behavioral treatment, and group- delivered psychosocial exercises. Programs were brief, and all but one ranged from one to eight sessions. Seven studies provided personal adjustment counseling directly in person or synchronously via teleconferencing, and three of these used a group format. The remainder used other formats such as self- help programs, web- based tools, or asynchronous Internet- based programs (Armitage et al., 2017; Garnefski & Kraaij, 2012; Maidment et al., 2022; Manchaiah et al., 2014; Molander et al., 2018).

Evidence synthesized across three studies (107 participants) showed that personal adjustment counseling had a large significant benefit on the quality of life of adults with hearing loss  $\mathrm{(SMD = 1.1}$ $95\%$  CI [0.3, 1.91]). In the combined data of six trials (244 participants), personal adjustment counseling had a small positive effect on self- perceived hearing disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.27}$ $95\%$  CI [0, 0.54]). Overall, counseling resulted in a small significant

increase in amplification use  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.46$ $95\%$  CI [0.16, 0.77]), which was greater  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.8$ $95\%$  CI [0.12, 1.49]) for experienced amplification users (Aazh, 2016). Pooled analysis of five trials (202 participants) showed small significant improvements in mood from personal adjustment counseling  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.32$ $95\%$  CI [0, 0.64]) and somewhat larger gains  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.73$ $95\%$  CI [0.08, 1.38]) for individuals described as deaf or with severe- to- profound hearing loss due to neurofibromatosis 2 (Funes et al., 2019; Greenberg et al., 2019). Personal adjustment counseling had either no overall effect or no statistically significant effect on communication  $\mathrm{SMD} = - 0.03$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.57, 0.51]), device benefit  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.22$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.14, 0.58]), device satisfaction  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.29$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.04, 0.63]), device uptake  $\mathrm{HR} = 1.13$ $95\%$  CI [0.95, 1.33]), mental health  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.14$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.32, 0.59]), perceived disability  $\mathrm{SMD} = - 0.07$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.33, 0.18]), self- efficacy  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.15$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.2, 0.5]), social engagement  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.63$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.02, 1.27]), or speech perception  $\mathrm{SMD} = 0.01$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.46, 0.48]). No studies about personal adjustment counseling provided data on hearing satisfaction, cognitive function, third- party disability, or adverse effects (see Appendix C2 for personal adjustment counseling AR evidence tables).

# Evidence-Based Recommendation:

ASHA recommends that clinicians make personal adjustment counseling available for adults with hearing loss as part of a person- centered approach to aural rehabilitation (low certainty).

# Justification and Key Practice Points: Personal Adjustment Counseling AR

The Panel recognized the value of reducing distress that can be brought on by the diagnosis or worsening of a chronic condition such as hearing loss. Mastering an open environment to articulate feelings and readiness for change (e.g., pursuing amplification), strengthening coping skills and motivation for behavioral change, and supporting the social and emotional needs of the individual with hearing loss are key factors in a person- centered AR program. Additionally, failure to understand an individual's motivation or incorporate it in shared decision making may result in decreased participation in or adherence to the AR plan.

Personal adjustment counseling substantially increased the quality of life of adults with hearing loss, including those with severe- to- profound losses. It was also effective with other patient- important outcomes such as hearing disability, mood, and device use. Although there was no direct evidence on the adverse effects or harms, treatment satisfaction data suggest that personal adjustment counseling was acceptable to participants and that they had a favorable impression of the intervention (Aazh, 2016; Ferguson, Maidment, et al., 2016; Funes et al., 2019; Maidment et al., 2022; Preminger & Yoo, 2010).

Counseling was effectively delivered in a variety of formats (e.g., group, remote, and indirect), and almost all of the interventions were short term (eight sessions or fewer). Given this, the Panel felt that personal adjustment counseling could reasonably be administered in most clinical settings with relatively low costs. Furthermore, some lower cost formats such as group counseling may, when appropriate, provide an opportunity for individuals with hearing loss to interact with one another, find commonality in their experiences and feelings, and acquire increased acceptance of their hearing- related difficulties. The Panel voted unanimously to recommend personal adjustment counseling with an overall low certainty of effect due to serious or very serious limitations in the imprecision of the results (e.g., small number of participants and wide CI) as well as the risk of bias of the evidence base for all of the critical or important outcomes (see Appendix C2).

# Question 3: Should Perceptual Training AR Interventions Versus Control/Alternative Be Used for Adults With Hearing Loss?

# Summary of the Evidence: Perceptual Training AR

Twenty- six studies from 25 publications investigated perceptual training (see Appendix D1). Eighteen trials provided descriptions of the participants' degree of hearing loss, with the majority in the mild- to- moderate range (15 studies). Three studies included participants with hearing loss in the severe- to- profound range. One only included individuals with asymmetrical or unilateral hearing loss (Arts et al., 2018). When reported, most trials included hearing aid users (16 studies) or cochlear implant users (four studies), and one included two separate comparisons of hearing aid and cochlear implant users (Miller et al., 2008). The majority of studies examined the effects of perceptual training on new amplification users or nonusers (nine studies), experienced amplification users (11 studies), or a combination of new and experienced users (two studies). Amplification experience was not described in the remaining four studies.

The perceptual training interventions differed across studies and incorporated various components of auditory training, audiovisual training, speechreading, and sound awareness training. Auditory training programs utilized either analytic approaches (e.g., individual phonemes or syllable based), synthetic approaches (e.g., sentence based), or a combination of the two. The training stimuli varied between single- and multitalker inputs presented in both

quiet and noise. Additionally, some studies used an auditory and visual format (i.e., audiovisual) where individual training sounds were presented in conjunction with visual cues. Adaptive online training programs often included engagement with games, puzzles, and videos during training. Some interventions focused on improving visual- only speech perception abilities (i.e., speechreading), others utilized neurocognitive auditory training to target auditory processing abilities, and one incorporated sound awareness and listening exercises prior to hearing aid fitting. In all but one of the studies, training was provided in an individual format; the study by Preminger and Ziegler (2008) used a group format. Half of the studies provided perceptual training completely in person, whereas the other half provided it completely or partially in an indirect and/or remote format. Most programs occurred over approximately 3- 8 weeks and ranged from 4 to  $40\mathrm{hr}$  of training time.

Pooled analysis of 24 trials (809 participants) showed that perceptual training had a small significant benefit on the speech perception/listening abilities of adults with hearing loss  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.26}$ $95\%$  CI [0.08, 0.44]).For new users and nonusers of amplification, perceptual training had a significant small effect  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.45}$ $95\%$  CI [0.07, 0.84]) for self- perceived hearing disability (Baungaard et al., 2019; de Miranda et al., 2008; Ferguson et al., 2014; Oberg et al., 2008) and a significant small effect  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.4}$ $95\%$  CI [0, 0.8]) for mood (Baungaard et al., 2019; Ferguson et al., 2014; Oberg et al., 2008).

Perceptual training had either no overall effect or no statistically significant effect on cognitive function  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.23}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.1$  0.56])communication  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.16}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.16$  0.48])device use  $\mathrm{(SMD =}$  0.24,  $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.24$  0.73])electrophysiological changes  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.11}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.73$  0.94])device benefit  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.11}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.25$  0.48])device satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.12}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.57$  0.8])hearing disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.2}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.03$  0.43])hearing satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.15}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.29$  0.59])perceived disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.09}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.38$  0.56])quality of life  $\mathrm{(SMD = - 0.19}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.68$  0.31])self- efficacy  $\mathrm{(SMD = - 0.02}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.78$  0.75])mental health  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.2}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.45$  0.84])mood/well- being  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.04}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.27$  0.36])or social engagement  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.02}$ $95\%$  CI  $[- 0.26$  0.29]; see Appendix D2 for perceptual training AR evidence tables).

# Evidence-Based Recommendation:

ASHA recommends that clinicians make perceptual training available for adults with hearing loss as part of a person- centered approach to aural rehabilitation (low certainty).

# Justification and Key Practice Points: Perceptual Training AR

The Panel recognized the burden that hearing loss often puts on the ease and effectiveness of communication as well as the associated cognitive strain. Because the deficits caused by hearing loss are not always limited to hearing sensitivity, perceptual training offers a systematic way for adults with hearing loss to optimize their decoding abilities by helping the person learn to interpret sound either with or without their device. Improved speech understanding is a common goal of those with hearing loss, and perceptual training, as a component of a personcentered AR plan, serves a critical role of helping patients achieve their full potential. Additionally, the active engagement with the training program may lead to an acquired sense of empowerment and ownership of one's hearing loss.

Perceptual training had an overall effect on the key critical outcome of speech perception. As noted earlier, we only included data from untrained tasks in our analysis. Given that the measured speech perception changes were on untrained tasks, the Panel felt that these effects reflect important aspects of rehabilitation, specifically generalization and transfer of learning. Additionally, a small improvement in self- perceived hearing disability and mood for new amplification users and nonusers supports the use of perceptual training techniques among these patient groups. Although adverse effects were not considered a critical or important outcome for this clinical question, the Panel reviewed evidence from the included studies that identified possible harms associated with training (i.e., sound aversiveness for hearing aid users or cochlear implant discomfort). There was no negative impact of perceptual training; therefore, the Panel concluded that the small potential benefits outweighed the harms.

The variety of delivery options for perceptual training makes it feasible for all types of patients. Additionally, two studies investigated direct versus indirect perceptual training Lonka,1995;Yu et al.,2017) with equivocal findings (see results from Clinical Question 6). This suggests that there is flexibility with regard to delivery options. As such, training may be rendered in person in an individual or group format, online through a web application, through a mobile phone application, or via DVD/CD, allowing a customizable approach based primarily on the patient's needs and preferences. The costs differ depending on the format, with in- person training being the most expensive Reis,2019).Given that the price of several online and mobile applications ranges from free to a few hundred dollars Olson,2015),the Panel considered perceptual training to be a relatively low- cost intervention overall. Although limited, patient satisfaction data of experienced hearing aid users from one study Preminger & Ziegler,2008) indicated that more

than half of recipients perceived benefit in auditory and visual speech perception following perceptual training. Additionally, adherence with remote training regimens was relatively high across the included studies (Abrams et al., 2015; Ferguson et al., 2014; Humes et al., 2019). Although there is no single "gold- standard" perceptual training program, the Panel felt confident that there are options available to meet the individual needs and goals of most patients seeking improved speech perception abilities. The Panel voted unanimously to recommend perceptual training with an overall low certainty of effect due to very serious limitations with study quality (see Appendix D2).

# Question 4: Should Multicomponent (e.g., Informational Counseling + Perceptual Training) AR Interventions Versus Control/ Alternative Be Used for Adults With Hearing Loss?

# Summary of the Evidence: Multicomponent AR

Twenty- eight studies evaluated some combination of information counseling, personal adjustment counseling, and/or perceptual training (i.e., multicomponent AR intervention) compared to either a single component of AR, no treatment, or a sham treatment (see Appendix E1). Three studies examined the effects of AR programs incorporating perceptual training and personal adjustment counseling, and two additional trials combined all three components. The remaining provided informational counseling in conjunction with personal adjustment counseling (12 studies) or perceptual training (11 studies). Most provided services in person (22 studies), and many used a group format (14 studies). Six studies used a remote format (e.g., web tool, booklets, and phone calls), and one used a mix of remote and in- person services. When reported (21 studies), multicomponent AR programs delivered in person were typically limited to fewer than seven sessions, with only two providing interventions of eight or more sessions. Most indirect or remote programs were implemented over 4- 10 weeks.

When reported (25 studies), the effects of multicomponent AR treatments were examined on new amplification users (six studies), experienced hearing aid or cochlear implant users (11 studies), nonusers (three studies), or a mixture of amplification users and experienced users (five studies). Twenty- five studies reported participant pure- tone averages, all of which were in the mild- to- moderate hearing loss range.

Multicomponent AR interventions resulted in medium significant increases in device benefit  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.61}$ $95\%$  CI [0.13, 1.08]; seven studies) and small significant improvements in device satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.38}$ $95\%$  CI [0.07, 0.69]; three studies), hearing disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.23}$ $95\%$  CI [0.07, 0.4]; 17 studies), mood  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.2}$ $95\%$  CI [0.02, 0.38]; 13 studies), and speech perception  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.24}$ $95\%$  CI [0.05, 0.43]; seven studies). The pooled analysis of eight trials showed a small significant effect on communication skills  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.3}$ $95\%$  CI [0.09, 0.51]) that was larger  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.52}$ $95\%$  CI [0.14, 0.9]) for experienced amplification users (Andersson et al., 1997; Malmberg et al., 2017; Preminger & Yoo, 2010; Preminger & Ziegler, 2008). Small benefits were also noted for hearing satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD =}$  0.45,  $95\%$  CI [0.08, 0.82]; three studies), with new device users from Olson et al. (2013) and nonusers (Rivera et al., 2020) showing the greatest satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.59}$ $95\%$  CI [0.14, 1.04]).

Very limited evidence (Bernstein et al., 2021) revealed that multicomponent AR had a large positive effect on quality of life for CI users  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.89}$ $95\%$  CI [0.06, 1.72]) and a small significant effect on mental health (Ferguson, Maidment, et al., 2016) for new amplification users  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.67}$ $95\%$  CI [0.18, 0.45]) even though it had no overall effect for either of these outcomes. Multicomponent AR programs that included personal adjustment counseling (Jennings, 2005; Kricos et al., 1992; Preminger & Yoo, 2010; Preminger & Ziegler, 2008; Thoren et al., 2014) resulted in small significant gains in social engagement  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.28}$ $95\%$  CI [0.02, 0.55]), but this effect was not seen in the overall analysis.

Multicomponent AR programs had either no overall effect or no statistically significant effect on cognitive function  $\mathrm{(SMD = - 0.01}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.24, 0.22]), device use  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.25}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.08, 0.57]), mental health  $\mathrm{(SMD =}$  0.04,  $95\%$  CI [- 0.36, 0.45]), quality of life  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.16}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.24, 0.56]), perceived disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.07}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.16, 0.3]), self- efficacy  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.22}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.16, 0.59]), social engagement  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.3}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.07, 0.68]), and adverse effects  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.21}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.82, 0.39]). No included studies assessed electrophysiological outcomes for multicomponent AR programs (see Appendix E2 for multicomponent AR evidence tables).

# Evidence-Based Recommendation:

ASHA recommends that clinicians make multicomponent aural rehabilitation available for adults with hearing loss as part of a person- centered approach (low certainty).

# Justification and Key Practice Points: Multicomponent AR

The Panel acknowledged that adults with hearing loss are a diverse group with varying needs. Comprehensive, holistic programs addressing more than one aspect of AR provide the flexibility, efficiency, and convenience needed in a PCC framework to address common hearing loss sequelae in a customized manner. Moreover, combinations of AR components yielded not only differential benefits but collective benefits as well.

Multicomponent AR interventions resulted in smallto- medium benefits on nearly all the hearing- and devicerelated outcomes including device benefit, device satisfaction, hearing disability, hearing satisfaction, and speech perception. It was also effective in domains more related to everyday activities and participation such as mood, communication, and social engagement (for multicomponent AR programs incorporating personal adjustment counseling).Although, overall, the magnitude of the benefits was not larger than those from single- component AR these benefits were seen across a greater number of patient- important outcomes than other forms of AR with no associated adverse effects. Therefore, the Panel concluded that multicomponent AR could be a useful and effective option for those with difficulties across a range of outcome domains.

The Panel reviewed studies investigating early versus later multicomponent AR and those examining the effects of multicomponent AR provided with a communication partner (see results from Clinical Questions 5 and 7). They discussed the additional gains of early multicomponent AR on hearing disability with new amplification users and decided that, when appropriate, it was important to encourage and promote multicomponent AR at that stage. However, given the overall benefits of multicomponent AR on individuals across the hearing loss continuum, they cautioned against viewing first- time amplification as a critical window and noted that it should be offered as an option at all stages when warranted by the individual's profile. The Panel considered the limited evidence of including a communication partner in the multicomponent AR program. They recognized that although not necessary for a successful AR program, a helpful communication partner could enhance the rehabilitation program and provide additional support for the individual with hearing difficulties and should be offered as an option.

Based on qualitative and satisfaction data from several of the included studies e.g.,Aazh,2016;Hickson et al., 2007; Ferguson, Maidment, et al., 2016; Norman et al., 1995; Preminger & Yoo, 2010), multicomponent AR treatments were not only acceptable to patients but valued as well. Additionally, most of the multicomponent AR interventions examined in this review were of similar dosage e.g., total number of sessions and duration of treatment) as single- component AR treatments and could be delivered either in person or remotely (e.g., online tools), making them as clinically practical and feasible as other types of AR.

The Panel examined the limited data available on the costs of multicomponent AR. Costs varied depending on the format of the program, but all were similar to single- component AR. Other evidence from a recent costbenefit analysis (Hogan et al., 2020) found that providing hearing rehabilitation services (e.g., readiness training, education, self- efficacy, and motivational training) in addition to or in lieu of hearing aids resulted in an estimated savings of  $61\% - 82\%$  over the current program model of providing hearing aids alone. Based on this information and the overall effects of the intervention, the Panel considered multicomponent AR to be an efficient and cost- effective approach to managing the rehabilitative needs of adults with hearing loss. The Panel voted unanimously to recommend multicomponent AR with an overall low certainty of effect due to serious to very serious risk of bias of the evidence base and imprecision of the results (e.g., small number of participants and wide CI) for many of the included outcomes (see Appendix E2).

# Question 5: Should Early/Immediate AR Interventions Versus Later/Delayed Be Used for Adults With Hearing Loss?

# Summary of the Evidence: Early/Immediate Versus Later/Delayed AR

Four studies directly addressed the question of timing, but what defined early versus late treatment varied across the studies (see Appendix F1). Two studies examined the effect of the Listening and Communication Enhancement (Sweetow & Sabes, 2006) program, a multicomponent intervention comprising perceptual training and informational counseling in new hearing aid users (i.e., less than 6 months of experience) compared to experienced hearing aid users, defined as either more than 6 months (Saunders et al., 2016) or 2 years of experience Olson et al., 2013).One study Lansing & Davis, 1988) investigated the delivery of a different multicomponent AR program that also combined perceptual training and informational counseling either 1 month (i.e., immediate) or 9 months (i.e., delayed) post cochlear implant activation. The final study investigated the difference in delivering an informational hearing aid orientation pre hearing aid fitting versus post hearing aid fitting Kemker & Holmes, 2004).

Early provision of AR also yielded significant improvements  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.44}$ $95\%$  CI [0.11, 0.76]) in hearing disability Olson et al., 2013;Saunders et al., 2016). Either no overall effect or no statistically significant differences were noted for cognitive function  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.17}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.17,0.51]),device use  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.09}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.47,0.65]),device satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.16}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.57,0.89]),hearing satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.43}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.45,1.31]),perceived disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.87,0.87]),quality of life  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.43}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.45,1.31]),device benefit  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.28}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.03,0.59]),or speech perception  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.07}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.24,0.37]). No included studies examined adverse effects, communication, electrophysiological changes, mental

health, mood/well- being, self- efficacy, or social engagement (see Appendix F2 for early vs. late AR evidence tables).

# No Recommendation:

The Panel did not make a recommendation regarding early/immediate versus later/delayed aural rehabilitation for adults with hearing loss.

# Justification: Early/Immediate Versus Later/Delayed AR

The Panel discussed the available evidence and decided that it was not only too limited but also too varied (e.g., patient populations, types of interventions, and definition of early and late AR) to make a global recommendation for the timing of AR (unanimous panel decision). However, they indicated that some of the results could be used to provide information about specific AR interventions (see the justification section for multicomponent AR).

# Question 6: Should Direct (e.g., Clinician-Directed, Clinician Involved) Versus Indirect (e.g., Pamphlet, DVD) AR Interventions Be Used for Adults With Hearing Loss?

# Summary of the Evidence: Direct Versus Indirect AR

Three studies addressed this clinical question (see Appendix G1). One randomized controlled trial compared the outcomes of an educational program delivered through a book supplemented with telephone consultations from an audiologist to the same educational program delivered just through a book to experienced hearing aid users with mild hearing loss (Lundberg et al., 2011). The results suggest that individuals who received direct informational counseling from an audiologist demonstrated significant improvements in hearing disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.76}$ $95\%$  CI [0.27, 1.24]), social engagement  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.83}$ $95\%$  CI [0.33, 1.32]), and mood  $\mathrm{(SMD =}$ $0.7,95\%$  CI [0.21, 1.19]). Either no overall effect or no statistically significant differences were noted in device use  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.19}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.29, 0.66]), device benefit  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.38}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.09, 0.86]), device satisfaction  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.28}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.2, 0.75]), mental health  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.29}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.19, 0.76]), perceived disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.21}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.27, 0.68]), or quality of life  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.3}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.18, 0.77]).

Two other studies examined perceptual training via videotape or mobile app compared to in- person training on speech perception outcomes (Lonka, 1995; Yu et al., 2017). Training programs ranged from 4 to  $20\mathrm{hr}$  and included participants with mild- to- moderate hearing loss, and one included new hearing aid users (Yu et al., 2017). There were no statistically significant differences between the direct and indirect formats on speech perception  $\mathrm{(SMD = - 0.27}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.78, 0.24]). No included studies assessed cognitive function, communication, hearing satisfaction, self- efficacy, or adverse effects (see Appendix G2 for direct vs. indirect AR evidence tables).

# No Recommendation:

The Panel did not make a recommendation regarding direct versus indirect aural rehabilitation for adults with hearing loss.

# Justification: Direct Versus Indirect AR

The Panel reviewed the findings and concluded that further evidence would be needed to make a recommendation regarding the provision of direct or indirect AR (unanimous panel decision). However, they indicated that some of the results could be used to provide supporting information about specific AR interventions (see the justification sections for informational counseling AR and perceptual training).

# Question 7: Should AR Interventions Including a Communication Partner Versus Control/Alternative Be Used for Adults With Hearing Loss?

# Summary of the Evidence: AR With a Communication Partner

Seven studies investigated the inclusion of a communication partner in multicomponent AR interventions (see Appendix H1). The majority used a group, in- person format (six studies), and one was delivered indirectly using videos and a booklet (S. E. Kramer et al., 2005). Three of the studies (Preminger, 2003; Preminger & Meeks, 2010; Taylor, 2003) isolated the effect of a communication partner by providing the same treatment to both groups, with the sole difference being the addition of the significant other in the intervention group. Despite no overall effects, very limited evidence from one study (Taylor, 2003) suggests an improvement in hearing disability for new users of amplification  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.56}$ $95\%$  CI [0.01, 1.11]). The four remaining studies (Cardemil et al., 2014; Hallberg & Barrenas, 1994; S. E. Kramer et al., 2005; Lucia Habanec & Kelly- Campbell, 2015) did not isolate the effect. Instead, they compared a multicomponent intervention including a communication partner to a no- treatment control group. The evidence from one study (Cardemil et al., 2014) suggests a large significant increase in device use

$\mathrm{(SMD = 0.8}$ $95\%$  CI [0.5, 1.11]) overall, whereas one other study (Lucia Habanec & Kelly- Campbell, 2015) showed improvement for third- party disability for nonusers with the inclusion of a communication partner  $\mathrm{(SMD = 3.11}$ $95\%$  CI [1.86, 4.36]). Either no overall differences or no statistically significant effects were noted in communication  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.03}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.57, 0.62]) hearing disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.04}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.34, 0.41]) perceived disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = - 0.08}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.64, 0.49]) social engagement  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.37}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.18, 0.91]) third- party disability  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.99}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.4, 2.38]) mental health  $\mathrm{(SMD = - 0.28}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.94, 0.38]) or mood  $\mathrm{(SMD = 0.19}$ $95\%$  CI [- 0.19, 0.57]). No included studies examined cognitive function, device benefit, device satisfaction, hearing satisfaction, duality of life, selfefficacy, or adverse effects (see Appendix H2 for AR with communication partner evidence tables).

# No Recommendation:

The Panel did not make a recommendation regarding aural rehabilitation interventions including the communication partner for adults with hearing loss.

# Justification: AR With a Communication Partner

The seven studies included for this clinical question all addressed multicomponent AR interventions with a communication partner, but only three directly isolated the effect of including a communication partner. Therefore, the Panel indicated that the evidence was too limited to make a recommendation regarding the inclusion of a communication partner across all AR interventions (unanimous panel decision), but the results were used to provide supporting information for multicomponent AR. It should be noted that these research findings are only specific to the direct involvement of the communication partner in a distinct AR intervention and do not reflect the participation of the partner as part of PCC (e.g., determining the individual's needs and preferences, personalized goal setting).

# Limitations of the Evidence and Future Research

There are several inherent concerns with the quality of the overall body of evidence. First, there is a lack of rigorous randomized controlled trials within the scope of this guideline. Specifically, adequate randomization and blinding of both participants and assessors was missing in many studies. This is not particularly surprising as blinding is often difficult to implement in behavioral intervention research due to the nature of these types of interventions and offering a placebo is not always possible.

Additionally, when participants are not blinded and they assess their own performance through patient- reported outcomes (which were frequently used in the included studies), assessor blinding is also affected. Next, several studies provided insufficient information about important methodological details needed to adequately assess the risk of bias, which led to many "unclear" risk of bias designations across all categories. These study limitations in conjunction with small numbers of participants and wide CIs of many of the pooled effects contributed to the overall low certainty of effect for each of the evidence- based recommendations.

Additionally, as part of this guideline, there were not any prioritized outcomes or clinical questions addressing post- intervention data provided from follow- up time points, and we were unable to investigate the maintenance of effects. Another limitation relates to our prioritized outcomes, which were considered critical or important to adults with hearing loss. The Panel discussed the potential that AR may impact outcome domains that were not included as part of this guideline and that are not typically assessed (e.g., interest in rehabilitation).

Given that AR encompasses an array of interventions and that those with hearing loss present with a wide range of deficits, there was significant variation among the included studies in terms of intervention type, format, and dosage. We attempted to combat this by narrowing the range of AR into the distinct components and targeting each with specific clinical questions, but the interventions and the associated service delivery aspects within each component still varied significantly. For example, the interventions examined within the perceptual training clinical question include auditory training programs using different approaches (e.g., analytic vs. synthetic, single- talker vs. multitalker), speechreading training programs, and auditory- visual training programs. This variability, in combination with the insufficient level of methodological detail, limited our ability to drill down and analyze the data more granularly.

Similarly, there was a wide range of outcome measurement tools used across the studies. Multiplicity (e.g., more than one outcome within a single domain) within studies posed a challenge during the meta- analysis and often required us to average the effects. This occurred across many outcome domains, especially for the speech perception outcome domain as scores were reported in different listening conditions (e.g., 0 and  $+5$  dB SNR) or with different stimuli. There is the potential that combining the outcome measures into an average effect size may have reduced the specificity of the results. Additionally, in our efforts to combat multiplicity (e.g., using self- reported measures over communication partners' reports), we may have introduced selection bias, which could have influenced the results.

Another challenge of combining multiple outcome measures was that each tool had different minimal

clinically important difference (MCID) values or, in some cases, did not provide MCID scores at all. This limited our ability to determine a single threshold for clinical significance for the effects of each outcome. However, it is important to note that although some of the effects were small, this was in addition to any benefits obtained from amplification alone. As such, the Panel considered the magnitude of the effects, in conjunction with their clinical expertise, when making recommendations.

Future adequately powered and high- quality research studies should directly examine the effectiveness of providing AR early versus late, using direct versus indirect provision of services, and with and without the inclusion of a communication partner. Communication partners are recognized as an essential element of PCC (e.g., participating in the needs assessment and involvement in shared decision making), and there is much interest in understanding the possible advantages and efficacy of further involvement of communication partners as active participants in AR interventions (Manchaiah et al., 2012). Next, as part of the evidence- to- decision process, there was not any available evidence examining the impact of AR on health equity, so this is an area in which more research is needed. Last, the development of a core outcome measurement set would reduce outcome variability, make it easier to combine data across studies, and increase the interpretability of the results.

# Summary

As the third most common chronic condition affecting  $20\%$  of the world's population, hearing loss poses a significant public health challenge (Blackwell et al., 2014; Chadha et al., 2021). This challenge is exacerbated by the common sequelae of hearing loss, especially when left untreated, which include but are not limited to depression (Lawrence et al., 2020), social isolation (Nachtegaal et al., 2009), reduced quality of life (Ciorba et al., 2012), cognitive decline (Lin et al., 2011; Loughrey et al., 2018), and an increased risk of falls (Jiam et al., 2016). Despite its functional impact, hearing loss and its sequelae often go unaddressed. This may be due to the patient's lack of awareness, hesitation (e.g., stigma), or limited health care coverage for AR, including amplification, in the United States.

ASHA, together with a multidisciplinary panel of subject matter experts, conducted a comprehensive systematic review and developed evidence- based clinical practice guidelines and recommendations on the provision of AR to adults with hearing loss across the severity continuum. AR is a broad umbrella term that comprises a range of interventions for those with hearing loss including sensory management, informational counseling, personal adjustment counseling, and perceptual training. Each intervention may stand alone or be implemented in combination with other components (i.e., multicomponent AR) as part of a holistic AR plan.

Although sensory management is a critical component of AR, the positive effects have been well documented previously and, therefore, were not included in the scope of this guideline. The importance and utility of appropriate amplification for adults with hearing loss to improve the auditory signal should continue to be a central tenet within AR.

Due to the multifaceted nature of AR overall, the Panel investigated the effects of each intervention in isolation through a series of targeted clinical questions to understand the unique contribution of each on key patient outcomes in addition to or in lieu of those provided by amplification. The findings revealed that each aspect of AR provided additional benefits beyond amplification on outcomes considered critical or important for clinical decision making. Therefore, in addition to appropriate sensory management, ASHA recommends that adults with hearing loss receive AR that includes at least one of the four approaches, informational counseling, personal adjustment counseling, perceptual training, or multicomponent AR, and that rehabilitation decisions be made within a person- centered framework.

Due to the gravity of the problem of hearing loss and the documented benefits of intervention, AR should be considered essential and made available for all adults with hearing loss. The selection of an approach should be based on a shared decision- making process and determined by the individual's unique abilities, needs, goals, and preferences. Clinicians should incorporate the relevant key practice points into the development and implementation of a customized AR program to promote PCC.

# Implementation Resources

For guideline summary and implementation tools, see Supplemental Material S4. There is a patient- focused decision aid tool intended to educate those with hearing loss on AR and the available options. Additionally, there is a clinician- focused resource that summarizes the findings of the guideline into a checklist according to the common difficulties and challenges experienced by adults with hearing loss. In combination, these support tools can be used to educate both patients and clinicians, guide collaborative clinical discussions, and provide decisional support when developing an AR plan.

# Updates to the Guideline

The guideline will be updated at a minimum of every 5 years following publication. N- CEP will monitor the research on an ongoing basis and conduct a full, updated systematic search every 2 years for any new relevant studies that meet the inclusion criteria. If warranted

based on new research, a panel will be convened to discuss whether this guideline needs to be updated sooner.
