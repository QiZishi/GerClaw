# 3. Guidelines

# 3.1. Patients with cerebrovascular disease

CQ: Should older adult patients with cerebrovascular disease undergoing rehabilitation receive enhanced nutritional care?

Recommendation: We suggest enhanced nutritional care for patients with cerebrovascular disease undergoing rehabilitation to reduce complications related to infection and to improve activities of daily living (ADL) (certainty of evidence: low; recommendation level: weak). The appropriate dose and route of nutritional care should be selected according to individual conditions such as swallowing ability and intestinal functioning, and favourable types of enhanced nutritional care should include oral nutritional supplements, protein-rich foods, and other supplements.

# 3.1.1. Commentary

Brain damage by cerebrovascular disease leads to physical and mental disabilities and some patients need to receive comprehensive rehabilitation. Concurrently, malnutrition is often seen in patients with cerebrovascular disease mainly due to dysphagia [5,24,], and it causes deteriorated functioning, a decreased QoL, and higher mortality [25,26]. Therefore, rehabilitation and nutritional care should be included in the routine care of these patients [9,27].

We selected all-cause mortality, complication rates, and activities of daily living (ADL) as favourable outcomes and pneumonia as an unfavourable outcome. A total of 10 RCTs from 9 articles were selected via an SR [28-36], and all of them were used in the following meta-analysis except for one RCT [34] (Fig. 1, Table 2). Four studies had 1-2 high risk of bias [28,29,31,36] whereas residual studies had no high risk of bias (Suppl. 5).

In an RCT investigating 116 patients with primary cerebrovascular disease in rehabilitation facilities, Rabadi et al. found that patients taking commercial oral nutritional supplements (ONS) (240 kcal,  $11\mathrm{g}$  of protein) showed significant gains in the Functional

Independence Measure (FIM) total score, FIM motor score, and the six-minute-walk-test (6MWT) score, although there was no improvement in the FIM cognitive score [28]. Ha et al. reported that acute stroke patients with individualised nutritional care, both with and without protein-enriched food, experienced an improved QoL (EQ-5D VAS score) during a 3-month follow-up [29].

The largest multicentre RCT by Dennis et al., enrolling 859 acute stroke patients from 15 countries who were unable to orally intake food, indicated that the early initiation of enteral tube feeding (within 7 days of admission) was associated with an absolute reduction in the risk of death and a reduction in death or poor outcomes [30]. Gariballa et al. found that acute (<1 week from onset) ischemic stroke patients receiving ONS in addition to a normal hospital diet showed slightly lower mortality rates at a 3-month follow-up than those receiving an un-supplemented diet [31]. Similarly, Zheng et al. investigated the usefulness of nutrient-

enriched supplements compared to standard meals for stroke patients. Nutritional supplements significantly decreased mortality and infectious complication rates compared with standard meals [32]. However, the respiratory infection rate tended to be higher in the supplemented group than in the standard meal group.

Boselli et al. tested the effect of EAA-enriched supplements in people with brain injury (caused by stroke, trauma, and anoxic coma) who were admitted to a rehabilitation ward in an RCT and found that the incidence of infectious complications was lower in the supplemented group than in the iso-caloric placebo group [33]. Aquilani et al. reported that EEA plus physical therapy (5 times/ week) for sub-acute stroke patients did not demonstrate an improvement in the FIM score [34].

Yoshimura et al. tested the effect of a leucine-enriched amino acid containing a jelly-type supplement in post-stroke patients

# 3.2. Patients with hip fracture

CQ: Should older patients with hip fracture undergoing rehabilitation receive enhanced nutritional care?

Recommendation: We suggest enhanced nutritional care for hip fracture patients aged  $\geq 65$  years old undergoing rehabilitation to reduce mortality and complication, or improve activities of daily living (certainty of evidence: low; recommendation level: weak).

# 3.2.1. Commentary

Patients after hip fracture often experience declines in walking ability and ADL [37]. A high level of mortality is particularly evident in older adults, underweight patients, and patients with complications [38]. Hip fracture patients also experience malnutrition at the onset [39], and tend to experience higher re-admission rates

and mortality [40]. However, older patients sometimes decrease their energy and protein intake, which is linked to the loss of body weight and muscle mass [41]. Therefore, rehabilitation nutrition is an important approach for preventing malnutrition and resuming ADL in patients after hip fracture.

We selected ADL, all-cause mortality, QoL, and complications as favourable outcomes, and the onset of pneumonia as an unfavourable outcome. Ten RCTs were extracted for the SR by the literature search (Fig. 2, Table 4) [41-49]. ADL were reported by three RCTs, using the Groningen activity restriction scale (GARS) [41], Barthel Index [42], and FIM [43]. QoL was examined in four articles, using the EuroQoL [41,47], SF-12 [45], and AQoL-4D [49]. Three studies had no high risk of bias [42,45,49] whereas another four studies had 1-2 risks of bias [41,43,45,46]. The rest of studies had more (3 and 5) risks of bias [44,47] (Suppl. 6).

Wyers and colleagues examined the effects of routine nutrition counselling by dietitians and ONS in 152 older patients after hip fracture compared with standard nutritional care in an RCT [42]. Both intervention and control groups received physical therapy during hospitalization and following discharge from the hospital. # 3.3. Adult patients with cancer

CQ: Should adult patients with cancer, without refractory cachexia, undergoing rehabilitation receive enhanced nutritional care?

Recommendation: We suggest enhanced nutritional care for patients undergoing (or after) anti-cancer treatment and rehabilitation (certainty of evidence: moderate; recommendation level: weak). It is desirable to make a decision based on the type of cancer due to uncertainty of the benefits and risks associated with the combined intervention of rehabilitation and enhanced nutritional care. Due to a lack of evidence, any recommendations pertaining to enhanced nutritional care cannot be made for the patients who are malnourished, have cachexia, or restricted activities of daily living, while undergoing (or after) anti-cancer treatment and rehabilitation.

# 3.3.1. Commentary

Patients with malignant tumours have a high risk for sarcopenia and frailty due to malnutrition caused by not only cancer-induced cachexia but also by secondary factors such as adjuvant chemotherapy and/or radiation therapy [50]. Since these factors decrease ADL functioning, QoL, and survival rates [51,52], intervention based on the concept of rehabilitation nutrition may be useful to prevent or treat malnutrition and/or cachexia in adult patients with cancer.

We selected QoL, overall survival, attenuating symptoms such as pain or fatigue, ADL, and rate of treatment completion as favourable outcomes, and rate of treatment withdrawal as an unfavourable outcome. After searching the literature, three RCTs were selected for the SR (Fig. 3, Table 6) [53-55]. We could not find any article examining the overall survival or rate of treatment completion. There is one study that investigated the effects of exercise and a low-fat diet with enriched vegetables and fruits for adult menopausal women with breast cancer after surgery and following adjuvant chemotherapy [53]. Another study examined the effects of combined therapy with nutritional counselling and resistance training in patients with head and neck cancer who had radiation therapy planned or already performed within 1 week after the therapy [54]. One remaining study aimed to clarify the efficacy of the concurrent use of nutritional counselling and exercise for patients with stage II or III breast cancer undergoing preoperative chemotherapy [55]. Each study showed 1-3 high risks of bias (Suppl. 7) [53-55].

An RCT conducted by Wahnefried et al. included menopausal female patients undergoing postoperative adjuvant chemotherapy [53]. The patients were allocated into three groups: Calcium-enriched diet (Attention control arm: CA), CA + exercise (EX), and EX + high fruit and vegetable, low-fat diet (EX + FVLF). Eight percent of the patients in the EX + FVLF group dropped out of the study due to refusal of further participation. There were no significant increases in the EX or EX + FVLF groups in QoL, energy intake, or body composition at the 3- or 6-month follow-ups,

Based on the included outcome measures in the eligible studies, meta-analyses were performed for QoL (FACT-G) and fatigue. Only one pilot trial addressed fatigue as the study's outcome [54]. Considering the risk of bias of QoL and fatigue, we set the certainty of evidence as moderate (Table 7). The effect of the combined program with nutritional counselling and rehabilitation on FACT-G is uncertain due to a smaller absolute difference than MCID [53,54], and significant improvement was found only for the vitality subscore of the SF-36 [54]. There were no apparent disadvantages regarding the intervention program; however, its feasibility (dropout rate:  $0\% -29\%$ ) caused by the contents of the program may be an issue that needs to be resolved.

In addition, no study stated the use of validated assessment methods for malnutrition or cachexia. Moreover, the usefulness of ONS and enteral nutrition for the patients who were malnourished or at a risk of malnutrition cannot be assessed because all studies employed nutritional counselling as an intervention. It cannot be denied that the included studies did not examine patients with malnutrition or cachexia who would be expected to experience the effectiveness of enhanced nutritional care. Therefore, whether the combined program with enhanced nutritional care and rehabilitation is beneficial remains unclear. The cancer types in the analysed studies included only breast cancer and head and neck cancer. Since there are differences in pathology, symptoms, gastrointestinal functioning, and swallowing functioning among patients with various kinds of cancer, the effects of enhanced nutritional care combined with rehabilitation may vary.

Based on the supporting evidence, we concluded that enhanced nutritional care for patients undergoing (or after) anti-cancer treatment and rehabilitation is weakly recommended due to the potential benefits of improving QoL and attenuating some cancer-related symptoms, and the low likelihood of adverse outcomes. # 3.4. Patients with acute illnesses

CQ: Should patients with acute illnesses undergoing rehabilitation receive enhanced nutritional care?

Recommendation: We suggest enhanced nutritional care for patients with acute illnesses undergoing rehabilitation. However, in addition to voluntary rehabilitation, it is desirable to combine the enhanced nutritional care with an enhanced rehabilitation programme (certainty of evidence: very low; recommendation level: weak).

# 3.4.1. Commentary

Acute illness is defined as "the disease which tends to have very quick onsets and typically last for only a brief period. Examples of acute illnesses with hospitalization include pneumonia, urinary tract infection and acute myocardial infarction" [56]. These illnesses requiring hospitalisation often lower patients' ADL levels and QoL, as well as worsen their nutritional status. Therefore, both appropriate nutritional support and rehabilitation are expected to improve patients' outcome.

We selected QoL, all-cause mortality, ADL, and no complications as favourable outcomes, and falling, incidence of pneumonia, and hospital re-admission as unfavourable outcomes. After searching the literature, four RCTs were selected for the SR (Fig. 4, Table 8) [57-60]. Two studies used protein or energy-protein contained in ONS for older patients with acute illnesses [57,58], while the rest of the studies included patients in intensive care units and used ONS that included glutamine plus essential amino acids [59], or an added mixture that contained  $\beta$ -Hydroxy- $\beta$ -methylbutyrate (HMB), glutamine, and arginine via an enteral tube or orally for critically ill patients [60]. All studies had 1-3 high risks of bias (Suppl. 8) [57-60].

A study conducted by Gade et al. concluded that protein supplementation (approximately 500 kcal with  $25\mathrm{g}$  whey and  $2.5\mathrm{g}$  casein protein,  $2.8\mu \mathrm{g}$  vitamin D) has no additional effects on ADL, QoL, or mortality compared with iso-caloric placebo supplements with  $1.5\mathrm{g}$  protein among geriatric medical patients undergoing resistance training [57]. However, due to the uncertainty of the absolute value of ADL (Barthel Index) and QoL (EQ-5D-3L) at the follow-up time point, these data could not be quantitatively integrated in the meta-analysis. Hegerova et al. reported that ONS (600 kcal [2510 kJ], protein  $20\mathrm{g/day}$ ) added to a standard hospital diet initiated on the day of admission, along with a physical therapy programme, showed no significant decline in the Barthel index 6 months later; in contrast, the control group (standard diet, standard rehabilitation) showed significant decreases in the Barthel index [58]. There were no differences in the Barthel index between the groups. An RCT conducted by Jones et al. included 93 patients who were allocated into 4 groups by applying a two-by-two design using ONS and enhanced rehabilitation [59]. Their results showed that the enhanced rehabilitation with ONS group and enhanced rehabilitation with placebo group both showed significant improvements in QoL scores. However, between-group differences were not provided in the article [59]. Nakamura et al. conducted an RCT using supplements that contained HMB ( $3\mathrm{gHMB},$ $14\mathrm{g}$  arginine, and  $14\mathrm{g}$  glutamine) combined with electrical muscle stimulation therapy in intensive care unit patients from day 2 to day 10 after admission [60]. There were no differences in mortality or prevalence of complications (vomiting and diarrhoea) between the intervention and control groups [59].

According to the meta-analysis, the effects of enhanced nutritional care on mortality and the reduction of complications are uncertain because of the limited number of subjects included from two articles  $(N = 59 - 236)$  and a very low certainty of evidence (Table 8) [57,60]. Enhanced nutritional care possibly increase the Barthel index, but its clinical relevancy may be weak because the absolute difference did not reach the MCID [58]. For QoL, clinically meaningful, but not statistically significant, improvement was found with enhanced nutritional care and enhanced rehabilitation in one study [59]. There was no effect on complications from Nakamura's study [60]. Of note, the majority of potential advantages of enhanced nutritional care were derived from the studies with ONS. Therefore, it is unclear if enhanced nutritional care is beneficial for patients who cannot intake food orally due to unconsciousness and/or dysphagia. Overall, the certainty of available evidence was very low. In addition, heterogeneity in terms of population or intervention was considerable between the studies; one included intensive care patients aged 20 years or older and used HMB, whereas another examined geriatric medical patients with protein supplementation [57-60].

Based on the supporting evidence, we have come to the conclusion that enhanced nutritional care can be weakly recommended for patients with acute illness due to its potential merit for the improvement of ADL and QoL, and its unlikelihood of increasing complications; however, it is desirable to combine it with enhanced rehabilitation, if possible.
